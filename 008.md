# Chapter 8: The Whisper

Rain against the windows. Not the harbour rain that came sideways off the water—this was straight, steady, the kind that meant it would last all night. The Weeping Dog was nearly empty. One fisherman in the corner, asleep or dead. A pair of net-menders playing dice for matchsticks. Soric behind the bar.

Hark took his usual seat. Back to the wall. Sightlines to the door, the kitchen stair, the rain-streaked window. The geometry of leaving, mapped fresh. The glass in his hand was half-full of watered ale. He'd been drinking it for twenty minutes. It was still half-full.

Soric was cleaning a glass. One hand, a rag, the slow circular motion of a man who'd been doing this longer than Hark had been alive. The stump of his left arm moved in the same rhythm—muscle memory, the body still working a job the flesh had quit. He didn't look at Hark when he spoke.

"Heard something you might want to know."

Hark waited.

"Escaped mages. Seven of them. In the islands. The League's hiding them, or someone is." Soric held the glass up to the lamplight, squinted, kept wiping. "One of them's teaching the others. Guarding techniques. Reservoir work. Order-independent."

Hark's hand stopped halfway to his ale.

A half-second pause. Not long. Long enough. He forced his hand to continue, closed his fingers around the glass, brought it to his mouth. The ale tasted of nothing. It always tasted of nothing.

"Where'd you hear that."

"Here and there." Soric set the glass down and picked up another one. "Sailors talk. Merchants talk. Everyone talks in a port town."

"Everyone lies in a port town."

"True." Soric's rag kept moving. "But they lie about the same things. When enough of them tell the same lie, it stops being a lie. It's just news with bad phrasing."

Hark looked at the rain. Looked at the door. Looked at Soric's one hand, still moving, still cleaning a glass that was already clean. "Seven."

"Seven that they know about. Could be more. Could be fewer. The number changes depending on who's telling it." Soric set the second glass down and finally looked at him. His eyes were pale, washed-out, the eyes of a man who'd spent years squinting at horizons. "The part about the training doesn't change. Someone down there is teaching mages to guard without the Order's methods. If that spreads—"

"It won't."

"If it does, the Order's monopoly breaks. No monopoly, no leverage. No leverage, no Order." Soric shrugged with his one shoulder. The motion was uneven, a little lopsided. "That's what I heard. You can do what you want with it."

Hark's pulse was steady. His face was still. Inside, the inventory was running.

*Order informant?* Soric was old, connected, knew everyone in Salthaven who passed through the Factorage. He could be feeding information to the Cradle. But he'd been doing it for years if he was, and Hark was still here, still uncollected. That argued against. Or for a longer game.

*League agent?* The Maelstrom League had the most to gain from destabilizing the Order. Their naval academy was already training Edge specialists to counter mages. Hiding seven escapees was exactly the kind of thing the Admiralty Council would fund. But Soric wasn't League. His accent was wrong. His missing arm was too old, too clean—a land amputation, not a sea wound.

*Random drunk's rumor?* Possible. Soric heard everything. Most of it was noise. But Soric didn't pass on noise. Soric passed on things he thought mattered. That was what made him dangerous. That was what made him useful.

He had dreamed of finding others.

In the forest, the first year, when the fever spikes left him shaking on the ground and the cold in his hands was so deep he couldn't grip a blade. He'd been eleven, maybe twelve—the years blurred, in the forest—and he'd spent whole days staring at nothing, his body trying to burn him alive from the inside while the frost on his fingers crept further up his wrists.

He'd dreamed then. Not the dream with Lea's grey eyes—that one came later, after his voice came back, after Joel. This was a different dream. A campfire. Faces around it. People who knew what the silk felt like. People who understood that the scars on their hands were a ledger written in the same language. Someone who didn't need him to explain.

The dream had faded. The forest had taught him that fire meant smoke and smoke meant discovery. The years after Joel had taught him that people meant contracts and contracts meant exposure. He'd learned that alone was safer. Alone meant no one to betray you. Alone meant no one to bury.

He hadn't dreamed of campfires in a decade. The dream was still there, somewhere, buried under the cold inventory and the running joke and the locked door of the ropewalk room. But he didn't open that door. He'd learned not to open doors.

"Why tell me."

Soric picked up a third glass. Inspected it. Set it down. "You're the one who's always watching the door. Thought you might want to watch a different door for a change."

"Not my door."

"Didn't say it was. Just said it was open." Soric turned away, hung the rag over the tap. The conversation was closing. Soric's conversations always closed the same way—a door swinging shut, not slammed. "Rain's getting worse. You should head back before the streets flood."

Hark stood. Left coins on the bar. The right amount. He didn't count twice. Soric never skimmed. That was something. He didn't name what.

Outside, the rain hit his face. Cold. Clean. The streets were empty, the cobbles slick and shining in the dark. The harbour was invisible behind the weather. No boats. No gulls. Just the sound of water on water, and his boots splashing through puddles he didn't bother to avoid.

*Seven escaped mages. In the islands. Teaching each other.*

The number was probably wrong. Soric's numbers were always fluid. But the shape of the rumor was consistent with what he already knew. The League had been pushing back against the Order for years—their naval academy, their Silencer recruitment, their quiet investment in anything that might level the field. Hiding mages was the next logical step. Training them was the step after that.

*One of them is teaching guarding techniques. Order-independent.*

That was the part that stuck. Guarding was the Order's foundation. Every Sensitive they collected, they trained to guard first—locking the internal reservoir, hiding the gate-signature, controlling the debt-flow. The Order's monopoly rested on that training. If someone else could teach it, the monopoly cracked. If the monopoly cracked, the Order fell.

Hark had learned to guard in the Cradle. Eleven months of pushing against his own furnace until the door finally closed. He remembered the relief. He remembered the instructor's voice saying *again* and the frost spreading across his knuckles and the children who didn't learn fast enough and disappeared into other parts of the facility. He remembered all of it. He'd never tried to teach anyone else. Teaching meant exposure. Exposure meant the Order.

But someone was teaching. Someone who'd escaped, like him. Someone who'd decided that alone wasn't enough.

The ropewalk stairs were dark. He climbed them without thinking, his boots finding the familiar grooves in the wood. The door at the top was unlocked. He'd forgotten to lock it this morning. Or he'd locked it and the lock had failed. Or someone was inside.

He pushed the door open. The room was empty. The cot was unmade. The window was streaming with rain. He stood in the doorway for a long moment, his hand on the hilt of Joel's sword, his heat-sight flickering open just enough to read the cold shapes of the furniture, the colder shape of the window, the absence of any warm body that shouldn't be there.

Nothing. No one. He stepped inside. Did not lock the door.

The cot creaked under his weight. He sat on the edge, his coat still dripping, and stared at the wall. Crack east to west. Water stain shaped like a hand. The rain drummed on the roof. The room was cold. His fingers were colder.

*Seven escaped mages. In the islands.*

The rumor was a splinter. Small. Sharp. Lodged somewhere he couldn't reach. He turned it over in his mind the way he turned over coins—checking the weight, the edges, the feel of it. It didn't change. It just sat there. Irritating. Persistent.

He didn't sleep. He didn't undress. He sat on the cot in his wet coat and stared at the wall, and the splinter was still there when the grey light finally crept through the salt-crusted window. The rain had stopped. The day had come. The rumor was not gone.
