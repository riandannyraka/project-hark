# Chapter 16: The Warning

The cottage was the same. Smoke from the chimney. The net on its frame. The rain barrel full, the water stirring faintly as he came down the path—Renne's element responding to her presence, or to his, or to the space between them. The evening light was grey and going gold at the edges. The wind was up. A storm coming, maybe. Or just the ordinary weather of an island that didn't care what happened on its shores.

Renne was outside. Mending the net. Her hands moved in their familiar rhythm—knotting, pulling, testing each strand before she moved to the next. She didn't look up when he reached the garden. She didn't need to. The water in the barrel had already told her he was coming.

"The Order is coming."

Her hands stilled.

The silence that followed was worse than any sound. The net hung loose on its frame. The water in the barrel went flat and motionless. Renne looked at him—her grey eyes steady, unblinking, the same grey as the sea before a storm—and for a long moment, she didn't speak.

"How do you know."

"Courier job. Message from someone inside the Order. Delivered it to an old woman on the next island. She said the cell needs to move."

"What else did she say."

"Days. Maybe a week. Message said *soon*. Said *be ready*."

Renne stood. The net fell from her hands—not dramatic, just dropped, the twine pooling at her feet. She wiped her palms on her trousers. The motion was practical. Unhurried. Her hands were steady, but the water in the barrel was stirring again, small ripples spreading from the centre, and Hark knew she was working to keep it calm.

"You came back." Not a question.

"Yes."

"You could have run."

He looked at her. At her hands—still now, finally still, the first time he'd seen them motionless since he'd walked into her garden three days ago. At her face—the lines around her eyes, the set of her jaw, the way she was holding herself together with a kind of deliberate calm that he recognized. He'd done the same thing. For years. He was still doing it.

"I did run," he said. "For fifteen years. I'm tired."

The words came out before he could stop them. His legs ached. His shoulders ached. The cold in his fingers had been there so long it was just weather now—permanent, unremarkable, the same grey sky every morning. He'd mapped a thousand exits. Checked a thousand locks. Eaten a thousand meals that tasted of nothing. The ropewalk room. The Factorage. The Weeping Dog. The same faces, the same rhythms, the same silence at the end of every day.

The carriage. The silk. The handler's clean hands. The instructor's flat voice saying *again*. The forest, the fever spikes, the years of being no one in particular. A loose thread. Frayed. Faded.

He was still standing. That was the problem. Fifteen years, and he was still standing, and he didn't know why.

The admission didn't kill him. That was something.

Renne said nothing. She took his hand. His gloved hand. The leather was old and cracked, worn thin at the palm where his knife-hilt rubbed, and her fingers wrapped around his and held on. She held on. The water in the barrel went still.

"Your hands are cold."

"They're always cold."

"I know." Her thumb pressed against the seam of the glove—the one he'd stitched on the cot in the ropewalk room, twelve stitches, waxed cord. "I'm sorry."

He didn't know what to do with that. The words were locked behind the door, and the door was still closed, and her hand was warm. Water Gates ran cool, but her hand was warm. Or maybe his was just so cold that everything felt warm by comparison. He didn't pull away. He didn't know how.

Inside the cottage, the fire was burning low. The door closed behind him. Window—unshuttered, dark. Back door—ajar, a crate propping it open. Nine people. Two exits. Bad math.

Sword on his back. Knife at his belt. Crossbow still at the ferry. Enough. It would have to be enough.

Corin was pacing. The boy's hands were fidgeting worse than usual—tapping his thighs, his belt, the hilt of a knife that looked too new to be useful. The air around him was unsettled, pressure spiking and dropping in irregular waves. Pulse mutation. The living communication relay, broadcasting stress to anyone who knew how to listen.

Marta was packing. Her hands were steady as always—rolling blankets, wrapping dried fish in cloth, stacking supplies in a crate that had seen better decades. She didn't look up when Hark and Renne entered. She didn't need to. The packing told the story.

The others were there. Five of them, names Hark hadn't learned. A young man with a burn scar across his jaw—Fire Gate, base state, his debt-pattern shallow and uneven. A woman with grey-streaked hair and the particular stillness of an Earth Gate who'd learned to guard by becoming immovable. Two boys who might have been brothers, both Wind, both fidgeting in different rhythms. A girl with a shaved head and a tattoo on her wrist that might have been a fish or might have been a knife—the barmaid from the Gull's Rest, the one who'd said *questions cost extra*. She met his eyes. Didn't smile. Didn't look away.

"This is all of you," Hark said.

"This is all of us." Renne closed the cottage door. The fire flickered. The room was too small for nine people, but they were in it together, and the walls were holding.

"You can't all run together. That's a pattern. The Order hunts patterns."

"I know."

"A group of nine moving through the islands leaves a trail. Ferrymen talk. Dockmasters talk. The Order has informants everywhere. You move as one, they find you as one. They find you as one, they take you all."

Renne looked at him. "Then what."

"You disperse. Multiple cells. Multiple islands. Two here. Three there. No group bigger than four. No communication between cells unless it's face-to-face. No patterns. No routines. You move. You keep moving. You don't stop until the Order stops looking."

"The Order never stops looking."

"No. But they prioritise. A single mage is a loose thread. A network of cells is a threat. Right now you're a network. Break the network. Become threads again. Threads are harder to find."

Marta set down the crate. Her hands were still. "He's right."

Corin stopped pacing. "I hate when he's right."

"Someone has to be." Hark looked around the room. The firelight caught their faces—young, old, scared, determined. The burn-scarred Fire-mage was watching him with something that might have been hope. The Earth-mage woman was nodding slowly. The barmaid had her arms crossed and her jaw set. "You've been building something here. Guarding techniques. Training. A way to survive without the Order. If the Order takes you all at once, that knowledge dies. You disperse, the knowledge survives even if the cell doesn't."

"And if the Order finds one of the cells?" Corin's voice was sharp, too fast. "What then?"

"Then that cell goes silent. The others keep running. You don't come back for them. You don't send messages. You don't look back."

"That's cold."

"It's survival."

Corin opened his mouth. Closed it. His hands were still fidgeting, but the rhythm had changed—slower, more deliberate. "How do you know all this."

Hark didn't answer. He didn't need to. The frost-scars under his gloves were answer enough. The fifteen years of running. The geometry of leaving, mapped fresh every morning. The carriage. The silk. The handler's hands.

Renne was still holding his hand. She hadn't let go. He didn't pull away.

"We'll need a false trail," she said. "Something to draw them away from the real routes. Something flashy enough to look like a cell escaping."

"I can do that."

Everyone looked at him. The fire popped. A log shifted, sending sparks up the chimney. Renne's hand tightened on his.

"You're Fire," Corin said. "Light mutation. You could—"

"Yes."

"That's not a trail. That's a beacon. They'll follow it straight to you."

"That's the point."

Moss on north-facing stone. Vivid green against grey.

He held it. One breath. Two. The stone was wet with mist. The moss was soft and thick and alive in a way that had nothing to do with fire or frost or the ledger in his flesh. Small. Fragile. His alone.

Then it faded. The silence in the room was heavier than before.

"You'd burn your cover," Renne said. Her voice was low. "Fifteen years of hiding. Gone."

"They already know a Fire-mage delivered the message. The old woman will tell them, if she hasn't already. I'm not invisible anymore."

"You never were." She looked at him. Her grey eyes were steady, unblinking. "Not to me."

He didn't answer. He didn't know how. The plan was forming—the false trail, the Light mutation, the sacrifice of the invisibility he'd spent fifteen years building. He would draw the hunters away from the real cells. He would be the pattern they chased. He would be the fire they followed, and while they followed him, the others would scatter. Renne. Corin. Marta. The girl with the shaking fingers. The knowledge would survive. The teaching would survive.

He might not.

That was the contract. Bad terms. Worse payout. He'd signed worse.

"We move tonight," he said. "Pack light. No more than you can carry at a run. Renne, you take the girl and head east—there's a fishing village on the next island, a woman who owes me a favour. Corin, you and the Earth-mage go south. Marta—"

"I'll take the brothers," Marta said. "They need feeding."

"Fine. The barmaid and the Fire-mage go west. Stay off the ferries. Steal a boat if you have to. Don't tell anyone where you're going. Don't tell each other. The less you know, the less the Order can take."

"And you?" Corin's voice was still sharp. Still too fast. But his hands had stopped fidgeting.

"I'll give them something to chase."

The room was quiet. The plan settled over them like a second silence. Then Marta picked up her crate. Corin turned toward the door. The others started moving—gathering packs, rolling blankets, the quiet efficiency of people who'd been expecting bad news and had finally received it.

Renne didn't let go of his hand.

"You don't have to do this."

"I know."

"Then why."

He looked at her. The firelight caught her face—the grey eyes, the dark hair, the hands that were finally still. The vortex. The way she'd said *invitation*. The way she'd sat across from him at the fire and not watched him eat. The way she'd held his hand and said *I'm sorry* like she meant it.

The door in his chest was open. He didn't know what was on the other side.

"I'll find you," he said. "After."

"You'd better."

"I don't make promises."

"I know." She squeezed his hand. Once. Then let go. "Don't die."

"That's the plan."

"It's a bad plan."

"Most plans are."

She almost smiled. The almost was enough. She turned and started packing, and Hark stood by the fire and watched the room empty—watched the cells form, the supplies divide, the maps get drawn on scraps of paper that would be burned before anyone left. The false trail was forming in his mind. Fire. Light. A path through the islands that would be visible to any mage-detector within a hundred miles. He would be the beacon. He would be the pattern. He would be the thing they chased.

He flexed his fingers. The leather creaked. The cold was still there. But his hand was warm where Renne had held it, and the warmth was still there too.

The night was waiting. The Order was coming. Somewhere in the dark, hunters were already moving.

He'd been running for fifteen years. He was done running.

That was the plan. Bad terms. Worse payout. He'd signed worse.
