# Chapter 17: The Dispersal

No moon. The dark was absolute, the kind of dark that made the islands disappear and left only the sound of the sea and the wind in the scrub and the quiet shuffle of feet on packed dirt. Hark stood at the edge of the garden and watched them go.

Corin first. The boy's hands were still fidgeting, but the rhythm was different now—controlled, deliberate, the tremor of someone who'd stopped panicking and started moving. He was heading east, to an island with a pulse-relay station, where his mutation would be an asset instead of a liability. Hark had given him the route. The destination. The contingency. *If the relay station is compromised, keep going. There's a fishing village two miles past it. A woman with a red door. Tell her Soric sent you.* Corin had nodded, his eyes too wide, his voice too fast. "You're really staying." "Someone has to." "That's insane." "That's the job." Corin had opened his mouth. Closed it. Then he'd walked into the dark, and the air pressure had settled behind him, and he was gone.

Marta next. She took the brothers—both Wind Gates, both too young to be running, both old enough to know what happened if they stopped. Marta's hands were steady as always, a pack on her back, a crate in her arms, her eyes pale and clear in the dark. She didn't say goodbye. She said: "You're too thin. Eat something." Then she walked west, toward a fishing village where she had family, or said she did, and the brothers followed her like ducklings. Hark watched until the dark swallowed them. He didn't wave. He didn't call out. He didn't know how.

The barmaid—her name was Kell, he'd learned it five minutes before she left—went south with the burn-scarred Fire-mage. She had a knife on her belt that wasn't for show and a tattoo on her wrist that was definitely a fish, and she met his eyes before she walked. "You're the one from the Gull's Rest," she said. "Didn't smile then. Not smiling now." "Consistency," he said. "It's overrated." Then she was gone, the Fire-mage trailing behind her, his debt-pattern still shallow and uneven but his shoulders set. He'd survive. Or he wouldn't. The Archipelago didn't care. The Order didn't care. Hark filed the thought and moved on.

The others scattered. North, south, deeper into the islands. The girl with the shaking fingers—the one who'd been learning to shape water—went with the Earth-mage woman, their packs lighter than they should have been, their path winding up into the hills where the trees grew thick and the Order's hounds would struggle to track. Hark gave each of them a route. A destination. A contingency. He had been planning escapes since he was ten years old. The geometry of leaving was his first language. The dormitory. The east door. The delivery yard. The wall. The forest. He'd planned Lea's escape, and it had failed, and she had died. He'd planned his own escape, and it had succeeded, and he had lived. The difference was luck and light and the angle of a crossbow bolt. He didn't think about that. He kept planning.

The cottage was empty now. The fire was dead. The packs were gone. The net still hung on its frame, half-mended, the twine loose where Renne had dropped it. The rain barrel was full and still. The garden was trampled from a dozen feet. The silence was the silence of a place that had been alive and was now waiting.

Renne was the last to leave.

She stood with him in the dark. The cottage was a shadow behind them. The sea was a sound below. Her pack was on her back, lighter than his would have been, and her hands were still—finally still, the first time he'd seen them motionless without effort. She'd stopped moving. She'd stopped because she was looking at him.

"What about you."

"I'm staying."

"That's suicide."

"It's misdirection."

She understood. He saw it in her face—the way her jaw tightened, the way her grey eyes flicked toward the sky and back, the way her hands stayed still because if they moved she might not be able to stop them. She was a strategist. She'd kept a cell alive for years under the Order's nose. She knew what a false trail cost. She knew what it was worth.

"You're going to draw them away."

"Someone has to."

She took his hand. His gloved hand. The leather was cold—colder than usual, the night air sinking into it, the frost underneath spreading its slow white lines. She held it. Her thumb found the seam he'd stitched on the cot in the ropewalk room, twelve stitches, waxed cord, and pressed against it.

"Come back."

He said nothing. He couldn't promise. Promises were contracts, and he didn't sign contracts he couldn't honour. The odds were bad. The plan was worse. He might not come back. She knew that. He knew she knew.

She kissed him.

Quick. Hard. On the mouth. Her lips were warm—Water Gates ran cool, but her lips were warm, or maybe his were just so cold that everything felt warm by comparison. The kiss tasted like salt. The sea. The sweat on her upper lip. The particular brine of an island that never let you forget where you were.

Then she was gone. Walking into the dark, her pack on her back, her hands still at her sides. She didn't look back. He didn't call out. The dark swallowed her, and the sound of her footsteps faded, and he was alone.

Moss on north-facing stone. Vivid green against grey.

The image surfaced—unbidden, unwelcome—and he held it. The stone was wet with mist. The moss was soft and thick and alive in a way that had nothing to do with fire or frost or the ledger in his flesh. Small. Fragile. His alone.

One breath. Two.

Then it faded. The night came back. The silence came back. He was still standing.

Lea had never kissed him.

They had been children. Too young. Too scared. Too focused on the next breath, the next meal, the next working that wouldn't kill them. The dormitory was dark and cold and full of the breathing of other children who were also trying not to die. He had wondered, sometimes, in the quiet between sleep and waking, what it would be like. He had imagined something soft. Something warm. A hand on his face that wasn't checking for fever. Lips that didn't speak orders.

This was not soft. This was desperate and quick and tasted like salt. Renne had kissed him like she was trying to leave something behind—a mark, a memory, a warmth that would last longer than the cold. He stood in the dark, alone, and touched his lips. The glove was rough against his skin. His lips were cold. They were always cold. He didn't know if that would ever change.

The sounds of their departure faded. Footsteps on dirt. A pebble dislodged. The creak of a pack strap. Then nothing. The wind. The sea. The silence of a place that had been alive and was now empty.

He waited. Counted to a hundred. Counted to a hundred again. Then he walked. Not toward Salthaven. Not toward the ferry. Toward the mainland. Toward the Order.

He opened his gate.

Not the internal reservoir—the furnace in his chest, the heat that was his alone, the warmth he'd guarded for fifteen years. He drew from the environment. The night air was cold, but there was heat in it. Ambient heat. The residual warmth of the day still bleeding out of the earth. The faint thermals rising from the sea. He pulled it in. Concentrated it. Held it.

The frost spread. Across his fingers. His palms. His wrists. The debt was immediate and physical—a sting, a splitting of skin, new lines writing themselves across his knuckles, the leather of his gloves creaking stiff with cold. His body was a ledger, and the ledger was updating itself, and the entry was large and red and would never be erased.

He walked for an hour. The path climbed. The trees thinned. The sea fell away behind him, and the mainland rose ahead, and the sky was huge and black and full of stars he'd forgotten to look at. He stopped at the crest of a ridge. The highest point on the island. Visible from every direction. A beacon waiting to be lit.

He raised his hand.

The Light mutation ignited. Not flame—pure illumination, white and searing, a beam thin as a needle. It cut through the clouds, a pillar of light reaching up into the dark, brighter than the stars, brighter than anything. The frost on his knuckles screamed. The fever-heat climbed inside him, his body paying the debt internally, his skin steaming in the cold air. He was burning alive from the inside and freezing to death from the outside, and the light was a wound in the sky, and it was beautiful.

He held it for ten seconds. Twenty. Long enough to be tracked. Long enough to be remembered. Then he closed his gate, and the light died, and the dark came rushing back.

Sword on his back. Knife at his belt. Crossbow at the ferry—too far now. His hands were shaking. The frost had reached his elbows. The ledger was updated.

Enough. It would have to be enough.

He walked down from the ridge. Toward the mainland. Toward the Order. The false trail was lit. The hunters were coming. The plan was in motion.

The night was dark. The path was long. The cold in his fingers was the only company he had.

He walked. Didn't look back. Didn't stop.

The stars were still there. He didn't look at them.
