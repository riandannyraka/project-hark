# BOOK ONE: FIRST PRINCIPLES

*A Note on Audience: This book is written for the novice, the regulator, and the scholar. The veteran mage will find nothing new here except the shape of their own mistakes described in cold prose. The regulator will find the legal definitions that underpin magical jurisdiction. The scholar will find the axioms from which all else derives.*

---

## 1. What Magic Is

Magic is borrowing. This is the First Principle, from which every other rule follows.

The universe exists in a state of equilibrium — energy distributed, temperature balanced, pressure equalized, charge neutralized. To work magic is to disturb that equilibrium intentionally, taking something from one place (or body) and putting it somewhere else, usually with force.

The mage does not create. The mage redirects. A flame bolt is not new fire. It is borrowed heat, concentrated and released. A wave is not new water. It is borrowed fluid, shaped and thrown. The breath stolen from an enemy's lungs is not destroyed. It is simply elsewhere — dispersed into air that will never taste it again.

This is why magic tires. This is why magic scars. This is why magic kills. The mage is a conduit, not a source. Every working leaves a deficit somewhere.

---

## 2. The Two Reservoirs

Every mage has access to two sources of their element: the internal and the environmental.

**Internal reservoir:** The element already present in the mage's own body.

- Fire: body heat, blood-warmth, metabolic fire.
- Water: blood, interstitial fluid, cellular water.
- Wind: breath in the lungs, gas exchange, vocal pressure.
- Earth: minerals in bone and blood, bio-potential.
- Lightning: nerve signals, muscle impulses, bioelectricity.

The internal reservoir is always available. It requires no setup, no external conditions. But it is finite. Deplete it too far, and the body begins to consume itself. Fire-mages grow cold and die of hypothermia in summer. Water-mages desiccate from within. Wind-mages suffocate on full lungs. Earth-mages' bones crumble. Lightning-mages' nerves go silent.

The internal reservoir replenishes naturally — heat from digestion and movement, water from drinking, breath from breathing, minerals from food, charge from rest. But replenishment takes time. A mage who empties their internal reservoir cannot refill it quickly enough to matter in a fight.

**Environmental sources:** The same element present outside the mage's body.

- Fire: sun-baked stone, geothermal vents, ambient heat, the warmth of other living things.
- Water: humidity, standing water, rivers, sap, the water inside other creatures.
- Wind: moving air, thermal currents, pressure gradients, the exhalations of others.
- Earth: soil, stone, deep-root systems, seismic hum.
- Lightning: atmospheric charge, static friction, storm potential, piezoelectric stress.

Environmental borrowing is more powerful than internal borrowing, sometimes vastly so. But it is not always available. A fire-mage in a frozen cave, a water-mage in a desert, a wind-mage in a sealed room, an earth-mage on a ship at sea, a lightning-mage under a clear, still sky — all are reduced to their internal reservoirs. This is why mages carry environmental triggers (kindling, waterskins, open spaces, bare ground, conductive rods) and why experienced mages choose their battlegrounds carefully.

---

## 3. The Debt Principle

Every working incurs a debt. The debt is the deficit left behind — the heat that was taken, the water that was moved, the breath that was stolen, the mineral that was borrowed, the charge that was discharged.

The debt must be paid. This is the Second Principle, as inviolable as the First.

Payment takes three forms:

**Environmental debt:** The element is taken from the environment. The environment manifests the deficit — local cold, aridity, windless silence, brittle ground, dead zones of charge. Environmental debt disperses naturally over time, though large debts can leave permanent scars on a landscape.

**Self debt:** The element is taken from the mage's own body. The mage manifests the deficit directly — their own limbs numbing, their own mouth drying, their own lungs labouring, their own bones aching, their own nerves misfiring. Self debt is paid immediately and personally.

**Affliction debt:** The element is taken from another living creature, usually an enemy. The victim manifests the deficit — hypothermia, desiccation, suffocation, bone-softening, neural disruption. Affliction debt transfers the cost of magic onto the target. This is why Affliction is treated as a distinct category of magical assault, carrying harsher penalties than conventional combat magic. The debt falls on a person rather than the environment.

---

## 4. The Ledger in Flesh

Debt that is not immediately paid does not disappear. It accumulates. It compounds. It waits.

This is the Third Principle: **The body remembers.**

Every mage bears physical markers of unpaid debt — the permanent record of every working that took more than was replenished. These markers are not merely cosmetic. They are the ledger written in flesh.

- Fire-mages who habitually borrow heat without environmental replacement carry frost-scars on their fingers, permanent numbness in their extremities, a bone-deep chill that never fully leaves.
- Water-mages who overdraw their internal reservoir develop cracked lips, dry eyes, a thirst that water cannot quench.
- Wind-mages who exhaust their breath without environmental replenishment suffer reduced lung capacity, a perpetual shortness of air.
- Earth-mages who borrow minerals from their own bones develop chronic anaemia, brittle joints, a grey pallor.
- Lightning-mages who overclock their nerves accumulate tremors, stutters, phantom numbness, a creeping neural fog.

These markers are legible. A trained observer — a combat veteran, a specialised physician, a Mage-Auditor — can read the gross history of a mage's debt in the tremor of their hands, the frost-scars on their fingers, the grey of their skin, the distant quality of their gaze.

---

## 5. The Five Gates — Introduction

The five Gates are the fundamental channels through which magic flows. Each Gate governs a distinct domain of physical reality. A mage is born to one Gate and may deepen within it indefinitely, but never crosses to another.

- **Fire:** Domain of transformation. Fuel into flame, matter into energy, living into memory.
- **Water:** Domain of transition. Liquid, solid, gas; solvent and solution.
- **Wind:** Domain of motion and vibration. Displacement of air, transmission of pressure.
- **Earth:** Domain of structure and persistence. The solid, the mineral, the slow architecture of the world.
- **Lightning:** Domain of signal and impulse. The spark of thought, the flash of reaction.

No Gate is superior to another in principle. In practice, circumstances determine advantage. A water-mage on a river is formidable. A water-mage in a desert is desperate. A lightning-mage in a storm is terrifying. A lightning-mage in a copper-lined room is helpless.

---

## 6. Guarding the Reservoir

A mage can actively prevent their internal reservoir from being borrowed — whether by an enemy's Affliction or by their own overuse. This is called guarding.

Guarding is not a separate spell. It is a conscious partitioning of attention. The mage "holds" their internal element, refusing to let it flow outward without consent.

**The Cost:** Guarding degrades offensive casting. A mage who guards fully cannot cast at all — their element is locked away, inaccessible. They are a fortress, but a passive one. A mage who splits attention between guarding and casting suffers diminished power and precision in both. A mage who casts without guarding is fully potent but fully exposed.

**The Dynamic:** Combat between mages becomes a calculus of risk. Guard too heavily, and you concede the initiative. Guard too lightly, and your enemy steals your body heat before you finish your first working. The most lethal duellists are those who read their opponent's rhythm and drop their guard only in the instant of their own strike — a window of vulnerability so brief only years of training can exploit it.

**The Exception:** The desperate and the untrained cannot guard. They do not know how. This is why Affliction is so devastating against civilians, and why untrained sensitives are such easy victims for predatory mages.

---

## 7. The Debt Cannot Be Cheated

Every working incurs a debt. That debt must be paid, by someone, somewhere. Attempts to evade payment result in the debt accumulating interest — worsening, compounding, until it is called due all at once.

History records mages who tried to cheat the economy. Their fates are instructional.

The most famous case is the Ash-Mage of Kestrel Downs, who attempted to pre-pay environmental debt into a geothermal vent, then draw from it simultaneously, hoping the flow would cancel. For seventeen days, it worked. On the eighteenth day, the vent went supercritical, the debt interest compounded to seven times its original value, and the Ash-Mage was found as a scattering of cinders across three acres. The vent still smokes.

---

## 8. The Mage as Conduit

The most feared mage is not the one with the deepest internal reservoir. It is the one who has learned to make others carry the debt while they walk away clean — or appear to. Such mages are rare. Most are eventually consumed by the accumulated moral and metaphysical interest of their choices.

A mage who consistently uses Affliction — forcing enemies to pay their debts — may appear untouched for years. Their flesh bears no frost-scars, no dry veins, no tremors. They seem to have cheated. But the ledger does not forget. Affliction debt transferred to another is still debt, and debt transferred through violence leaves a different kind of mark — not on the body, but on the self. Affliction-mages grow cold in a way that has nothing to do with heat. They lose the capacity for surprise, for joy, for grief. They become instruments.

This is not metaphor. This is the Fourth Principle: **The moral debt is also real.**

# BOOK TWO: THE INSENSITIVE AND THE SENSITIVE

*A Note on Terminology: This book addresses two populations that exist outside the normal economy of magehood — those who cannot touch magic at all, and those who cannot stop being touched by it. They are opposites but not equivalents. One is immune. The other is exposed. Both are essential to the Order's work.*

---

## PART ONE: THE INSENSITIVE

### 1. Definition

The Insensitive — sometimes called blanks, voids, nulls, or (in formal legal contexts) "immune persons" — are born with a total absence of magical conductivity. Where a mage's body acts as a conduit, the Insensitive's body acts as an insulator. Magic flows around them, not through them.

This has two consequences, equally absolute:

**First:** The Insensitive cannot work magic. They have no internal reservoir to borrow from. Environmental sources do not respond to them. The Gates are closed. A lifetime of training would produce nothing.

**Second:** The Insensitive cannot be worked upon. Affliction passes through them without effect. An enemy fire-mage cannot steal their body heat. A water-mage cannot desiccate them. A wind-mage cannot steal their breath. An earth-mage cannot leach their bones. A lightning-mage cannot disrupt their nerves. Guarding, for the Insensitive, is not a skill — it is their permanent, effortless state.

This immunity extends to environmental magic as well. An Insensitive standing in the path of a flame bolt is not burned by the magical fire — though if the fire ignites a mundane wooden floor, the mundane fire will burn them normally. The immunity applies to the magical working itself, not to its secondary, non-magical consequences.

---

### 2. Origin

Insensitivity is congenital. It cannot be acquired through injury, training, or ritual. It cannot be faked. It cannot be cured.

The trait appears to be recessive, though the genetics are poorly understood. Two mage parents may produce an Insensitive child. Two Insensitive parents always produce an Insensitive child. A mage and an Insensitive produce a mage approximately seventy percent of the time, with the remaining thirty percent split between Insensitive and non-magical (see Appendix: The Non-Magical Population).

There is a persistent folk belief that Insensitivity results from prenatal exposure to heavy debt — a mother who suffered Affliction during pregnancy, or who worked magic while carrying. The Magical Academies have studied this extensively and found no causal link. The belief persists anyway. Insensitive children born to mage families are sometimes treated as omens, curses, or punishments.

---

### 3. The Savants

A subset of the Insensitive — approximately one in twelve — possess an additional capacity that makes them invaluable and feared.

**Savants can read debt.** Where a trained physician sees frost-scars and guesses at a fire-mage's history, a Savant sees the full ledger: every deepening, every unpaid balance, every Affliction transferred, every year of accumulated interest. They perceive magical debt with the same direct, unfiltered clarity that an ordinary person perceives colour.

This reading is not learned. It is instinctive. A Savant does not deduce a mage's history — they *see* it. The testimony of a Savant is considered definitive in magical law. Mages who wish to hide their history fear Savants above all others.

The mechanism is unknown. The leading theory, proposed by the Magisterium of the Third Century, suggests that Savants perceive the residual disturbance of the magical economy — the "shape" left behind when debt moves through a medium. Mages, being conductive, retain that shape indefinitely. Savants simply look and describe what they see.

**Limitations of Savants:**

- They cannot read non-magical persons (the ledger is blank).
- They cannot read environmental debt except in its most concentrated forms (e.g., a geothermal vent that has been used as a pre-payment sink).
- They cannot read debt that has been fully paid (a healed fire-mage with no remaining frost-scars registers as clean).
- The reading requires proximity. A Savant must be within arm's reach of the subject for precise detail, though gross impressions (gate, rough depth of deepening, presence of unpaid Affliction debt) can be read from across a room.

**Social Status of Savants:**

Savants occupy a strange and often uncomfortable position. They are Insensitive — unable to work magic, immune to Affliction — but their perceptual gift makes them indispensable to the magical legal system. Most jurisdictions require a Savant's testimony in any case involving disputed Affliction or contested debt. Without a Savant, a mage could lie about their history with impunity.

This necessity grants Savants a kind of power. A Savant can destroy a mage's reputation with a single sentence. A Savant can exonerate or condemn. Unsurprisingly, Savants are often hated by the mages they examine. Unsurprisingly, Savants are also often protected by the authorities who rely on their testimony.

The relationship is transactional and tense. Savants are paid well, housed separately, and rarely trusted. Many live isolated from both magical and non-magical communities, moving from jurisdiction to jurisdiction as their contracts dictate.

---

### 4. Insensitivity vs. Guarding

The difference between an Insensitive and a guarding mage is frequently misunderstood.

A guarding mage *chooses* to lock away their internal reservoir. The choice requires training, attention, and constant maintenance. A moment's lapse, a moment of surprise, and the guard falls. A guarding mage can also drop their guard deliberately, casting at full potency when the moment is right.

An Insensitive has no choice because they have no reservoir to lock. Their immunity is not maintained — it simply *is*. They cannot drop their guard because they have no guard to drop. They cannot cast, ever, under any circumstances.

To use a fortress analogy: a guarding mage is a citadel with gates that can be opened from within. An Insensitive is a solid block of stone. The stone is safer. The stone also cannot send messengers, launch sorties, or surrender.

---

### 5. Social Status of the Non-Savant Insensitive

The majority of the Insensitive — those who cannot read debt — occupy a different social position than Savants. They are, from a magical perspective, invisible. They cannot be targeted. They cannot be used as reservoirs. They cannot be read. They simply *are*.

In magical societies, this invisibility is often treated as deficiency. The Insensitive are pitied, dismissed, or quietly excluded from positions of authority (how can someone who cannot work magic understand the cost of magic?). In non-magical communities, the Insensitive often pass unnoticed, indistinguishable from the non-magical population.

Some Insensitive choose to hide their status, pretending to be non-magical to avoid the complicated social dynamics of magical society. Others wear markers of their immunity proudly — a silver ring, a particular cut of coat — signalling that they cannot be borrowed from.

**Legal Protections:** In most jurisdictions, Affliction against an Insensitive is not merely assault but *impossible* — the charge becomes attempted Affliction, which carries its own penalties but is treated as incompetence rather than malice. Killing an Insensitive with mundane means carries the same legal weight as killing anyone else. Killing an Insensitive by magical means is impossible, though killing them by the secondary effects of magic (the mundane fire from a flame bolt, the falling debris from an earth-working) is fully possible and legally indistinguishable from mundane murder.

---

### 6. The Insensitive in Warfare

Armies value the Insensitive for one specific role: bodyguards to high-value targets.

A general who fears magical assassination surrounds themselves with Insensitive attendants. The assassin's Affliction slides off. The assassin's bolts disperse before they touch. The assassin must resort to mundane methods — a crossbow, a poison, a knife — which can be countered by mundane means.

Insensitive bodyguards are expensive. They are rare. And they are often killed first by mundane soldiers precisely because they are known to be the general's last line of defence.

Some Insensitive have trained as warriors specifically to counter this vulnerability. A Savant who can also wield a sword, an Insensitive who has learned the crossbow — these are terrifying figures, immune to magic and deadly at mundane range. They are called **Silencers**, and they are the single greatest threat a mage can face. A Silencer walks through flame bolts and lightning arcs without flinching, then shoots the mage with a steel-tipped bolt. The mage dies wondering why their magic failed.

The existence of Silencers is the final check on magical power in this world. No mage, no matter how deep their mutations, no matter how vast their internal reservoir, is safe from a determined Insensitive with a crossbow. The debt always settles. Sometimes it settles in blood.

---

## PART TWO: THE SENSITIVE

### 1. Definition

In common usage, "Sensitive" refers to any untrained magical child who has not yet learned to guard their internal reservoir. The term carries no implication of congenital difference — all mages are born Sensitive. The distinction is purely developmental: a Sensitive is a mage who has not been taught to hide.

A Sensitive is a child with an open Gate and no training.

That is all. Every mage is born with the capacity to borrow. But borrowing is not automatic — it requires intent, or desperation, or the slow accretion of habit. A child who has never worked magic, who has never been taught to reach for their element, is still a Sensitive because their internal reservoir is unguarded.

An unguarded reservoir leaks. Not element — the element stays in the body until borrowed. What leaks is *information*. A trained mage can sense another trained mage's gate, depth, and approximate debt only through close contact or active reading. But an untrained child broadcasts these things constantly, like a candle in darkness, visible to anyone who knows how to look.

This is the Sensitive's tragedy: they are not dangerous. They are not powerful. They are simply *visible* — and visibility, in a world that collects magical children, is a death sentence to freedom.

---

### 2. Visibility and the Savants

The Order maintains a network of agents trained to detect Sensitives. Most of these agents are mages with a specific skill: the ability to perceive the "leakage" of an unguarded reservoir. This is not a mutation. It is a learnable sensitivity, akin to developing an ear for a language. Any mage can be trained to do it, though some are naturally better than others.

But the Order's most valuable assets are the Savants — Insensitives who can read debt itself.

A mage-detector *feels* the presence of an unguarded reservoir. A Savant *sees* the ledger. Where a trained mage might sense a child as "Fire, shallow depth, probably no mutations," a Savant reads the exact history: "Fire, second deepening, ice-path initiated, unpaid debt from a working three days ago, scar pattern consistent with a teacher who pushed too hard."

Savant testimony is definitive because Savants cannot be fooled. A trained mage can guard their reservoir, hiding their signature. But a Savant does not read the reservoir. They read the *debt* — and debt is permanent. A mage who has ever worked magic, who has ever borrowed, carries that debt in their flesh forever. A Savant sees it as clearly as a scar.

This is why Savants are feared. A mage can hide from other mages. A mage cannot hide from a Savant.

---

### 3. The Order's Collection Practices

The Order collects Sensitives. This is not a secret. The Order has legal authority to remove untrained magical children from their families in every major jurisdiction. The justification is simple and brutal: an untrained Sensitive is a danger to themselves and everyone around them.

The danger is not that the child will spontaneously work magic. The danger is that *someone else* will work magic on them.

An unguarded reservoir is an open door. A predatory mage — or a desperate one — can borrow from a Sensitive without consent, drawing heat, water, breath, minerals, or charge directly from the child's body. The Sensitive cannot resist. They do not know how to guard. They do not even know what is happening until they feel their warmth draining, their lungs seizing, their bones aching.

The Order collects Sensitives to protect them from this. That is the stated purpose. That is the legal justification. That is what the Order tells itself, and what it tells the families, and what it writes in its charters.

But the Order also *uses* Sensitives. A collected child is trained, or contained, or — in the case of those deemed unsuitable for training — simply held. The Order does not release Sensitives. Once collected, a child belongs to the Order until the Order decides otherwise.

Some get out. Most don't.

---

### 4. The Silk

The Order binds Sensitives in silk. White silk, clean silk, expensive silk.

Silk does not chafe. Silk does not leave marks. Silk is not cruel — or rather, its cruelty is invisible. A child bound in silk can be transported without visible injury, without evidence of coercion. If questioned, the Order can show the unmarked wrists and say: *We treat them well.*

Silk also serves a practical purpose. It does not conduct magic. A metal chain might accidentally ground a lightning-Sensitive's accumulated charge. A rope might absorb moisture from a water-Sensitive's skin. Silk is inert, clean, and safe.

The Order does not use silk for mercy. The Order uses silk because it is efficient.

---

### 5. The Price of Untrained Sensitivity

A Sensitive who is not identified and trained is not necessarily in immediate danger of death. Unlike other magical systems where untrained power leads to self-destruction, the Sensitive in this world faces a different risk: *others will use them.*

An untrained child can live for years without ever working magic. Their reservoir remains unguarded but not necessarily active. The danger is external — a mage who needs a quick source of heat, a desperate water-mage in a dry climate, a predator who collects children for their elemental charge.

The Order's position is that every untrained Sensitive is a potential victim. Therefore, every untrained Sensitive must be collected. The logic is circular and, within its own terms, unassailable.

There is no recorded case of an untrained Sensitive spontaneously combusting, drowning on dry land, suffocating from full lungs, petrifying, or seizing to death — unless acted upon by an external mage. The catastrophic self-destruction described in earlier, discredited theories of Sensitivity has never been documented. The Order quietly removed those theories from its training materials decades ago, but the folk belief persists.

A Sensitive left alone is safe. A Sensitive left alone in a world with predatory mages is not. The distinction matters.

---

### 6. The Order's Authority and the Law

The Order operates outside most mundane legal frameworks. They are answerable to no noble, no council, no king. Their authority derives from a simple, brutal logic: *we are the only thing standing between you and a predator who will drain your child's warmth until they die.*

Most jurisdictions have formal treaties with the Order, granting them right of entry, right of seizure, and right of confinement. In exchange, the Order provides trained mages for military service (on a contract basis, never as conscripts) and shares intelligence about Sensitive births.

The relationship is tense. Families fight the Order. Nobles try to bribe the Order. Armies try to steal from the Order. The Order outlasts them all. Centuries of precedent, centuries of saving lives, centuries of being right — the Order does not negotiate. The Order does not explain. The Order takes the child, and the world continues spinning.

**Criticism:** The Order has its detractors. Some argue that forced removal of Sensitive children is a violation of bodily autonomy, regardless of the danger. Some argue that the Order's training methods amount to coercion. Some argue that the Order hoards trained mages as a political weapon, deploying them only when it serves the Order's interests rather than the world's.

The Order's response to all criticism is the same: *Show us a better way, and we will adopt it. Until then, be grateful we exist.*

No one has shown them a better way.

---

### 7. Trained Sensitives as Mages

A trained Sensitive is simply a mage. The term "Sensitive" drops away once the child learns to guard. There is no permanent distinction between a mage who was collected as a child and a mage who was trained by a parent or a private tutor. The only difference is the method of recruitment.

This is why the Order collects. They do not believe that private training is sufficient. They do not believe that parents can be trusted to teach guarding effectively. They believe that only the Order's methods produce mages who are fully protected, fully controlled, and fully loyal.

Hark is evidence to the contrary. Hark was collected. Hark got out. Hark learned to guard somewhere else — probably on his own, probably through trial and error, probably at great cost. He is a trained mage. He is not loyal to the Order.

The Order knows Hark exists. The Order does not know where he is. The carriage in the street was not looking for him. But if a Savant ever stands close enough to read him, Hark's debt will tell its story, and the Order will come.

---

### 8. The Insensitive and the Sensitive: A Relationship

The Insensitive cannot work magic at all. They are immune, permanent, static. A subset of them (Savants) can read debt with perfect accuracy. They are the Order's most valuable assets and the mage's greatest fear.

The Sensitive is any untrained magical child. They *can* borrow — but they do not know how to guard. Their visibility is their vulnerability. They are the Order's raw material.

A trained mage is a former Sensitive who has learned to guard, to hide, to control their borrowing and their debt. Hark is a trained mage. The boy with the ruby eyes is not.

The Order collects Sensitives. It employs Savants to read them definitively. It trains some, contains others, and never releases anyone except by accident or escape.

The Insensitive are outside the magical economy entirely. The Sensitive are inside it — but without protection, without agency, without the one skill that would make them safe.

# BOOK THREE: THE FIVE GATES

*A Note on Terminology: Throughout this book, the term "Affliction" refers to a working that transfers debt directly into an opponent's body rather than through environmental damage. Heat-theft, desiccation, breath-stealing, calcium-leaching, and neural disruption are all Afflictions. The term carries legal weight: in most jurisdictions, Affliction is treated as a distinct category of magical assault, often carrying harsher penalties than conventional combat magic because the debt falls on the victim's body rather than the environment.*

---

## 1. FIRE

**Domain:** Transformation. Fuel into flame, matter into energy, living into memory.

**Internal reservoir:** Body heat, blood-warmth, metabolic fire.

**Environmental sources:** Sun-baked stone, geothermal vents, any ambient heat, even the warmth of living things.

**Debt signature:** Localised cold, frost forming on surfaces, the mage's own limbs going numb.

### Base State: Flame Manipulation

Borrowing and directing heat into combustion. Standard combat pyromancy — bolts, walls, explosive spheres. The mage gathers heat from environment and body, focuses it, releases it.

### Body Sense Application

Heat-sight. Perceives warm-blooded creatures even with eyes closed, reading them as blobs of heat-loss in the cool air — a form of infrared sensitivity. Hidden enemies register as warmth-anomalies.

### Kinetic Amplifier

Flash-heats the air beneath their feet for thermal updraft-assisted leaps. Briefly superheats their own muscle fibres to push strength beyond normal limits — at the cost of a debilitating, bone-deep chill afterwards.

### Affliction

Heat-theft. Pulls warmth out of an enemy's body, inducing rapid hypothermia, uncontrollable shivering, confusion, and eventual sleep. Fear often comes as a by-product of the sudden, inexplicable cold.

### Mutation A: Light

Fire stripped of its heat, refined to pure illumination. The mage borrows only luminous energy while leaving the thermal debt unpaid in their own body. Light-mages can blind, signal across vast distances, create illusions of pure light, and — at mastery — produce coherent, cutting beams that sever without burning.

**Debt:** The mage's own body temperature rises dangerously with each working. Light-mages are walking radiators of fever-heat; their skin steams in cold air. Prolonged use is lethal hyperthermia.

### Mutation B: Warmth

Fire tamed to the hearth path. Slow, steady, distributed heat rather than violent combustion. A warmth-mage can heat a frozen village through a blizzard, ripen crops out of season, or sustain a dying soldier by maintaining body temperature against shock. This is the healer-equivalent of the fire gate, treated with corresponding reverence.

**Debt:** The mage pays in persistent, internal chill. Warmth-mages are always cold, always wrapped in blankets, their internal furnace running at a permanent deficit because they gave it away.

### Mutation C: Ash

Fire's memory. The mage learns that combustion leaves behind something precious — information. By tasting ash, they reconstruct what burned: the species of wood, the face of the corpse, the contents of a burnt letter. This is fire-magic as divination, forensic and haunted.

**Debt:** Paid in memory-bleed. The ash-mage gradually loses their own past, the details of their life becoming as scattered as the cinders they read. Old ash-mages forget their own names but can recite the history of a dead empire from a single ember.

### Mutation D: Forge

The crafting path. The smallest, most precise applications of heat — not for destruction, but for transformation of materials. Welding, smelting, glass-blowing, the hardening of steel. Forge-mages are artisans rather than warriors, working at the intersection of fire and the mineral world.

**Debt:** Paid in time. Every item forged shortens the mage's lifespan by a measurable, cumulative fraction. A master smith dies young, surrounded by indestructible swords and armour that will outlast empires.

---

## 2. WATER

**Domain:** Transition. Liquid, solid, gas; solvent and solution; the medium through which life flows and drowns.

**Internal reservoir:** Blood, interstitial fluid, the water in every cell.

**Environmental sources:** Humidity, standing water, rivers, sap in plants, and — most dangerously — the water inside other living beings.

**Debt signature:** Local aridity, cracked lips, wilted leaves, a dry-mouthed thirst that haunts the mage after a working.

### Base State: Liquid Manipulation

Borrowing and redirecting liquid water. Standard combat hydromancy — waves, whips, drowning spheres, pressure-blades. The mage gathers water from environment and body, shapes it, weaponises it.

### Body Sense Application

Life-reading through water. Feels the precise location and emotional state of creatures by sensing their blood flow, sweat, and the minute hydraulic pressure-changes of a beating heart. Hidden enemies are as obvious as if they were sloshing loudly.

### Kinetic Amplifier

Pressurises their own body's fluids to create a hydraulic exoskeleton — an instant, fluidic strength-boost that turns a punch into a water-hammer, or lets them flow through strikes like liquid.

**Debt:** Overuse risks rupturing the mage's own veins.

### Affliction

Desiccation. Draws free water out of an opponent's tissues, leaving them dizzy, cramping, and too weak to stand. In extreme cases, a sudden shift of internal fluids causes incapacitating vertigo or lung-oedema — the enemy drowns in their own phlegm.

### Mutation A: Ice

Water sharpened. The mage learns not just to move water, but to hold it still — stealing heat from the water itself until it locks into crystalline rigidity. This is a double art: the water-mage must also think in terms of heat, drawing thermal energy out of the target. Early students can only freeze standing water; masters flash-freeze the humidity in air into blades, or the fluid in an enemy's joint into immobilising shards.

**Debt:** The void left behind is intense, localised cold that can turn the mage's own fingers black with frostbite. Ice-mages permanently lose sensitivity to warmth.

### Mutation B: Healing

Water sensing deepened to obsession. The mage perceives the body not as flesh but as hydraulics — blood pressure, lymphatic flow, the cerebrospinal tide. They can manually unblock a clot, drain an abscess, rehydrate a dying organ by borrowing water from a healthy one. The rarest and most revered water-path.

**Debt:** A healing always leaves the mage briefly suffering a sympathetic version of the wound — internal bleeding that resolves over days, temporary blindness for restored sight, a month of bone-ache for a mended fracture. The healer takes a portion of the damage into themselves, then slowly flushes it out.

### Mutation C: Blood

Healing taken to its darkest extreme. If the body is a river, blood is its most concentrated expression. A water-mage who learns to sense blood specifically can manipulate it while it's still inside an enemy — borrowing its pressure, halting its flow, boiling it.

**Debt:** All physical debts are paid in the enemy's body rather than the mage's. But the moral debt accumulates. Blood-mages become cold, emotionally still, gradually freezing into something that was once human. They are universally pariahs, not because their power differs from a healer's, but because they have chosen to stop taking the debt upon themselves. That choice warps them.

### Mutation D: Poison

Water as universal solvent, taken to its endpoint. The mage infuses controlled water with extracted essences — plant toxins, mineral corrosives, their own altered saliva or sweat — and delivers them through the water they command. A poison-water mist that burns lungs. A whip that leaves weeping, infected welts.

**Debt:** This path requires the mage to first become immune to their chosen poisons by micro-dosing their own system, paying in chronic, low-level sickness. They are never fully healthy. They smell faintly of the toxins they carry. Recovery is not possible; the poisons are permanent tenants.

## 3. WIND

**Domain:** Motion and vibration. The displacement of air, the transmission of pressure, the medium of sound and respiration.

**Internal reservoir:** The breath in the lungs, the body's own gas exchange, the subtle pressure of the voice.

**Environmental sources:** Any moving air, thermal currents, pressure gradients, the exhalations of other creatures.

**Debt signature:** Windless silence, a suffocating vacuum pocket, an oppressive ear-popping drop in pressure around the mage.

### Base State: Air Manipulation

Borrowing and redirecting air — pushing, pulling, creating pressure differentials, shaping gusts and vacuums. Standard combat aeromancy.

### Body Sense Application

Pressure-reading. Every moving body displaces air; the mage feels these ripples on their skin like a spider feels the web. They can track the breathing rhythm of a hidden enemy, or map a room blind by the shape of its still air.

### Kinetic Amplifier

Lightens their own mass by altering the air-pressure envelope around them, granting long glides, near-silent footsteps, and landings soft as a falling leaf. Punches arrive with a compressed-air piston behind them. A sudden tailwind can triple sprinting speed for a few seconds.

**Debt:** The mage's own lungs labour afterwards, as if they've run the distance themselves.

### Affliction

Breath-stealing. Draws air from an opponent's lungs, inducing instant suffocation and panic. A subtler attack creates inner-ear pressure chaos, triggering vertigo, nausea, and total loss of balance — the enemy stumbles and falls without ever understanding why.

### Mutation A: Sound

A wind-mage who learns that air is not just substance but signal. They can throw their voice miles, listen to conversations through solid walls by feeling vibration, shatter glass with a precisely pitched note, or whisper words directly into a single ear across a crowded room. Masters can cancel sound entirely by matching pressure waves with inverse reflections — active noise cancellation as a combat art.

**Debt:** Paid in the mage's own hearing. Sound-mages are progressively deaf, their eardrums scarred by the constant micro-damage of their own work. Old sound-mages live in perfect silence.

### Mutation B: Silence

The opposite of sound. Specialisation in stillness — the absence of air movement, the vacuum, the pressure-dead zone. A silence-mage moves without rustling fabric. Their footsteps produce no sound. They create pockets of absolute quiet where even a scream cannot reach its own source's ears. The assassin's wind-path.

**Debt:** Paid in the mage's own breathlessness. Silence-mages have reduced lung capacity, always slightly short of air, as if the vacuum they create follows them home. They breathe shallowly, perpetually on the edge of suffocation.

### Mutation C: Breath

The healer-path of wind. A mage who learns to sense and manipulate the breath inside living bodies — clearing obstructed airways, restarting stopped lungs, enriching oxygen intake, or breathing for someone else by manually cycling their diaphragm. In combat, they steal breath; in medicine, they are the patron of the drowned and the choking.

**Debt:** Shared breath. Whatever respiratory ailment they cure, they briefly suffer. A breath-mage who saves an asthmatic gasps for an hour afterwards. They live with the ghosts of every breath they've given away.

### Mutation D: Storm

The war-path of wind, taken to catastrophic scale. A storm-mage manipulates not just local air but pressure systems — creating low-pressure zones that suck in surrounding air, condensing moisture into clouds, spinning the inflow into a vortex. They are walking weather events.

**Debt:** Enormous and environmental. A storm-mage exhausts the local atmosphere, leaving behind dead, still air that can take days to recover. Using this mutation indoors is suicidal, as the pressure differentials collapse buildings. The mage themselves suffers barotrauma — burst capillaries, joint pain, a body battered by its own weather.

## 4. EARTH

**Domain:** Structure and persistence. The solid, the mineral, the slow architecture of the world.

**Internal reservoir:** The minerals in bone and blood, the latent bio-potential in one's own microbiome, the stubborn persistence of life itself.

**Environmental sources:** Soil, stone, deep-root systems, the slow seismic hum of the planet's mantle.

**Debt signature:** The ground grows brittle; nearby plants sicken and grey; the mage's own bones ache with a mineral-deep weariness.

### Base State: Stone/Soil Manipulation

Borrowing and shaping earth, rock, and mineral. Standard combat geomancy — shifting ground, stone projectiles, opening fissures, raising walls.

### Body Sense Application

Tremor-sense through the soles of the feet, feeling footsteps miles away. By listening to root-fibres and the stress-language of growing things, the mage senses the precise location and weight of every moving body touching the earth — even a bird landing on a branch registers.

### Kinetic Amplifier

Stone-flesh integration. Draws mineral density into skin and bones, turning a fist into a granite hammer, or rooting themselves so deeply that no charge can knock them down. They move with tectonic deliberation — slow, but irresistible.

**Debt:** The borrowed density must be returned; afterwards, the mage's bones are briefly brittle, vulnerable to fracture.

### Affliction

Leaching petrification. Steals the calcium from an enemy's bones, leaving them soft and easily broken, or saps their iron until they're too exhausted to lift a sword. An indirect variant uses spore-breath and plant-pheromones to induce deep, narcoleptic drowsiness or disorienting hallucinations drawn from the earth's oldest chemistries.

### Mutation A: Metal

Earth refined. The mage learns to sense and manipulate specifically metallic earth — ores, refined iron, the trace elements in blood. A metal-mage can buckle a sword mid-swing, snap an iron gate, or draw trace iron from the soil into a crude, jagged blade. The most directly martial earth-path.

**Debt:** Paid in the mage's own mineral depletion — chronic anaemia, brittle bones, a grey pallor. They leach themselves of iron to give it to their working.

### Mutation B: Flesh

The unsettling discovery that "earth" includes the mineral scaffolding of living creatures — calcium in bone, iron in blood, salt in nerves. A flesh-mage applies earth-senses inward, to the body's solid architecture. They can accelerate bone-knitting, harden their own skin with temporary calcification, or — in darker applications — cause an enemy's skeleton to soften, their teeth to loosen, their fingernails to slough off.

**Debt:** Paid in the mage's own structural integrity. Flesh-mages suffer early arthritis, crumbling joints, a body that ages faster than the mind. This path is heavily regulated, bordering on blood-magic territory.

### Mutation C: Root

The green path. An earth-mage who learns that soil is not dead — it is a living matrix of roots, mycelium, bacteria, and slow conversation. A root-mage can accelerate plant growth by borrowing nutrients from deeper earth, communicate across forests through fungal networks, or command vines and roots as slow but relentless combatants. They are the mages most likely to become hermits, as their power requires deep, long-term attunement to a specific place.

**Debt:** Paid in rootedness. A root-mage who overuses their power becomes physically unable to leave their territory, suffering wasting illness if they travel too far from the soil they've bonded with.

### Mutation D: Bone

The death-path of earth. A bone-mage senses and manipulates the mineral structures that persist after death — not just bones, but fossilised wood, petrified organisms, the calcium carbonate of seashells and coral. They animate the physical remains of the dead as silent, mindless constructs of mineral and memory. Universally feared, but culturally variable — some societies revere bone-mages as honoured ancestors who let the dead protect the living.

**Debt:** The worst of any earth-path. The mage's own skeleton begins to listen, developing a slow, mineral sentience. Old bone-mages hear their own bones whispering. They say it's the voice of the earth itself, and that it has no love for the living.

## 5. LIGHTNING

**Domain:** Signal and impulse. The spark of thought, the flash of reaction, the hidden architecture of attraction and repulsion.

**Internal reservoir:** The body's own bioelectricity — the microvolt signals of nerve and muscle.

**Environmental sources:** Atmospheric charge, static from friction, ionisation during storms, the piezoelectric hum of strained rock.

**Debt signature:** Local dead-zones of charge; the mage suffers creeping neural fog, phantom numbness, or a temporary stutter as their own synapses repay the borrowed spark.

### Base State: Arc Manipulation

Borrowing and discharging electrical potential. Standard combat fulmancy — bolts, arcs, chain-lightning, disruptive touch.

### Body Sense Application

Electroreception. Perceives the living as crackling webs of neural firing — every twitch of a muscle, every tell-tale pulse before an attack, is an electrical flash read like a whispered warning. Ambushes are impossible against a lightning-mage.

### Kinetic Amplifier

Overclocking their own nerves for bursts of impossible speed and reflex, moving before conscious thought can catch up.

**Debt:** The aftermath is paid in nerve-pain, tremors, or a brief period of partial paralysis. Each overclock borrows from the mage's future mobility.

### Affliction

Neural disruption. A touch sends convulsing false signals, causing uncontrolled muscle spasms, a dropped weapon, or a heart stuttering in arrhythmia. A more subtle application deadens the enemy's peripheral nerves, leaving them clumsy and numbed without ever feeling a shock.

### Mutation A: Nerve

The healer-path of lightning. A nerve-mage reads and modulates the body's own bioelectricity — the microvolt signals that carry sensation, command, and memory. They can restart a stopped heart, restore sensation to paralysed limbs, or — as a dark mirror — override an enemy's motor control for a split second, turning a sword-thrust into a stumble.

**Debt:** Neurological. Every nerve-working causes a brief, corresponding misfire in the mage's own system — a twitch, a phantom pain, a moment of lost motor control. Nerve-mages live in bodies that are never entirely their own.

### Mutation B: Magnet

The discovery that moving charge creates fields, and fields create attraction and repulsion. A magnet-mage can pull metal objects, repulse iron projectiles, or — at high mastery — levitate themselves by pushing against a sufficiently large metal mass. The only flight-path in the five gates.

**Debt:** Paid in disorientation. The mage's inner ear, which relies on gravity, rebels against the constant reorientation. Chronic vertigo, nausea, and a permanent sense of "falling sideways" haunt old magnet-mages. They walk with canes, not for their legs, but for their balance.

### Mutation C: Pulse

The communication path. A pulse-mage encodes information in electrical discharge — becoming a living radio. They can send voice and image across vast distances to other pulse-mages, or imprint a message onto any conductive surface for later retrieval. Invaluable to empires and armies; pulse-mages are often kept as strategic assets rather than battlefield combatants.

**Debt:** Paid in brain-noise. The constant background hum of the world's electrical fields slowly erodes the mage's own thoughts. Old pulse-mages develop aphasia, seizures, and a strange, serene detachment as their inner monologue is gradually replaced by static.

### Mutation D: Static

The patient path. A static-mage accumulates charge without discharging it — holding potential in their body like a coiled spring for hours or days, releasing it in a single, catastrophic instant. They are the trap-setters, the mages who walk into a room and make everyone's hair stand on end.

**Debt:** Paid in continuous, low-grade discharge into the mage's own nervous system — chronic pain, insomnia, and a permanent, visible trembling. Old static-mages are twitchy wrecks who can level a building with a touch but can no longer hold a cup without shattering it.

# BOOK FOUR: THE MUTATION PRINCIPLE

All mutations share a common logic:

1. **No new gate opens.** A water-mage never touches fire. They discover that water, understood deeply enough, contains ice and healing and blood and poison — all expressions of the same fundamental substance, differentiated only by state, purity, and application.

2. **Mutation requires mastery.** Mutation is not the beginner's path. The base state must be understood before its refinements can be accessed. Ice before water is impossible; it is the deepening, not the starting point.

3. **Each mutation has its own debt-profile.** The deeper the mutation, the stranger and more personal the debt becomes. Base water-magic costs hydration. Healing costs sympathetic injury. Blood-magic costs humanity. The price escalates with the power.

4. **Mutations hybridise within the same gate.** A master water-mage might use ice for offense, healing for allies, and a touch of blood when desperate — all from the same gate, each debt adding to the others. This is what makes archmages so dangerous and so visibly damaged. They carry the scars of every deepening they've undertaken.

5. **Unconventional applications exist for every gate.** Body sense, kinetic amplification, and affliction techniques are not separate mutations but *skills* — alternative ways of applying the base state that every trained mage of sufficient creativity can access. They are the mark of a mage who has moved beyond simple force and begun to understand the subtler dimensions of their element.

# BOOK FIVE: UNIVERSAL RULES

## The Debt Cannot Be Cheated

Every working incurs a debt. That debt must be paid, by someone, somewhere. Attempts to evade payment result in the debt accumulating interest — worsening, compounding, until it is called due all at once. History records mages who tried to cheat the economy. Their fates are instructional.

## The Debt Cannot Be Transferred Indefinitely

A mage can make the enemy pay. A mage can make the environment pay. A mage can pre-pay into an object. But the debt always settles. It is not a loan; it is a law.

## The Mage Is a Conduit, Not a Source

The most feared mage is not the one with the deepest internal reservoir. It is the one who has learned to make you carry the debt while they walk away clean — or appear to. Such mages are rare. Most are eventually consumed by the accumulated moral and metaphysical interest of their choices.

## The Body Remembers

All mages bear physical markers of their gate and their mutations. These are not merely cosmetic. They are the ledger written in flesh — the permanent record of every debt incurred and every deepening undertaken. A trained eye can read a mage's history in the tremor of their hands, the frost-scars on their fingers, the grey of their skin, the distant quality of their gaze.

This reading is a learned skill, not a sensitivity. Any sufficiently trained observer — a veteran combat mage, a specialised physician, a Mage-Auditor — can read the gross markers. But the Savants (see Book Two, The Insensitive) read with perfect, instinctive precision. Where a trained physician sees "frost damage," a Savant sees "ice-mutation, third deepening, unpaid debt accumulated across eleven years." Their testimony is definitive in magical law. Mages who wish to hide their history fear Savants above all others.

## Guarding Against Affliction

A mage can actively guard their internal reservoir against theft. This is not a separate spell but a conscious partitioning of attention: the mage "holds" their internal element, refusing to let it be borrowed without consent.

- **The Cost:** Guarding degrades offensive casting. A mage who guards fully cannot cast at all — they are a fortress, but a passive one. A mage who splits attention between guarding and casting suffers diminished power and precision in both. A mage who casts without guarding is fully potent but fully exposed.

- **The Dynamic:** Combat between mages thus becomes a calculus of risk. Guard too heavily, and you concede the initiative. Guard too lightly, and your enemy steals your body heat before you finish your first working. The most lethal duellists are those who read their opponent's rhythm and drop their guard only in the instant of their own strike — a window of vulnerability so brief only years of training can exploit it.

- **The Exception:** The desperate and the untrained cannot guard. They do not know how. This is why Affliction is so devastating against civilians, and why untrained sensitives are such easy victims for predatory mages.
