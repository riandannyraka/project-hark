# Chapter 3: The File

The branch was quiet.

The handlers had gone home. The clerks had gone home. The children — the ones who'd been assessed, the ones who'd been processed, the ones who'd been taken to the dormitories or the training halls or the place under the stairs — were somewhere else. Sleeping, maybe. Or trying to.

Alex sat at her desk. The window was dark now. The harbour was a scattering of lights on black water. The Rubbert yards were invisible — no dry-docks at night, no skeletons of half-built vessels, just the absence where they should have been. She'd lit a candle. The flame was steady. The wax was dripping onto the saucer she'd placed beneath it. She'd learned to do that after the first time — hot wax on the desk, a stain that wouldn't come out, a clerk's raised eyebrow. She'd been twenty-two. She'd still believed that stains were the worst thing that could happen in this office.

The file was on her desk.

House Corway. The heron sigil on the cover — the same bird, the same long neck, the same flight toward something she couldn't see. She'd pulled it from the branch archives an hour ago, after the clerks had left, after the handlers had locked the main door, after the building had settled into its night-time quiet. The archives were in the basement, one floor above the Silk Wards. She'd walked past the door behind the kitchens without looking at it. She always walked past it without looking at it.

She opened the file.

Troy Corway. Third son. Age thirty-two. No magical ability — confirmed Insensitive, tested at age six by a Savant whose name she didn't recognize. No debt-signature. No reservoir. No Gate. The page was a catalogue of absences.

She read further. Red Coin School, trained under Master Sera Kein. That stopped her. Red Coin was the duelist's path — the Single Exchange, the philosophy that any fight should end in one strike. She'd read about it in the Order's supplementary files, the ones that catalogued the Edge Schools and their notable practitioners. A Red Coin fighter trained for thousands of repetitions of a single motion until the motion was no longer a decision. The sword simply arrived. The duel was over. The practitioner walked away.

The file noted his proficiency. *Single Exchange timed at under two-tenths of a second from neutral.* She read the number twice. Two-tenths of a second. The time it took to blink. The time it took to make a choice and unmake it. He'd been standing outside the Factorage with a sword on his back and a performance on his face, and the whole time he could have drawn and struck and ended something before she'd registered the movement.

He hadn't. He'd performed instead. The arena commentary. The spread hands. The smile that didn't reach his eyes.

No criminal record. No Order contracts. No known affiliations with unmarked mages or fugitive networks. The file was thin — thinner than it should have been for someone flagged. A minor noble, a third son, a duelist who'd trained under a master and then vanished into the margins of the League's port cities. The kind of person the Order tracked but didn't pursue. Not important enough.

Except for the note.

She found it in the margin of the third page, written in a hand she didn't recognize — smaller than the clerk's, more precise, the letters angled backward like someone who'd learned to write in a hurry and never unlearned it. *Possible exposure to precursor artifacts. Recommend observation.*

The flame flickered. The wax dripped. The saucer caught it.

She knew what precursor artifacts were. Every Savant knew. The Order's most closely guarded category — objects that predated the Cradle, objects that predated the current civilization, objects that did not officially exist. Debt-nulls that broke the First Principle. Resonant bones that amplified spell effect without increasing debt. Gate-keys that allowed working outside one's Gate. The Still Children — seven petrified corpses that Savants read and saw nothing, not a blank, but an *absence*. The files said they were stored separately. The files said no Curator would discuss them.

House Corway had been exposed. Possibly preserved. The word *observation* was carefully chosen — not *detainment*, not *intervention*, not *sanction*. Someone in the Order had decided that the Corways were worth watching but not worth moving against. Someone had filed the note and closed the file and waited.

She should report this.

The protocol was clear. Any confirmed contact with precursor artifacts required immediate notification of a Curator. The Silencers would be dispatched. The questioning would follow — not an assessment, not the gentle handling of a frightened child, but the other kind. The kind the Order reserved for threats it didn't understand. The vault would open. The artifacts would be confiscated, if they hadn't been already. The Corway estate would be searched. The family would be detained. The third son — the Insensitive duelist with the heron on his collar and the performance on his face — would be brought in.

The children under the stairs. The girl with frost-scarred hands who'd said *I'm used to it.* The corridor of identical doors. The dull grey of containment. The sick yellow of Affliction worked on children. The particular wrongness of debt that was never meant to exist.

She stopped the thought. She'd gotten very good at stopping the thought. The file was still open. Her pen was still in her hand. The candle was still burning.

She set the pen down.

---

The letter was on her desk. She didn't remember pulling out the paper. She didn't remember reaching for the pen. It was simply there — the same way the file was there, the same way the candle was there, the same way the kettle in her room was copper and the window faced the harbour and the east door was unguarded after the fourth bell.

*Mother —*

The word sat at the top of the page. Her handwriting was steady. It was always steady. The letters were even, the spacing precise, the slant exactly what her tutor had taught her when she was eight years old and still allowed to believe that handwriting mattered.

*The weather has turned. The harbour is grey most days. The fishing boats go out at dawn and come back at dusk. I can see the yards from my window — not the dry-docks themselves, but the cranes. The cranes are visible above the rooftops. They look like the skeletons of something that hasn't been built yet.*

She stopped. Read the words back. The cranes as skeletons. She'd written that without meaning to — the architecture metaphor bleeding into places it didn't belong. She didn't cross it out. Her mother would read it and understand something Alex hadn't said. Her mother had always understood the things Alex hadn't said.

*The work is the same. Children come. I assess them. The forms are filed. The days are very similar. I used to think that was a comfort. The sameness. The routine. The knowledge that tomorrow would be very much like today.*

*I'm not sure I think that anymore.*

The words were too close. She could feel it — the thing underneath, pressing up against the paper, asking to be written. If she wrote it, she would have to act on it. The letter was a door she wasn't ready to open.

She folded it instead. Twice. The way her mother folded letters — a precise crease, the edges aligned, the paper flat against the desk. If she didn't seal it, she hadn't decided. If she hadn't decided, she didn't have to act.

She left it on the corner of the desk, beside the candle, beside the file with the heron sigil on the cover.

The shipyards were still there. The light was the same — grey, always grey, the colour of the harbour and the sky and her mother's eyes. She thought about the dry-docks at dawn. The skeletons of half-built vessels against a pale sky. Her mother walking the yard floor, her boots loud on the wooden planks, her voice carrying across the water. Before the Order. Before the carriage. Before the first child's hands in hers.

She missed the light on the water. She'd never told anyone that. She'd written it in a letter she wouldn't send, and that would have to be enough.

---

She closed the file.

The heron sigil stared up at her — the bird in flight, the long neck curved toward something she couldn't see. She'd read everything. She'd read it twice. The third son, the Insensitive, the Red Coin duelist, the flagged note in the margin. The file was thin, but the weight of it was not. The weight of it was the vault beneath the Cradle. The weight of it was the Still Children and the debt-nulls and the resonant bones and the things the Order had buried and denied. The weight of it was a man with grey eyes who stood like he'd been in rooms he wasn't supposed to be in, who knew about the east door, who'd looked at her hands and said *you've been holding something heavy for a long time.*

She should report him. The protocol was clear. The Curator's office was one floor up. The message would take minutes to send. The Silencers would arrive before dawn.

She didn't.

She locked the drawer. The key was small — brass, worn smooth from use, the teeth leaving faint scratches on the lock plate. She'd locked this drawer a hundred times. She'd locked it on assessment forms and debt-records and the names of children she was trying to forget. She'd never locked it on something like this.

The omission was a choice. She'd made it. The paper was blank. The message was unsent. The Curator's office was dark. The ledger would not note the gap — there was no ledger for the things Savants chose not to see. She would carry it instead. Another stone. Another crack in the foundations.

The candle burned. The wax dripped. The saucer caught it.

The file was in the drawer. The heron was on the cover. The branch was quiet.

She was not.

She sat at her desk. The window was dark. The harbour was a scattering of lights. The Rubbert yards were invisible. The kettle in her room was copper. The east door was unguarded after the fourth bell. The letter to her mother was unsealed on the corner of the desk, the words still drying, the decision still unmade.

The door was grey. The walls were grey. The night was grey.

She didn't sleep. She sat with the file locked in the drawer and the letter unsealed on the desk and the weight in her chest pressing against her ribs with every breath. The omission was a choice. She'd made it. She'd make it again.

The heron was still on the cover. The bird was still in flight. The neck was still curved toward something she couldn't see.

She was beginning to see it.
