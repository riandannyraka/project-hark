# Chapter 1: The Door Was Grey

The child's hands were small. The silk had left no marks. Someone had been careful.

Alex took the child's wrists and turned them over. The debt-signature flared before she could look away—pale grey, faintly luminous, the colour of a winter sky before snow. Wind Gate. First-deepening, barely. No frost-scars. No Affliction residue. Just the natural leakage of an unguarded reservoir, the soft grey bleed of a child who'd never been taught to close the door. The child was frightened but undamaged.

She released the hands. The child didn't move. Dark eyes. Too still. The stillness of someone who'd learned not to draw attention.

"What's your name?"

"Maren."

The name landed like a stone. Alex's pen stopped. Her mother's name. The same grey eyes, a different face. She started writing again. The form required a name. She wrote it.

"Are you going to put me in the place under the stairs?"

The pen stopped again. The question hung in the air between them—small and quiet and utterly without inflection. The child had asked it the way another child might ask about supper.

"I don't know what you mean," Alex said. Her voice was steady. It was always steady. That was the job.

The child nodded. Didn't believe her. Alex could see it in the set of the small shoulders—the particular tension of someone who'd learned that adults lied. The child was seven, maybe eight. It was hard to tell with the collected ones. Malnutrition did things to growth. So did fear.

The handlers came. Grey tabards. Clean hands. They took the child by the arms—not roughly, not gently, the way you might move a piece of furniture you'd been told to relocate. The child didn't resist. She'd already learned that resisting didn't help.

The door closed. Grey. Like the walls. Like the silk. Like the light through the narrow window.

She filled out the form. Name. Gate. Depth. Debt. Recommendations: full training curriculum. Her handwriting was steady. The letters were even, the spacing precise, the signature at the bottom a flourish she'd practiced until it was automatic. *Alexandria Rubbert, Assessor, Salthaven Branch.* The name meant something, once. House Rubbert of the Maelstrom League — shipbuilders, engineers, nobility. The youngest of five. The second daughter. The spare.

Now it meant a signature on a form. A woman in a converted storage room. A ledger of children's names she tried not to remember.

The place under the stairs. The children knew. They always knew.

She'd found the Silk Wards two and a half years ago. A door behind the kitchens—grey, like all the doors, unremarkable. She'd been looking for a storage room. The manifest said the branch had surplus blankets, and the dormitory was short, and she'd been new enough to believe that solving small problems would make the larger ones bearable.

The door was unlocked. Stairs going down. The air changed as she descended—cooler, damper, carrying a smell she couldn't immediately identify. Mildew. Old silk. Something else. Something that reminded her of the shipyards at low tide, when the water pulled back and left things exposed that were never meant to be seen.

The corridor at the bottom was a line of identical doors. Grey. Narrow. Each one fitted with a bolt on the outside. She'd opened the first door because she hadn't understood yet what she was looking at. The room was small—a cot, a basin, a bucket. A child on the cot. Female. Twelve, maybe thirteen. Her hands were wrapped in grey cloth, and the cloth was stained with something dark, and her eyes when she looked up were the colour of old ice.

"Are you here to read me?"

Alex hadn't answered. She'd been reading already—couldn't stop reading, the debt-signatures screaming at her from every surface. The child's hands were frost-scarred, the white lines visible even through the wrappings. Fire Gate. Third-deepening. The debt was deep and old and wrong, the colours layered like sediment. Affliction residue. Self-debt. Environmental debt. The child had been used as a reservoir for years.

"I'm used to it," the child said. Her voice was flat. The flatness of someone who'd stopped hoping a long time ago.

Alex closed the door. Walked back up the stairs. Closed the door at the top. Stood in the corridor with her back against the grey stone and her hands pressed flat against her thighs.

She hadn't told anyone. She hadn't reported it. The paper was still blank. Two and a half years, and the paper was still blank.

The window was narrow. The light was grey. The harbour was visible through the salt-crusted glass—fishing boats already out, their lanterns smeared orange across the water. The Rubbert yards were just visible in the distance, the dry-docks rising against the pale sky like the skeletons of half-built vessels.

Her mother would be walking the yard floor now. Dawn inspections, the ritual she'd kept since before Alex was born. She'd be standing at the edge of the largest dock, her grey eyes—the same grey as the water, the same grey as the sky, the same grey as Alex's own—tracking the work, noting the gaps, filing the reports. She'd be calm. She was always calm.

Before the Order. Before the guilt. Before Alex had learned that buildings could be beautiful and rotten at the same time.

She thought about writing a letter. She never wrote the letter. The words were there—*I see things here, I know things now, the foundation is cracked*—but they never made it onto the paper. Her mother would read between the lines. Her mother always read between the lines. That was the problem.

She looked out the window. The harbour. The ships. The grey light.

A man was walking toward the Factorage.

She noticed him because he moved wrong. Not wrong—differently. Wiry. Watchful. His gait was economical in a way that reminded her of something—a fighter's walk, no wasted motion, the weight shifting from heel to toe with each step. His left hand was gloved. The right was bare. The glove was old leather, worn soft at the palm, and the way he held the hand slightly away from his body said he was used to carrying something in it. A blade. A hilt.

She couldn't read him. The distance was too great—debt-signatures required proximity, a handspan at most—but something about the way he moved made her look twice. His hair was dark, cut short, the kind of cut that meant he wanted to be unremarkable. His coat was patched at the elbow. His boots were cracked at the heel. He walked like someone who'd been walking for years and didn't expect to stop.

His eyes were grey. She caught them for a half-second as he turned toward the Factorage door—grey like the harbour, grey like the winter sky, grey like her own. Not the grey of debt. Just grey.

Then he was gone. The door closed behind him. The street was empty.

Alex sat at her desk. The form was still there. The child's name—Maren—was still there. The place under the stairs was still there. The paper was still blank.

The door was grey. The walls were grey. The light through the window was grey.

The next child would arrive soon. The carriage was already at the gate. Her hands were cold. They were always cold when a child arrived.

She picked up her pen.
