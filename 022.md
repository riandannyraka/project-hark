# Epilogue: The Tide

The ship cut through the waves, steady and slow, the way ships did when they weren't fleeing anything. Grey water. Grey sky. The Archipelago shrinking behind them—green hills fading to blue, blue fading to nothing. Salthaven was already gone. The ropewalk room with its salt-crusted window and its lock he'd checked twice every night. The Factorage with its iron sign and its walnut shells crushed into the floorboards. The Weeping Dog with its one-armed barkeep who poured ale without being asked.

The cottage was gone. The hearth. The net on its frame. The rain barrel that stirred when Renne walked past. The clearing where a girl with shaking fingers had learned to shape water.

The cave was gone. The blood on the stones. The Savant's clean hands. The Silencers' crossbows left behind.

He was leaving. But he was not running. Running was what you did when something was chasing you. Nothing was chasing him now. The Order thought he was dead. The Savant had filed her report. The Light-mage was a closed entry in a ledger somewhere in the Cradle, and the ledgers didn't lie, and the Savants didn't either. Except when they did. Except when someone proved that the Order's training didn't have to produce monsters.

He wasn't running. He was moving. There was a difference. He was still learning it.

Renne leaned against him. Her shoulder pressed into his arm. Her hands were still—finally still, the way they'd been in the cave, the way they'd been on the path when she'd kissed him and walked into the dark. The restlessness was still there, somewhere, but it was quiet now. She'd spent three days flushing the debt from her lungs. The borrowed damage. The portion of his wound she'd taken into herself. Her breathing was still shallow at night, a thin whistle when she slept. It would pass. It was passing. She said it was worth it.

"Where are we going."

"Don't know. Somewhere without an Order branch."

"That's nowhere."

"Then somewhere with a small one."

She laughed. The sound was small and quickly stifled—like Lea's, but different. Hers. Lea's laugh had been bright and surprised, a candle flame in the dormitory dark. Renne's was lower, rougher, the laugh of someone who'd spent ten years hiding and still found things worth laughing at. He loved her. He didn't say it. The words were there, in his chest, in his throat, behind the door that had been closed for fifteen years. The door was open now. The words would come. When they didn't feel like a trap. When they didn't feel like a contract he couldn't honour. He would say them. He was learning.

The sea was wide and cold and indifferent. It stretched to the horizon in every direction, grey on grey, the surface broken by whitecaps that formed and dissolved without pattern. He had crossed it to reach the Archipelago. A ferryman with one eye and a dog that watched the water. A rumor in his chest like a splinter. A woman with grey eyes and hands that never stopped moving. He was crossing it now to leave, and the water didn't care. The water didn't remember. The water just moved.

He was learning to be like the water. Not cold—he'd been cold for fifteen years, and the cold was still there, still writing its slow white lines across his hands. But moving. Not still, but steady. Not forgetting, but carrying. The debt was not settled. It would never be settled. Lea was still dead. The carriage had still come. The handler's hands had still never moved. The instructor's voice still said *again* in the back of his mind, flat and patient, the voice of someone who'd said the same words to a hundred children and would say them to a hundred more.

But the interest had stopped compounding. He was not adding to the debt. He was not running from it. He was carrying it. Carrying it was different. Carrying it was something you could do with someone else beside you.

That was enough for today.

A woman approached along the rail. She was middle-aged, weathered, her hands callused from rope and salt. Former sailor. Maybe still a sailor. Her eyes swept the deck once—the exits, the passengers, the stairs to the hold—and then settled on him. Hark's hand went to his dagger. Old habit. The geometry of leaving, mapped fresh. He didn't draw. He waited.

"The tide is high at the Sign of the Broken Keel."

The words landed. Old words. Words he hadn't heard in years. Kaelen's words—the man in the safe house, the one who'd patched his ribs and asked no questions, the one who'd said the League had eyes everywhere and doors for people who needed them.

"I don't need shelter."

The woman shrugged. "It's not shelter. It's an option." She didn't wait for an answer. She turned and walked back along the rail, her boots steady on the deck, her hands in her pockets. The wind caught her coat. She didn't look back.

Renne watched her go. "What was that."

"A door. Don't know if I want to open it."

The League. The hidden mages. The network of safe houses and pulse-relay stations and people who'd decided that the Order's monopoly wasn't unbreakable. He'd spent three months running from the rumor. He'd found a cell and scattered it. He'd lit a beacon and fought a Savant and walked away. The League had been watching. The League was always watching.

"You don't have to decide today."

"I know."

He didn't decide. He stood at the rail and watched the water and let the door stay closed. It would still be there tomorrow. The League would still be there. The option would still be there. He didn't have to open it. He didn't have to run toward it. He could just stand here, with Renne's shoulder against his arm, and breathe.

He took her hand. His glove was back on. The leather was stiff with old blood and new stitches—he'd repaired it on the ferry, twelve more stitches, waxed cord, the same pattern he'd learned from his mother in a kitchen he'd never see again. The scars were hidden. The ledger was covered. The Savant had read it. Renne had read it. He knew it was there. She knew it was there.

That was the difference. He was not hiding from her. He was hiding from the world. And maybe, someday, he wouldn't need to hide at all. The thought sat in his chest like a splinter. Like a door. Like a small green thing growing in the dark.

He thought of Joel.

The old man's voice, gravel and phlegm, coughing blood into a rag. The old man's sword on his back—plain steel, no name, no history, the edge still sharp after everything. The old man's coin in his pocket. The old man's jokes, dark and worn and somehow still funny. *Body's a bad contract. Termination clause came early.* The old man who'd found a mute boy in a forest camp and given him a name.

*Hark. It means listen. You don't talk, but you hear everything. So that's you. Hark.*

He'd listened. He'd learned. He'd counted coin twice and checked the lock twice and mapped the exits every morning. He'd survived. He'd not been caught.

But he'd done more than survive. He'd sat by a hearth and eaten stew he could almost taste. He'd watched a woman shape water into silver vortices and said *try* and reached out his hand. He'd been kissed, quick and hard and tasting of salt. He'd held someone's hand in the dark and not pulled away.

He'd lived. Just a little. Just enough.

Joel would be proud, probably. Joel had said *survive long enough* and he'd survived. Joel had said *never get caught again* and he'd not been caught. Joel had said *you're planning to live* and he was planning. He was still planning. The plan was small and fragile and his alone. The plan was a cottage somewhere, and a hearth, and Renne's hands shaping water in the morning light. The plan was stopping. The plan was staying.

Lea would be proud, too. Lea had wanted to go back to the river, to watch the wind make the water shiver. She'd never made it. But he'd carried her with him—through the forest, through the Confederacy, through the ropewalk room and the Factorage and the Weeping Dog. He'd carried her to the islands and back. He'd carried her through the cave and out the other side. He would keep carrying her. The weight was not gone. The weight would never be gone. But it was lighter now. It was something he could hold without drowning.

The ship sailed on. The wind was cold. The water was grey. The Archipelago was gone, and the mainland was ahead, and the world was wide and indifferent and full of doors he hadn't opened yet.

He stood at the rail, the wind in his face, Renne's hand in his. He was still moving. But he was moving toward something now.

That was the difference. That was everything.
