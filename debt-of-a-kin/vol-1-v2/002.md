# Chapter 2 – The Scalpel

The mage was younger than her. That was the first thing she noticed.

Alexandria stood at the edge of the assessment chamber, her back to the curved stone wall, her hands clasped behind her in the posture of a trainee awaiting instruction. Five other Silencer trainees flanked her, their grey uniforms identical, their faces identically blank. This was part of their education: witness a live debt-reading, understand what they were being trained to hunt.

The chamber was circular, the stone floor sloped toward a central drain. The air was cold and carried the faint iron smell of old workings. A single glow-stone in the ceiling cast pale light across the scene.

The captured mage was a boy, perhaps thirteen. Fire Gate, unregistered. His hands were bound in damp cloth—a crude suppression method, but effective on low-level debt. His face was bruised. A purple swelling closed one eye nearly shut. He had been caught in the Grain-Lords' Confederacy after accidentally burning a barn. No one had died. He was still here.

A Savant approached him. Not Orin—an older woman with grey hair pulled tight against her skull and fingers that looked cold even from across the room. She took the boy's bound hands, turned them over, read the debt written in his skin. The boy flinched. The Savant's voice was flat, carrying easily in the stone chamber.

"Fire Gate. Grey debt, deepening toward Amber. Uncontrolled reservoir. Two accidental ignitions in the past month. No guarding training. Recommend leashing for stabilization."

Alexandria watched the boy's hands. They were shaking. The damp cloth had slipped, and she could see the raw skin beneath where he had struggled against the bindings. Her jaw remained loose. Her breathing even.

Subject is non-compliant. Threat level low but unpredictable.

The senior Silencer stepped forward. A woman Alexandria had trained under—cold-eyed, efficient, her grey sleeves rolled to the elbow. She carried a collar of leather and iron, runes etched into the inner surface, their edges catching the glow-stone's light.

The boy saw the collar. He began to struggle.

The Silencer held him still. Not cruelly—just firmly. A grip on the back of the neck. A knee in the spine. The boy's struggles collapsed into trembling. The collar snapped closed around his throat.

The runes pulsed once. Amber. Then went dark.

The boy screamed.

It was not a sound of pain. It was a sound of loss—a Gate slamming shut, a reservoir locked behind a door he could no longer open. Alexandria knew the theory. Leashing forced a mage's internal reservoir into permanent guard state. They could not cast. They could not be cast upon. They were rendered Insensitive by force, their magic walled off behind iron and ink.

The boy stopped screaming. His breathing steadied. The collar's runes pulsed in time with his heartbeat, a slow amber rhythm. He was compliant now. He would be placed with a handler—an Edge practitioner, perhaps, or an Order auxiliary—and used as a tool until his debt stabilized or he died.

Threat neutralized. Protocol followed. Leashing is preferable to termination.

The trainees filed out. Alexandria lingered.

She watched the boy being led away through the west corridor—toward the Silk Wards, where leashed mages were quartered. His shoulders were slumped. The collar pulsed. She watched the Savant exit through the east door, toward the archives. She watched her own exit—south, toward the training hall. The room was not yet clear.

"You're still here."

Vellen's voice behind her. She did not startle. She turned.

"Observing."

"The assessment is over."

"The room isn't clear."

Vellen's mouth tightened—not disapproval. Assessment. The instructor's eyes moved over her face, her posture, the angle of her head.

"Training hall. Ten minutes. Don't be late."

---

The dummy had no hands. That was the problem.

Alexandria circled it in the grey morning light, knife loose in her grip, the blade angled flat along her forearm. The training hall was cold stone and colder air, the kind of cold that made the body slow if you let it. She didn't let it.

Exits. The main door stood ajar to her left, the corridor beyond empty. Two high windows on the east wall, rectangles of pale sky, a cross-breeze carrying the smell of oil and old sweat. Both windows too narrow for a body. Sightlines clear. Catalogued and dismissed.

The dummy hung from its post, scarred wood gleaming where a thousand strikes had polished the grain. Arms outstretched. No hands. Some previous trainee had sheared them off with a bad cut, and the instructors hadn't replaced them. Without hands, there was no weapon to read, no angle of attack to anticipate. Threat geometry degraded.

She stepped in. Hesitated half a beat, reading the empty wrists, recalculating. Her blade hung in the air.

"You think too much."

Vellen's voice from the doorway. Flat, carrying. The instructor stood with arms crossed, a block of old muscle in grey robes.

"The geometry won't save you when a mage has already started casting."

The geometry was the only thing that would. She knew it and did not say it. She reset her stance, weight on the balls of her feet, and struck. The knife entered the dummy's throat in a single clean motion. No wasted movement. The wood thudded and rocked on its post. She stepped back, breathing steady. Pulse unchanged.

Strike landed. Next.

Vellen made a sound that wasn't approval. "You're assigned to a new partner. Savant Orin. Report to briefing room three."

---

The old man entered the hall as if summoned by his name. Alexandria's attention shifted without moving her head.

Exits. Not relevant. He was not a threat.

Hands. Empty, loose at his sides. Not reaching. The skin was thin over the knuckles, mapped with veins, the fingers slightly crooked. Steady. No tremor. She watched them for a fraction longer than necessary and didn't know why.

Weapons. None visible. The grey Savant robes could conceal a small blade; she estimated probability and dismissed it.

Face. Pale eyes set in a face that had forgotten how to express surprise. He looked at her as if reading a page he'd already memorised. She looked away as soon as the data was collected. No lingering.

Vellen nodded at the old man. "She's ready."

Orin didn't acknowledge the instructor. He spoke to Alexandria. "You're the scalpel. I'm the map. Don't cut until I tell you."

A weapon analogy. She understood it. A scalpel was precise, single-purpose, clean. She gave a short nod.

Orin led her to a small Savant chamber. She mapped it on entry: one door, no windows, one table, two chairs. Tight geometry. Poor sightlines if the door was breached. She took the chair with her back to the wall, the door in peripheral vision. Orin sat opposite. Not a threat. Alert level downgraded.

He placed a file on the table. The cover bore an Amber seal, the wax still glossy under the thin light of a single glow-stone set in the ceiling. Amber debt. Chronic. A caster who had been paying for years in body and function.

"The Seed," Orin said. "A healer. Unregistered. Operating in the Grain-Lords' Confederacy. She's been moving Sensitive children out of Order reach for at least five years. Capture or terminate. Preference for capture."

Alexandria opened the file. The paper was old, the ink fading at the edges. She scanned with tactical eyes, discarding narrative, extracting data. Known associates. Last confirmed location—a way station two days east of the Grain-Lords' capital. Magical signature classified as Healing mutation, Water Gate.

Amber debt. Chronic pain in the hands. A healer who'd healed too much. Joints would be compromised. Grip strength reduced. Vulnerable to joint locks.

"Why a Silencer? Healing mutation is non-combative."

"She is protected. Former mercenaries. A few defectors. We expect resistance."

Alexandria returned her eyes to the file. The next page was a Savant's ledger, cramped handwriting in columns—dates, locations, reported sightings. She turned the page.

Her eyes caught on a notation near the bottom.

*Possible connection to an abandoned childbirth at the Cradle foundling house. Records incomplete. Infant presumed deceased or absorbed into foundling stock.*

The words didn't move. The rest of the room did.

Her mind stuttered. The phrase surfaced without permission, the clinical designation she'd constructed years ago to fill the space where something else belonged. *The woman who gave birth.* No face. No name. A biological event. A closed file.

Her knuckles whitened on the edge of the table.

She released the pressure immediately. Her hands returned to her lap. She looked up, mapped the room again—exits, sightlines, the door still closed, the glow-stone humming faintly. Threat assessment: none. Data acquired. Alert level nominal.

She turned the page. The notation was behind her now.

She did not ask about it. Irrelevant. Filed.

Orin's hands rested on the file, fingers interlaced. She watched them for another half-second. Still steady. No tremor.

"When do we leave?"

"Dawn. Grain-Lords' Confederacy. Report to the training hall at first light. Pack for wet ground."

---

The Silencers' armoury was empty when she returned. She took her knife kit from the bench and sat with her back to the wall. The oilstone was worn smooth in the centre from use. She laid out the blades—primary knife, secondary, a thin boot-knife she never wore inside the Cradle but maintained regardless.

Oil on the stone. Blade to the surface. One stroke. Two. Three. The rhythm was a quiet thing, metal on grit, steady as a pulse. She counted strokes. Twenty per side. Then the strop. Then the edge check, thumb hovering a hair's-breadth from the steel. Her body knew the ritual. Her mind went quiet. Not peaceful—quiet. The difference mattered.

She cleaned the blades, sheathed them, and sat for a moment in the grey silence. The leashing replayed behind her eyes—the boy's hands shaking, the collar pulsing, the Savant's cold voice. Successful protocol. She did not catalogue the weight in her chest.

The morning was gone. The day had moved around her without her noticing.

---

Evening. The mess hall was a long low room of stone tables and iron benches, lit by tallow candles that guttered in the draft from the main doors. Alexandria entered and didn't sit.

She scanned. Exits. Main doors east, service door west past the kitchens. Both clear. Sightlines. The room was half-full, trainees in grey clustered at three tables, a scattering of older Order personnel near the hearth. The noise was low, voices layered over the clink of bowls and spoons.

Mess hall's quiet tonight.

The quiet was just quiet. The empty benches were just empty. The laughter from the far table was just a sound. She collected a bowl of broth and a heel of dark bread and sat at a bench near the east exit. Optimal position. Clear path to the door. No disadvantage.

She ate without tasting. The broth was warm.

The other trainees left in groups, their voices trailing into the corridor. The candles burned lower. A kitchen hand came and cleared the bowls from the empty tables. Alexandria finished her bread, stood, and walked out without a backwards glance.

---

The dormitory was dark. Rows of cots stretched the length of the room, each with a grey blanket and a flat pillow, each identical. Hers was the third from the south wall. She lay on her back on the thin mattress and stared at the ceiling beams, grey on grey in the darkness.

She replayed the mission parameters. Target: the Seed. Healing mutation, Water Gate. Amber debt. Chronic hand pain. Vulnerable to joint locks. Likely protected. Grain-Lords' Confederacy. Wet ground. Pack accordingly.

Dawn departure with Savant Orin. His hands had been steady.

She did not think about the notation in the file. She did not think about the woman who gave birth. She did not think about the boy with the collar, his hands shaking, his voice breaking on a scream that was not pain.

She closed her eyes and listened to the breathing of the other trainees, the rustle of blankets, the distant tread of a night patrol in the corridor. The sounds were data. The dark was just dark.

Mess hall's quiet tonight.
