# Chapter 4 – The Healer

The morning clinic was for broken bones and lies.

The farmer sat on the stool by the kitchen table, his right arm cradled in his left, the shoulder sitting wrong under his tunic. Calloused hands. Dirt crusted in the knuckles, under the nails, ground in deep from a life of seed and soil. Renne took the wrist. Warm skin. No magic-scar, no debt-line threading the palm. A mundane injury. That was almost a relief.

She found the joint with her fingers, the ball of the humerus slipped forward out of the socket. The farmer grunted, a low animal sound. She didn't warn him. Warning made people brace, and bracing made the reduction harder. She pulled, rotated, and the bone slid home with a wet thud.

The farmer exhaled. His shoulder sat where it belonged. Renne let go and stepped back. Her hands were shaking.

It was always the same. The healing debt lived in her knuckles like old weather, a low-pressure ache that never fully cleared. Every reduction, every pulled infection, every life hauled back across the line added its own small weight to the ledger. She pressed her palms flat against her apron and waited for the tremble to pass. The farmer was rolling his shoulder, testing the joint. He hadn't noticed. They never did.

She sent him off with a sling and a warning about heavy loads. He paid in eggs. The basket sat on the table, six brown shells flecked with straw, and she let them sit there while she crossed to the hearth.

Lina was on her stool by the fire, a scrap of paper on her knee and a charcoal stick in her fingers. Twelve years old, mute since the fever that had nearly killed her at seven, the one Renne had pulled her through at the cost of two knuckles that still seized in the cold. Lina didn't look up. She was drawing.

Renne stood behind her and watched the lines take shape. A bird. A broken wing, the angle wrong, the feathers sketched in quick dark strokes. Lina's drawings sometimes came true. Renne didn't say that. She didn't have words for it, only a habit of watching the paper more carefully than she let on.

She touched Lina's hands. The fingers were cold. Too cold for the season, even with the morning chill still sitting in the kitchen corners. The girl's breathing was shallow but even. No rattle in the chest. No fever-heat behind the ears. Renne filed the observation: check pulse before dinner. Cold hands could be nothing. They could be circulation, or growth, or the lingering debt of the fever she'd paid for seven years ago. The body kept its own ledgers.

"We'll splint it," Renne said, nodding at the bird.

Lina didn't smile. She rarely did. But she turned the paper over and started a new drawing, and that was its own kind of answer.

The morning light came through the east window, grey and thin, the colour of early spring in the Grain-Lords' Confederacy. Mud season. The roads would be soft, the wagons slow. A good time for moving children, if moving became necessary. Renne pushed the thought aside and let her gaze catch on the windowsill.

The dried flower sat where it always sat. A star-thistle, brittle and grey-blue, its petals curled inward like a sleeping spider. She had picked it fifteen years ago, on a riverbank she didn't let herself name, with a person she didn't let herself remember. It had lived on windowsills ever since. She didn't touch it. She didn't name the memory attached. She let it exist for the span of three breaths, a small beautiful thing that asked nothing of her, and then she looked away.

A child with a splinter came next. Then a woman with a cough that Renne diagnosed as wet-lung and treated with steam and a poultice, no magic required. The morning filled with small mending. Hands on skin. Questions and answers. The rhythm of it was a refuge she didn't examine.

---

The afternoon brought the secret.

She closed the shutters before she opened the root cellar door. The two children came up blinking, a boy of nine and a girl of seven, their faces smudged with dirt from sleeping below ground. Unregistered. Sensitive. The faint lines on their hands caught the candlelight, pale threads of debt from magic they hadn't meant to do, couldn't control, had never been taught to contain.

Renne sat them at the kitchen table. Lina stayed by the fire, drawing, her presence a quiet constant. The boy watched Renne's hands. The girl watched the door.

"Magic is borrowing," Renne said. Her voice was low, the voice she used for lessons and for calming frightened animals. "Every time you reach for it, you take something from somewhere. Your own body, most often. Sometimes the ground. Sometimes the air. It leaves a mark."

She turned her palms up on the table. The scars ran across them in pale ridges, crossing the knuckles, tracing the life-line, mapping every healing she'd ever done. A ledger written in skin.

"This is what you pay," she said. "You choose when it's worth it."

The girl touched her own hand, the faint debt-lines that threaded from her index finger to her wrist. She was seven. The lines were already there. A fall from a tree, Renne guessed, caught by a gust of wind that had come from inside the child rather than the sky. The body borrowing from itself before the mind knew how to stop it.

"You are seeds," Renne said. The gardening words came easily, the same words she'd used for years, the metaphor worn smooth as river stone. "The Order is frost. It kills what it can't control. We hide you until you're strong enough to choose your own ground. Until you can choose for yourselves."

She didn't say *hope*. The word was a trap. Hope was what got people caught, what made them linger too long, what made them think the hunters wouldn't find them. She had learned that lesson young. She had learned it the night she walked away from the Cradle with empty arms and a word she would never speak again.

"The network still holds," she said instead. "That's not nothing."

The boy nodded. The girl's eyes were on the shutters, the cracks of daylight at the edges. Renne followed the gaze. Exits. The root cellar door behind them, a hatch set into the kitchen floor. The back window, shuttered now, opening onto the yard. Beyond the yard, a stand of trees, poplars and low scrub, thick enough to hide a running child. She mapped the routes without thinking. If boots sounded on the road, the children would go down. Lina would go out the back window and into the trees. Marta's tavern was four miles east, a waypoint. She'd drilled it a dozen times.

Her hands were aching. The pain had spiked while she talked, the chronic burn in her knuckles flaring sharp. Yesterday's healing—a neighbour's infected wound, a woman who would have lost the arm without intervention—was coming due. The debt was fresh, layered on top of the old scars, an ache that sang up the tendons into her wrists. She clasped her hands together on the table, hiding the tremor. The children didn't see. Lina might have, but Lina was drawing.

Pain is a ledger. Pay it now, walk later. The words were Order words, Cradle words, cold as iron. She hated them and they were true.

She sent the children back to the root cellar with blankets and a waterskin and a heel of bread. "Stay until I come." She didn't say *if*. Children heard the silences. They went down without protest, the hatch closing over them with a soft thud of wood on stone.

---

Evening came grey and damp. The shutters stayed closed. Renne lit a candle on the kitchen table and sat with her hands in her lap, the ache a steady companion. Lina was in the loft with her charcoal, a sliver of moonlight through the high window. The house was quiet.

The back door banged open.

Marta came through it like a storm, sixty-four years old and built of sinew and bad temper, a knife already in her hand. She kicked the door shut behind her and stood in the candlelight, breathing hard.

"Team Five," she said. "Quiet Death. They're two days out. Calen and Ellera. A Silencer and a mage-killer."

She didn’t say how she knew. Renne had stopped asking; Marta’s information arrived like the weather, uninvited and rarely wrong.

Renne was already on her feet. The names landed like stones dropped into still water. Calen. Ellera. She knew the names. The network kept records, and Marta kept more than most. Calen was the Silencer, young, precise, rumoured to be a prodigy. Ellera was worse. A mage-killer, trained to hunt casters, to read the debt in a working and strike before the payment came due. They moved as a pair, and they didn't leave survivors.

"The children," she said. "The two in the cellar. They need to move tonight."

"I can take them," Marta said. "Through the woods to my place. From there to the next station. It's not safe, but it's safer than here."

Renne nodded. She was already calculating. The root cellar would hold for a search, but not a thorough one. The children needed to be gone before the hunters arrived. Lina would go with Marta. The farmhouse would be empty, or close to it. She would stay and meet the hunters. A healer with no combat Edge, a Dull-grade non-combatant, couldn't fight a Silencer and a mage-killer. But she could talk. She could delay. She could give the network time to close the next set of doors.

Marta was watching her. The older woman's face was sharp in the candlelight, the lines around her mouth cut deep. "They'll use the children to get to you. You know that."

The pain hit before the thought did.

*The child.*

Not the ones in the cellar. Not Lina. The one she had surrendered. The infant in grey cloth, rain on her face, the weight leaving her arms. The one whose name she had spoken once and never again. The thought came with a spike of pain in her hands, a tightening of the scars as if the old tissue remembered what the mind refused to. Her knuckles whitened. Her fingers curled inward.

She forced the thought down. Her hands were shaking. She sat down at the kitchen table and let them.

Marta didn't speak. She'd seen this before. She pulled a chair to the door and sat with the knife across her lap, her eyes on the dark beyond the shutters.

Renne turned her palms up on the table. The candle flame bent in a draft she couldn't feel, and the light slid across her scars.

Left thumb. A child with fever, years ago, the first life she'd pulled back from the edge. She had been seventeen, new to her power, and the debt had nearly killed her. The scar was a white seam running from the knuckle to the first joint. She touched it and remembered the heat of the child's forehead, the way the fever had broken like a storm passing.

Index finger. A farmer's crushed hand, bones she'd knitted while her own fingers screamed in sympathy. The scar here was raised, a rope of tissue. She pressed it and felt the echo of the old pain, dull now, a ghost.

Middle knuckle. A woman in childbirth, bleeding out, the Water Gate open so wide Renne had thought she'd drown in it. The scar was a starburst, the skin pulled tight. The child had lived. The mother had lived. The debt had taken two months of mobility from Renne's right hand. She'd paid it. She'd pay it again.

Right palm. The burn-scar, wide and glossy, from a mage-hunter's fire. She had been shielding children, the same kind of children who were in the root cellar now, and the fire had found her. The scar ached when she thought about it. She touched it and didn't flinch.

Ring finger, right hand. A young man with the wasting sickness, a healing that had taken everything she had and more. The debt had settled in her lungs, a winter cough that never fully cleared. The scar was thin, almost invisible, but the cough was still there, waiting for cold weather.

Little finger. A girl with a broken spine, the hardest healing she'd ever done. The debt had been joint-deep, and it had stayed. The finger didn't straighten fully anymore. She touched the scar and remembered the girl walking. Walking.

She worked through the rest of them, each scar a name, each name a life. The ritual grounded her. Her breathing slowed. The shaking in her hands eased to a fine tremor and then to stillness. The candle burned. Marta kept watch.

When it was done, she folded her hands in her lap and sat in the quiet. The night pressed against the shutters. Somewhere in the house, a beam settled. Lina's charcoal scratched faintly from the loft.

She rose. She crossed to the windowsill and straightened the dried flower, a small unconscious adjustment, the petals shivering under her touch. Fifteen years. The star-thistle had outlasted everything she'd built and lost and built again. She didn't know what that meant. She didn't try to know.

She lit a second candle and placed it on the table beside the first. The flame doubled, the shadows pushed back a few more inches. Marta sat by the door, her knife a line of silver in the dark. The children were hidden. Lina was drawing. The network still held.

She lit a candle. She waited for the hunters.

An hour before dawn, Marta rose from her chair by the door. She sheathed her knife, pressed Renne's shoulder once—a wordless command to stay—and slipped out into the dark. The tavern needed securing before the message chain could start. Renne watched her go, then turned back to the candle. She would follow at full dark.
