# Chapter 8 – The Crack

The village was still smoking when they arrived. The report said rogue mage. The report was wrong.

Her instructors called her Alexandria. The name on her file, the name in the ledgers, the name Vellen barked across the training floor when she hesitated half a beat too long. She had been Alexandria for fifteen years. But her first blade-instructor—a wiry woman with a missing ear and no patience for syllables—had called her Alex. *Three sounds is two too many when someone's trying to kill you.* The woman was dead now. Killed on a mission Alex had never been told the details of. The name stayed.

She crested the low hill as dusk fell, Orin a half-step behind her, his breathing steady on the incline. Below them, a cluster of farmhouses huddled around a packed-dirt square. One structure had collapsed, its blackened timbers jutting from the rubble like cracked ribs. Smoke drifted in thin ribbons, grey on grey against the darkening sky. No voices. No livestock. The Grain-Lord's tithe-bell hung silent above the square, its rope stirring faintly in the wind.

Exits and sightlines. Open fields in all directions, one road leading east, a treeline to the north. The collapsed farmhouse had created new blind corners in what had been an open square. Hands. A body near the well—a woman, face-down, arms outstretched, hands empty. No weapon. No defensive wounds visible from this distance. Weapons. A pitchfork lay in the mud a few paces from the body. The handle was unmarked. No blade scores. No scorching. It had never been raised. Faces. She didn't look at the woman's face. Not yet.

"The report says a Fire-mage turned on the village," Orin said. His voice was level, the same voice he'd used in the briefing room three days ago, the same voice he'd used when he told her she was a scalpel. "Killed a child before the family subdued him. Local militia finished the job."

"Where's the mage's body?"

"Burned. By the family. Before the Order arrived."

No body meant no debt-reading. No debt-reading meant no verification. Convenient. She walked down the hill without waiting for him.

The collapsed farmhouse had been pulled apart by the villagers. A rough tarp covered the dead—four adult shapes, mud-stained, still. A child's body lay apart from the others, smaller, wrapped in a grain sack. Someone had placed a stone on the sack to keep the wind from lifting it.

Alex knelt. She didn't pray.

She lifted the sack. The child was a boy, perhaps six. The wound was a depression fracture at the temple, circular, about the width of a hammer-head. The bone had caved inward in a pattern that spoke of a single swung object, heavy, wielded with force. Blunt-force cranial fracture. Consistent with a swung object. Not a burn. Not a blast. Fire didn't do this.

She pulled back the tarp on the nearest adult. A man, his arms crossed over his chest where someone had arranged them. Burn marks on the forearms—defensive scorching, the palms charred where he'd raised his hands against the flame. These people had fought a Fire-mage. The man had died trying to shield himself. But the child hadn't died from fire.

She stood. Orin was behind her, silent.

She walked the perimeter. The burn patterns radiated from the farmhouse door—four distinct scorch-points, the wood blackened in starburst patterns that spoke of panic, not control. Base-state Fire. No narrow-beam charring, no surgical cuts in the timber. The mage hadn't reached mutation. Gate only. He had been cornered at the door, lashing out in all directions, flame without focus.

The child's body had been found twenty paces away. Near the well.

She walked the line between the door and the well. Ten paces from the door, the scorch marks stopped. Bare earth. No ash. The mage's fire had never reached the well. The mage had never reached the child.

She found the hammer in the rubble.

It was half-buried under a fallen beam, a household tool with a wooden handle and an iron head. She pulled it free. The handle was smooth from years of use, worn to the shape of someone's grip. The striking face was crusted with blood and hair. No scorching. No heat-warp. The mage had never touched it.

Orin approached. He looked at the hammer, then at the well, then at the child's body beneath the grain sack. He touched the handle with two fingers. His eyes went distant for a moment.

"No debt on it," he said. "This was swung by an Insensitive."

"The child wasn't killed by the mage." She held the hammer out, let him see the blood, the hair, the unmarked handle. "The mage was at the door. The child was at the well. The burn patterns stop ten paces short. The hammer has no scorching. No heat-warp. The mage never touched it."

She set the hammer down on the rubble.

"Someone used the chaos. Settled a score. Blamed the mage."

Orin was quiet. She could feel him behind her, but she didn't turn. She was watching the treeline, the failing light, the geometry of a lie.

"That's a serious accusation."

"It's arithmetic."

She waited. He didn't tell her to drop it. He didn't tell her to file the report as written. He just stood there.

She turned. She looked at his hands.

He had clasped them behind his back, but she caught the right one as he shifted his weight. A tremor. Small, fine, running through the fingers. She had watched his hands for years—during executions, during debt-readings that condemned children to leashing. His hands had never trembled. Not once.

Now they shook.

The feeling rose in her chest. Odds are bad. Exposure too high. This geometry fails.

"We should report this to the local magistrate," Orin said.

"They already know. Someone in this village swung the hammer. The magistrate is probably their cousin."

Orin said nothing. His hands stopped trembling. He clasped them behind his back again, the fingers interlaced, the tremor buried.

The village inn was the only building still lit. A low stone structure at the edge of the square, its windows glowing orange with candlelight. Alex entered first. One door, front. A serving hatch to the kitchen—too narrow for a man in armour, wide enough for a knife. Three windows, shuttered. Five villagers at the bar, farmers by the look of their hands, their voices low and indistinct. A man in Grain-Lord green sat apart from them, not drinking, watching the room with the stillness of someone who was paid to notice things. She took a corner table with her back to the wall.

Orin sat at the table beside hers. He produced paper, ink, the Cradle's seal from a leather pouch at his belt. His hands were steady as he wrote. She watched them move across the page, the quill scratching in the silence. She didn't read what he wrote. She already knew what it said. *Rogue mage. Child casualty. Threat neutralized by local militia. No further action required.*

She unfolded her own report. The parchment was local stock, untreated. No debt-sensitive fibres. No one would know she had kept it. She folded it again, carefully, precisely, along its original creases, and put it in her pocket.

Orin signed his report. The wax dripped red onto the paper, and he pressed the Cradle's sigil into the bead. He handed it to a courier waiting by the door—a young man in Order grey who had arrived while Alex was watching the man in green. The courier took the report and left. The door swung shut behind him.

Orin looked up. Found her watching. Held her gaze.

"The ledger requires consistency."

"The ledger requires the truth."

"The truth is what the ledger records."

She didn't answer. The man in Grain-Lord green rose from his table and left by the kitchen door. The farmers at the bar kept drinking. The candle on Orin's table guttered in a draft.

She folded the report and put it in her pocket. For later.
