# Chapter 10 – The Mountain Pass

The Hold-Kingdoms had a simple rule: everything above the treeline wanted to kill you, and most of it was faster.

He had been moving north for a week. The Grain-Lords' Confederacy had flattened behind him, its grey fields giving way to the foothills of the Crown of Winter, and the foothills giving way to rock and ice and air that cut the lungs with every breath. The paper was still in his coat. Elias's message. Renne's location. Two days east, she'd said. He'd had the paper for seven days.

Bad arithmetic.

The job was not find her. The job was get to her without the people trying to kill her watching you walk through the door. Five Order teams in the Confederacy when he'd arrived. Caravan talk had confirmed it. He had no reason to believe the number had decreased. An Insensitive passed through magical scans as nothing. Hark was not Insensitive. He was a collected mage with two decades of frost-scar debt, a Light mutation on his ledger, and two Order agents dead in a forest when he was ten. The Order had a file. Files did not expire.

If anyone had been tracking his movement through Elias's network—the clean-knuckled guard at the waystation, a dust-reader with a long memory, a Savant who'd read his scars in passing—walking directly east to Renne was the same as delivering her to the Cradle with a courier's note.

He needed to go the wrong direction first. A long way wrong.

The Dominion was west. Order presence sparse there—Dominion law resisted external jurisdiction, and desert terrain made magical tracking expensive. If he surfaced visibly in the Dominion, drew any tail across two mountain ranges into foreign territory, then vanished back east, any watcher would either break off or expose themselves.

Joel had called it the Pragmatic Retreat. Run a losing fight. Come back later with more friends. The friend he came back with was the absence of a tail.

The paper stayed in his coat. He did not open it.

---

The caravan was assembling at the base of the first mountain pass when he reached it. Five wagons, eight people: four merchants, three hired guards, one cook. The merchants were Hold-Kingdoms traders, their furs thick and their voices thicker, arguing about tolls and weather and the price of salt. The cook was a woman with flour-dusted sleeves and a knife that had seen more vegetables than violence. The guards stood apart, watching the treeline.

Hark offered coin for passage. They accepted. A scarred sellsword between jobs was common enough on the high passes. No one asked his name.

He took his place at the rear of the caravan and let his eyes run over the guards.

Two of them were career caravan men. Spear-calluses thickened their palms in the pattern of men who'd held a shaft through twenty winters. Their shield arms were heavier than their knife arms, the muscle built from bearing weight rather than striking. They moved with the tired competence of professionals who had learned that boredom was the best outcome a guard could hope for.

The third guard's hands were different.

Knuckles too clean. No weapon-callus pattern Hark recognised. His grip on the spear was correct—the form of someone trained in a School, the fingers spaced precisely, the angle exact—but unpractised. The wood sat in his hands the way a borrowed tool sat in a craftsman's grip: familiar but not worn to his shape. His stance was balanced. His breathing was even. His eyes were patient.

Too patient. Not bored, not watchful—waiting. A hunter's patience, not a guard's.

Hark looked away.

The pass narrowed above the treeline, the path squeezing to single file between rock faces. Bad geometry for anyone following. Good geometry for spotting a tail.

The caravan climbed. The snow deepened. The trees fell away below them, a dark smear against white that shrank with each hour until it was a memory. The air thinned. Hark's breath came harder, his chest working to pull warmth from the cold. His shoulder ached where the axe-man's haft had caught him in the alley—the bruise the altitude had woken from dormancy. His frost-scars throbbed, the old debt recognising the thin heat, the low pressure, the body's struggle to hold its own warmth.

He adjusted his gloves. Did not look at his hands.

The merchants talked. Hark listened without contributing, the way he'd learned to listen in a dozen caravan camps across three realms—ears open, mouth shut, presence forgettable.

"Five teams," one merchant said. He was a broad man with a beard that trapped snowmelt. "The Order's got five teams in the east. Never sent five for anyone."

"Four now," the cook said. She was wrapping hard cheese in oiled cloth. "Heard one team got destroyed. North of the Confederacy. Blood-mage went rogue, killed his own Silencer before they put him down."

The merchant grunted. "Good riddance."

Team destroyed. A Blood-mage. He'd seen the type before—collared, twitching, a weapon waiting for the leash to break.

"Storm-mage incident at a southern port," another merchant said. "Lightning in the harbour. Ships burning. The League's up in arms about it."

"League's always up in arms."

"This is different. They're saying it was an Order mage. Unaffiliated with the port authority. The League doesn't like the Order playing in their waters."

Bad odds. Too many teeth. Stay north.

"A Hold-Kingdom lord on the western slope has been asking questions about travellers crossing the pass," the first merchant said. "Unusual interest. Border tolls haven't changed, but his men are at the gate. Turning back anyone without proper seals."

"What kind of questions?"

"Descriptions. Destinations. Who they're working for."

The cook frowned. "That's not toll-work."

"No. It's not."

Hark stared at the snow ahead of the lead wagon. The clean-knuckled guard was walking point, his spear held at rest, his patient eyes on the pass.

---

The night shelter was carved into the mountain's gut, a waystation built over a geothermal vent field. Heat rose through cracks in the stone floor, warming the rock, warming the air, warming the eight bodies crowded into the small space. The smell of sulphur threaded through everything, faint and acrid. Hark spread his bedroll near a vent and sat with his back to the wall.

The warmth reminded him of something.

The Cradle's training halls had vents like this. Geothermal, piped up from the mountain's heart, the heat bleeding through grates in the stone floor. The instructors said it was for comfort. Lea said it was so they wouldn't freeze before they burned out. She was right about most things.

He drew his knife and the oilstone. Three strokes per side. The rhythm was a quiet constant. One. Two. Three. Turn the blade. One. Two. Three. His hands moved without thought. The edge brightened. The cold in his scars answered the warmth in the stone, and neither cancelled the other.

The clean-knuckled guard approached.

He sat across the fire without asking, a familiarity he had not earned. His hands were empty. His eyes were still patient. Up close, Hark could see the faint line of a scar along his jaw—old, healed clean, the kind a blade left when the wound was treated by someone who knew what they were doing.

"Headed west?" the guard asked.

"Dominion," Hark said. His voice was flat. The knife kept moving. One. Two. Three.

"What's your trade?"

"Salt merchant."

"Salt."

"Between contracts."

The guard nodded. His hands rested on his knees, the fingers still. Too still. Not the stillness of relaxation—the stillness of training. Someone had taught this man to suppress his tells. That was not caravan work. That was Order auxiliary, or a bounty collector working the network for information.

"Long way to the Dominion," the guard said. "Longer alone."

Hark said nothing. The knife kept moving.

The guard waited. The fire popped. The other merchants were arguing about tolls again, their voices a low rumble on the other side of the shelter. The cook was already asleep, her flour-dusted sleeves folded under her head. The two caravan guards were playing dice by the door, their spear-callused hands throwing bones against the stone.

The guard rose. He did not say goodnight. He retreated to his bedroll on the far side of the shelter, his back to the wall, his hands still visible. Still empty. Still too still.

Hark did not sleep.

---

He woke before dawn. He had never been fully under.

His pack had been moved. Not stolen—searched. The contents were all there. The debt-null fragments were untouched, their smooth weight still in the inner pouch. His coin had been counted and replaced in the correct pouch. A professional inventory. Someone had wanted to know what he was carrying, not what he was worth.

The clean-knuckled guard was gone. His bedroll was cold. He had left during the night.

Hark sat in the dark and let the arithmetic settle. The guard had not taken anything. He had catalogued. He was not a thief. He was not here on his own. He had sent word ahead, not back. Someone on the western side was expecting a description.

The Dominion detour was no longer precaution.

He broke from the caravan at the first fork. The main track continued north, winding toward the Hold-Kingdoms' interior. The northwest track descended toward the Dominion, a thinner path, less travelled, the snow deeper and the markers older. He took it without saying goodbye. The snow filled his tracks behind him.

---

He climbed alone.

The air was thin enough now that each breath cost. His shoulder ached with the steady throb of old injury woken by cold. His frost-scars burned faintly, a low constant hum beneath the skin. The path narrowed to a goat-track, then to a suggestion of a track, then to a ridge of bare rock scoured clean by wind.

Joel had said, near the end, when the blood cancer had taken everything but his voice: You're going to keep walking into things you shouldn't. I can't stop you. Just walk back out again.

He was still walking. He hadn't walked back out yet.

He adjusted his pack. Kept climbing.

The ridge rose to a narrow spine of rock, the highest point of the pass. He stopped. The view opened behind him: the green smear of the Confederacy, invisible now under cloud, a memory of fields and rivers and a farmhouse where a woman with scarred hands was waiting for someone who was not him. Ahead, the western slopes fell away into something drier, older, a haze of heat and dust. The Dominion.

The light on the snow was clean and sharp, a piercing white. The ridge caught the dawn and held it, a line of gold on the edge of the world. He registered it for half a second. Then he looked away.

He touched his coat pocket. The paper was still there. He dropped his hand.

The Dominion was on the other side of the mountain. So was everything else he'd been avoiding.
