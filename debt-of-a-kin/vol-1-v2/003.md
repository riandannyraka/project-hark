# Chapter 3 – The Ghost

The road was a scar on the snow. He walked it anyway.

Dawn came grey and thin, the cold a steady pressure against his face, his chest, the exposed skin of his wrists where the gloves had worn through. He had walked through the night, and his shoulder from the alley still ached, a deep bruise that the cold had numbed into something distant and manageable. The scar was a week old. The contract was older by only a few hours. He kept walking.

The caravan appeared ahead as a dark smudge on the white, wagons strung out along the trade road, oxen steaming in the cold. He let his gaze run over them in the order the old training had burned into him, the automatic sequence that never switched off.

Hands. Two drivers clutched reins in heavy mitts. A young guard walked beside the lead wagon, his right hand resting on a spear shaft, fingers flexing every few steps. A merchant sat in the bed of the last wagon, hands buried in furs, nothing visible. No one had steel drawn.

Exits. The road ran straight east into a sparse treeline. Open ground on both sides. No choke points, no ambush ledges, no cover for anything larger than a crouching man. If things went wrong, he could break for the trees and lose pursuers in the snow. Not ideal, but workable.

Weapons. The guard's spear was cheap iron, the blade uneven, the haft a fraction too long for close work. A farmer's weapon, not a soldier's. No other steel visible among the drivers. The merchant might have a skinning knife under the furs; Hark filed the possibility and dismissed it.

Faces. The guard glanced back at him, young, maybe twenty, eyes tired but alert. Not hostile. Not afraid. Just a man doing a job he wasn't paid enough for. Hark looked away before the glance could become an invitation to speak.

He fell in at the rear of the caravan without asking. One of the traders turned, saw him, and turned back. A sellsword between jobs was answer enough. The cold made everyone economical with words.

---

The treeline shifted on his left an hour into the march. The pines thickened, their branches heavy with snow, the spaces between them dark and still. Hark noticed the guard's posture change before he noticed why. The young man's hand tightened on the spear shaft. His head turned, fixed on a stand of pine fifty paces ahead. His stride shortened.

Hark scanned the trees.

Three shapes. Bundled against the cold, poorly concealed behind the first line of trunks. One carried a short bow, the curve of it unmistakable even through the branches. The other two had swords, rust-pitted iron that caught the grey light in dull flashes. Bandits. They were positioned badly, too far forward, expecting the caravan to walk into the killing ground without warning. The guard had seen them. That made the difference.

The guard moved to intercept, placing himself between the treeline and the wagons, spear angled across his body. Hark read his stance in the space between one step and the next. Honed, not Sharp. The footwork was solid but rigid, the grip correct but tense. He would handle the two with blades. He would hold them long enough for the wagons to clear. But the archer would pick him off before he closed the distance. The guard had placed himself in the open, and the archer had a clear shot.

Hark drifted left.

He didn't speak to the traders or signal the guard. He angled into the treeline at a walk that became a shadow's movement, his boots finding the deep snow without sound, the old instinct taking over. The cold bit at his face. His shoulder protested the shift in balance and he ignored it.

Hands. The archer was nocking an arrow, fingers clumsy with cold, the bowstring slick with frost. His draw would be slow. The two swordsmen were spreading out, their blades held low, no guard stance, no training. They were watching the guard, not the trees.

Exits. The archer had a clear line to the guard and no escape behind him. A dense thicket of dead thorn and frozen brush blocked any retreat. Once committed, he was fixed in place.

Weapons. Hark's knife was already in his hand. The ugly secondary blade stayed at his back. The weight of the knife was familiar, the wrapped hilt cold against his palm.

The guard engaged. Spear jabbed, quick and clean, keeping the swordsmen at distance. The first bandit lunged and caught the shaft across his ribs. The second circled wide, looking for an opening. The archer raised the bow, drew, aimed at the guard's exposed flank.

Hark threw.

The knife spun once in the grey light, a brief silver blur against the dark of the pines, and took the archer in the throat. The sound was wet and choked and short. The bow dropped. The arrow skittered sideways into the snow. The archer folded, both hands going to his neck, and then he stopped moving.

Hark was already in motion. He crossed the distance in four strides, retrieved the knife, wiped the blade on the dead man's cloak. The body was already cooling. By the time he straightened, the guard had run one swordsman through and the other was fleeing into the trees, crashing through branches, leaving a trail of kicked-up snow.

The guard stared at him. Eyes wide, breath steaming, the spear still braced in both hands. The body at his feet leaked red into the white. The guard's mouth opened.

Hark met his gaze for half a second, then looked away. He sheathed the knife. He walked back to the rear of the caravan as if nothing had happened.

The guard didn't speak. The traders, huddled by the wagons, hadn't registered the full sequence. They saw a dead man in the trees and a guard standing over another and a sellsword walking away with blood on his glove. That was enough. The caravan kept moving.

Hark's pulse had barely risen. The knife throw had been smooth, the shoulder holding steady, the release clean. Still sharp enough. He didn't dwell on it. The bandits were a tactical problem. Now they were snow-covered shapes receding behind the wagons, and the guard was still glancing back at him every few minutes, and Hark ignored it. Connection was not part of the contract.

---

The caravan halted at a frozen spring, a ring of trampled snow where other travellers had stopped before them. Mid-morning light, pale and weak, the sun a smear of white behind the cloud cover. The drivers unhitched the oxen. The traders built a small fire, more smoke than flame. The guard took up position at the edge of the camp, spear planted, still stealing glances at Hark. He didn't approach. Smart.

Hark sat apart, his back to a boulder, the camp spread out before him. Exits. The road east, the road west, the treeline now a hundred paces behind. He could see everything. No one could approach without crossing open ground.

He drew his knife and the oilstone from his belt pouch. Three strokes per side. The rhythm was a quiet thing, metal on grit, a sound that belonged to him alone. His hands moved without thought. One. Two. Three. Turn the blade. One. Two. Three. The edge brightened. The cold sat in his scars, the old frost-lines on his knuckles white against the reddened skin. He kept stroking. The repetition was a wall against the quiet, and the quiet was full of things he didn't name.

The traders talked. He heard them without listening, the way he heard the wind or the creak of the wagon wheels, background noise that his mind sorted and mostly discarded. Then a word snagged.

*Healer.*

Another. *Sensitive children.*

Then a phrase, dropped in a lower voice, as if the speaker half-believed saying it aloud would summon something. *The Seed.*

Hark's hand paused on the knife. A half-beat. Then resumed.

The second trader leaned closer to the fire. "Order's sent five teams. Five. Never sent five for anyone."

Five teams. The alley had been four men, and that had been bad odds. This was worse. He filed the number without flinching. The fragments in his belt pouch were smooth weights against his hip. The contract was signed. The job was the job.

"They say she's got hands scarred to hell," the first trader said. "Paid for every life she's saved."

Hark's chest tightened.

It was a small thing, a catch in the ribs, a band of pressure that squeezed once and released. His pulse kicked. His jaw clenched. He stared at the horizon, counting miles to the treeline, to the next ridge, to anything that was distance and geometry and not the thing curling in his chest.

Scarred hands.

The cold wind came across the spring and found his own scars, the frost-lines that mapped his knuckles, and the sensation was a key turning in a lock he had forgotten existed.

A young woman's hands. Healing-scarred, the palms ridged with pale tissue, trembling as they pressed a cloth to his side. The pressure of her fingers against the wound. The warmth of her, the only warm thing in a room that was all cold stone and colder air. Her voice, low and steady, a voice he hadn't heard in fifteen years: *Hold still. This will hurt.*

He didn't see her face. The memory stopped at her hands. The cloth reddening. Her fingers frosting over, the healing debt taking its payment in real time, ice crystals forming on her knuckles while the wound in his side closed. The pain had been extraordinary, the cold of her magic worse than the blade that had put him there, and she had borne it without a sound.

His own hand drifted to his side. The scar was still there, a seam of raised tissue under his tunic. He touched it through the cloth and pulled his hand away.

He resumed the knife strokes. One. Two. Three.

The name almost came. The first syllable rose in his throat, a sound he hadn't made in fifteen years, and his chest slammed shut around it. The pressure locked down, a physical refusal. His mind went blank at the exact point where the name should have been. The drawer closed. The key turned.

He breathed. The cold air bit his lungs. The horizon was still there. Miles to count.

Pay's good. Wouldn't mind getting paid. The words were a door he walked through, and on the other side there was only the knife in his hand and the road ahead and the contract waiting to be fulfilled.

---

The caravan readied to move. The traders kicked snow over the fire. The drivers hitched the oxen. The guard took his position at the lead wagon, and this time he offered a small nod across the camp, a gesture of acknowledgement that asked for nothing. Hark returned nothing. It wasn't coldness. It was the rules.

He fell in at the rear as the wagons lurched forward. The road unspooled east, a white scar on the white ground, the treeline thinning as the land opened into the long descent toward the Grain-Lords' Confederacy. His shoulder ached. His scars ached. The cold was a constant, and he walked inside it.

The snow filled his tracks behind him.

Her hands stayed with him. The image clung, refusing to fade, the scarred fingers, the frost forming on her knuckles, the tremor she couldn't hide. He didn't push it away. He walked, and the miles passed, and he didn't name the pull or the pain or the thing that tightened in his chest every time the wind blew cold across his hands.

He remembered her hands before her face. That was the problem with memories – they remembered what he'd forbidden to name.
