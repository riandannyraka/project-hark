# Chapter 13 – The Desert Hunt

He heard them before he saw them. That was the only thing that went right.

Pre-dawn in the dry riverbed, the walls of red stone rising twenty feet on either side, the floor cracked mud and loose shale. Hark had been choosing routes with cover since the lord's hall—old habit, old survival—and the riverbed had seemed like the right choice. Winding. Narrow. Hidden from the open flats above. Good cover. Bad exits. He was halfway through when he stopped.

The air was wrong.

Too still. The pre-dawn wind should have been moving through the passage, a low sigh between the rock walls. It wasn't. The silence was absolute, the kind of silence that wasn't natural, the kind that had been made.

He reached for the old sense, the one the Cradle had trained into him before they'd trained anything else. Not magic. Not a Gate opening. Just the Fire Gate's base body-sense—reading warmth the way a hound read scent. Joel had called it reading the warmth. The instructors had called it thermal perception. Hark called it staying alive.

Two signatures.

One north, on the ridge above the riverbed. Stationary. Waiting. One south, closing slowly along the riverbed floor. Heavy. Deliberate. The rhythm of footsteps transmitted through stone.

Flanked. In a dry riverbed. Exits: upstream—open flat, no cover. Downstream—narrows between rock, the walls closing to a slot. Terrain: loose shale, nothing to grip. Weapons: knife, secondary blade, three fragments. Against: unknown mages. Bad odds.

A voice came from the northern ridge. The desert carried sound perfectly in the pre-dawn stillness—clear, confident, almost conversational.

"You don't have to die here. My name is Vyktor. The man closing behind you is Rask. Tell us where the Seed is and we walk away. I'll even let you keep your hands."

Hark didn't answer. The offer was a delay. It gave the southern signature time to close the angle. He read the speaker: male, Storm Gate. The air pressure had shifted, a charge building like the moment before lightning. The hairs on his arms were lifting. The southern signature was heavier, slower—Earth Gate, vibration through the soles of his boots, the shale trembling faintly with each step.

He ran the arithmetic. The Storm mage wanted him to run north—toward the voice, toward the open flat where lightning had room to arc. The Earth mage was driving him there. Flush and strike. Classic paired-mage tactics. The Cradle had trained him to recognise them. The Cradle had trained him to be one of them.

He ran south.

Straight at the Earth mage. Which was wrong. Which was why it worked.

Joel's voice, a fragment pulled loose by the sprint: Broken Ground. Deny the enemy the push they planned. Make them fight on ground you choose.

The riverbed shuddered. The Earth mage had felt him coming—had read his footsteps through the stone—and was already reaching into the walls. Ancient desert rock, deep mineral layers, centuries of heat and pressure. The stone fractured. Fragments rose from the walls in a slow curtain of shrapnel, fist-sized and larger, hanging in the air for a heartbeat before they began to move. The mage was driving him toward the upstream flat. Toward the Storm mage. Toward the open sky where lightning didn't miss.

Hark didn't stop. A stone shard caught his left shoulder—a deep bruise, the impact spinning him half-around. He kept his feet. Kept moving. The shrapnel was thickest at the edges of the curtain, thinnest at the source. The mage was the source. The mage was where he was going.

Rask was huge.

Granite-cracked hands, arms thick with the muscle of someone who had moved stone for years, shoulders broad as a doorway. His face was blunt, unreadable, the face of a man who had stopped thinking about violence the way a hammer stops thinking about nails. He hadn't expected Hark to close. Earth working required contact with ground—Rask needed to read the stone, shape it, throw it. At range, he was a landslide. In close quarters, on shale he hadn't read, he was a man with heavy hands and slow feet.

Hark closed the last three paces before Rask could pull another curtain from the walls.

Rask swung. The same Iron Rib pattern the woman at the lord's hall had used—a rooted guard, a hammer fist powered by mass. Cruder than hers. Stronger. Hark ducked under it, felt the wind of the blow pass over his scalp, got inside the guard. His knife found the gap beneath Rask's ribs—but the angle was wrong, the muscle too thick, the blade skidding off a wall of sinew. Not deep enough.

Rask grunted. Backhanded Hark across the jaw.

His vision whited. He went down. The shale cut into his back. His knife was pinned beneath Rask's knee, the weight of the man a crushing pressure on his thigh. The secondary blade was trapped under his own spine. His left arm was free. His right was not.

Rask's hands found his throat.

Weight like a landslide. Thumbs digging into the soft tissue above his windpipe, the same place the Iron Rib woman had pressed two days ago, the bruise still tender, still blooming. His vision tunnelled. The edges went dark. Rask's face above him was calm, patient, a man doing a job that required pressure and time.

The desert stone beneath them had been absorbing heat for months. Every day of white sun, every hour of baked stillness, every degree of the Dominion's furnace had sunk into the rock and stayed there. Hark's Fire Gate had been closed for fifteen years. He opened it.

Not to the Light. Not to his internal reservoir—that was a last resort, a door he wouldn't open again, a thing that had killed two men in a forest and nearly killed him. He opened it to the stone. Drew the accumulated thermal energy out of the desert floor and converted it—not into a projectile, not into a beam, but into his own muscles. The Fire Gate's base state: thermal updraft, muscle superheating. The Cradle had called it a kinetic amplifier. The instructors had called it a tool. Lea had called it a way to burn out faster.

The heat came. His body temperature spiked. The frost-scars on his forearms screamed as the old channels were forced to carry environmental heat instead of internal debt—a different kind of burning, a different kind of cold. His left hand closed on Rask's wrist.

The grip strength was wrong. Impossible. A furnace in his fingers. He felt Rask's bones grind.

Rask's eyes widened.

Hark surged upward. Not standing—there was no leverage, no room. Just a single explosive movement powered by superheated muscle, his forehead crunching into Rask's nose. Cartilage shattered. Blood sprayed. Rask reeled back, his hands leaving Hark's throat, and Hark's right hand came free, found the secondary blade, and drove it up under Rask's chin before Rask could recover.

The heat left him. The frost came back—a wave of cold so intense his vision greyed. The thermal energy was spent. The debt was written: new frost-lines threading up his left forearm, branching like frozen lightning, white on reddened skin.

Rask fell. Dead before he hit the shale.

Hark was on his hands and knees, gasping. The fever was already building—his body paying for the muscle superheat, sweat beading on his forehead despite the pre-dawn cold. His left arm was a burning chill. His glove was singed but intact. He pushed himself up.

The northern ridge was empty.

He climbed out of the riverbed onto the flat. No choice. The open ground was a killing field and he was walking onto it. The sky was paling in the east, the first grey light before dawn.

Vyktor was waiting.

Younger than Hark had expected. Late twenties. His hands crackled with contained lightning—arcs jumping between his knuckles, brief blue-white bridges that snapped and vanished and snapped again. The debt was written in his body: a permanent twitch beneath his left eye, nerve damage, progressive. He looked at Hark the way a storm looked at a ship.

"You killed my partner."

Hark didn't answer. His left arm was on fire. His knife was still in his right hand.

Vyktor began building a discharge. On the flat, he had clear air—no cover, no terrain to disrupt the arc. A Storm mage with a full internal reservoir could build a localised strike without atmospheric source. Costly. Possible. The air ionised. Hark's hair stood on end. The charge was a pressure in his teeth, a copper taste on his tongue.

He could not close the distance. The flat was open for fifty paces in every direction. Vyktor would arc him before he covered half.

Last Breath. The Turn. Find the thing he doesn't expect.

His reservoir was already depleted from the muscle superheat. His left arm was frost-burned to the mid-forearm. The fever was climbing. But the stone was still there—still holding months of accumulated thermal debt. The environmental reservoir was not empty. Hark was.

Vyktor released the arc.

Hark opened the Gate a second time.

He pulled heat from the stone in a raw, unfocused burst. Not into his muscles—his body wouldn't take another kinetic draw, the fever was already too high, the frost too deep. Into the air between them. A thermal shock wave, superheated air exploding outward like a forge door thrown open at full blast.

The arc hit it and destabilised.

Lightning grounded into superheated sand. The discharge scattered—white-hot channels splitting in every direction, the backblast catching Vyktor across the arms, the chest, the side of his face. He went down on one knee, screaming, his own working turned against him.

The cost to Hark came immediately.

His left glove burned off. The frost-scars on his forearm were fully visible now—the old knuckle-lines, the new environmental-draw scars branching toward his elbow, the freshest ones still forming, white lines threading in real time. His internal temperature spiked. His vision swam. Blood dripped from his nose—the fever breaking vessels, the Light mutation's thermal echo, a warning he was approaching a line he hadn't crossed since he was ten.

He stayed on his feet.

Vyktor was still moving. Even burned, even with his discharge spent, he got a hand up. A secondary arc—smaller, desperate, pulled from his own nerve-signals—clipped Hark across the ribs. Not a full strike. Enough to knock him sideways. He hit the ground, rolled, got up. His ribs screamed. Cracked, probably. Not broken through.

Vyktor was standing. His arms were scorched. The side of his face was red and blistering. His hands were shaking—not the twitch, not the nerve damage, just shaking. His eyes flicked to the riverbed. To Rask's body. Then back to Hark.

The calculation was visible. Fight and risk dying. Retreat and live to hunt again.

Vyktor backed away. Not fleeing—withdrawing. His burned face was unreadable.

"You're dead," he said. Not a threat. A promise. "Maybe not today. But you're dead."

Hark didn't answer. He ran downstream into the narrowing rock passage, the walls closing around him, the shale giving way to solid stone. Vyktor didn't follow.

In the passage, Hark stopped. Breathed. His ribs pulled with each inhale—cracked, definitely cracked. His left arm was a ruin. The old frost-scars on his knuckles, the new ones branching up his forearm, the freshest lines still settling into the skin like ice forming on water. The environmental-draw scars were wider than the internal-use lines, more jagged. They looked like frozen lightning.

His right hand was steady. The knife was still in it.

He listened. Vyktor's footsteps, retreating. Not toward Rask's body—toward the northern ridge. Leaving his partner where he fell.

He's not reporting. He left the body. Which means he's not supposed to be here—or he doesn't want anyone to know he lost. Either way, he'll be back. Not for the Order. For himself.

The route east was no longer clean. Vyktor knew he was alive. Knew his Gate—had felt the heat draw, the thermal shock wave, the base-state Fire that didn't quite match the file. Vyktor had crossed two mountain ranges to find him. He would cross an ocean to finish it.

Not clean. Catalogued. The route east just got more expensive. He needed a different way back.

He sat with his back against the passage wall. His left arm was a mess—the scars visible now, no glove to hide them, the new lines reaching past his wrist toward the elbow. He flexed his fingers. They moved. That was enough.

Joel had looked at his hands once—the old frost-scars, the knife nicks, the crooked finger that had healed wrong—and said, You're going to run out of skin before you run out of people trying to kill you.

Hark had said, Then I'll use theirs.

Joel had laughed. It had been the last time Hark heard him laugh.

He pulled his sleeve down over the new scars. It didn't cover everything.

He moved deeper into the passage. It wound south, then west—deeper into the Dominion. He couldn't go east yet. Vyktor would be watching the eastern routes, waiting. He needed to go further wrong before he could go right. A port, maybe—the Dominion's southern coast, or the Maelstrom League's northern waters. A ship. A different set of eyes.

The paper was still in his coat. He touched it once, through the fabric. Didn't open it.

He counted what he had left. Water low. Knife intact. Three fragments. Ribs cracked. Fever manageable. Left arm a ledger of new debt. The needle still in his pocket.

Less than before. Still enough.
