# Prologue – The Foundling House

Cold.

Rain on her face before she knew she was standing still. The weight in her arms had stopped shifting. A wrapped stillness, warm where it pressed against her chest, but growing no warmer.

She looked down. The cloth was grey, Order-issue, already darkening with rain. A face inside it. Eyes closed. Lips a tiny seam. The rain touched the baby's cheek and the baby did not flinch.

*Daughter.*

The word arrived in her mind without permission. She let it sit there, a stone dropped into still water. She did not speak it. Her lips stayed closed. The rain ran across them anyway.

Grey stone before her. The foundling house rose out of the Cradle's inner ring, a block of silence against the rain-hiss. One window glowed, low and orange, a Sister's candle behind oiled parchment. The door was iron-banded oak. Closed.

Her arms were shaking. Not from the cold.

She walked. The mud sucked at her shoes. The baby's weight rode against her heartbeat, and her heartbeat was too fast, a runner's pulse with nowhere to run. Her hands—she felt them now, the ache in the knuckles, the old tightness across the palms where the healing debt had written its ledger into her skin—her hands were the only steady part of her. They held. They did what hands were meant to do.

The door was heavy. She pushed through into the smell of clean linen and cold stone and the faint iron tang that lived in every Order building, the taste of old workings, debts paid in blood and breath.

A Sister stood waiting. Grey robe. Grey veil. Hands folded at her waist. Clean hands. Uncracked. Hands that had never pulled a fever out of a child's chest or set a bone while the debtor's own marrow ached in sympathy.

The Sister's eyes moved to the bundle.

"Name?"

The word scraped out of her throat. "Alexandria."

She did not know she had chosen it until she heard it. The syllables landed in the air between them and became irrevocable. A name given. A name that would now exist in the Order's books, inked into a file, a child who was no longer hers.

"Your name?"

The Sister's voice was level. No judgement. No curiosity. The question was procedural, a slot in a ledger waiting to be filled.

She looked at her own hands. The scars ran white against the rain-reddened skin. Thumb to knuckle. Knuckle to palm. Palm to wrist. The fingers traced the path without her telling them to. A circuit of old pain, familiar as breath.

"No one."

The Sister did not blink. She reached out, and her clean hands received the bundle. The transfer was smooth. Practiced. A weight leaving Renne's arms that should have been a relief and was not.

The Sister turned. Her robes whispered against the stone floor. She carried Alexandria—the name now attached to a small grey shape in a Sister's arms—across the room to a row of iron cots against the far wall. The candlelight caught the edges of things: the rim of a water basin, the curve of a feeding horn, the iron bars of the cots, small and identical.

The baby did not cry.

Renne stood in the doorway. Her arms were empty. The rain blew in behind her, wetting the threshold. The Sister bent over the cot. Placed the bundle down. Adjusted the cloth. A gesture that might have been tenderness or might have been routine.

The door was still open. Renne's feet were still on the threshold. Her body had not yet decided what to do with itself.

The Sister straightened. Did not turn around.

Renne stepped back into the rain.

She did not look back. The door closed behind her with the sound of iron settling into stone. A sound that did not echo.

The road was mud. Her tracks from earlier had already softened, filling with rainwater. She walked. The rain needled her scalp, ran inside her collar, turned the grey cloth of her tunic dark. Her arms hung at her sides. They felt wrong without the weight. Light. Useless.

Twenty paces. Thirty. The foundling house became a shape behind rain-curtain. Then a suggestion of a shape. Then nothing.

She stopped.

Her hands came up to her chest. Pressed flat against the sternum. The scars on her palms were cold. The skin beneath was warm. Her heart was still running.

*The river remembers.*

The thought surfaced from somewhere deep, a silt-stirred thing. She did not know what it meant. Only that it was true. The river remembered. Water carried. Water held. The river was miles behind her now, and she was walking away from it, but the words stayed, a pebble in her shoe, a thing she could not shake loose.

She pressed harder. The pressure against her chest was almost pain. The rain filled her tracks behind her, erasing her passage one footprint at a time.

She started walking again.

The road led east, away from the Cradle's centre, toward the outer rings where the traders' carts would be hitching up at dawn, where a girl of seventeen with scarred hands and no name might find space on a wagon heading anywhere. Anywhere but here. The rain came down harder. It washed the mud from her shoes. It washed the last warmth of the baby's weight from her chest.

She did not speak. She did not weep. She walked, and her hands kept moving, thumb to knuckle, knuckle to palm, palm to wrist, a prayer without words, a debt without ledger, a river that remembered everything.

The road unspooled ahead of her into the dark. The rain erased her from behind.

She did not look back.
