# Chapter 15 – The Grain-Lords' Confederacy

The farm looked smaller than he remembered.

He walked the last mile through the Confederacy's black soil, the sky enormous above him. His left arm was bound in a torn sleeve, the new scars hidden but throbbing. His ribs pulled on each inhale. He had been walking for five weeks and it showed in the way he carried his weight, the favouring of the right side, the hollows under his eyes.

Elias stepped out of the barn. His knuckles were more swollen than before, the joints distended, the bone-brittleness advancing. The Soil Gate took its payment in calcium, and Elias had been altering land-debt records for weeks. His eyes moved over Hark—the torn sleeve, the favouring, the five weeks written in his face.

"You're still alive."

"Mostly."

Inside, the same kitchen. The wooden box was still on the shelf. Hark didn't ask to see it. The clay bird was inside, and the folded ledger page, and the thumbprint of a boy who had been twelve years old. That was enough.

He took the chair with its back to the wall. Elias reached for his left arm. Hark let him.

Elias pushed back the sleeve. The new scars were livid—branching frost-lines, wider than the old internal-use marks, threading from knuckles to mid-forearm. They looked like frozen lightning, jagged and pale against the reddened skin. Elias traced a line without touching it.

"Environmental draws. Desert stone, by the pattern. The debt is fresh. Weeks, not months." His swollen finger moved to a second scar—wider, more diffuse, the edges blurred. "This one's different. A burst. You threw heat at something."

"Someone."

"Did they die?"

"One did. The other walked away."

Elias turned Hark's arm slightly in the grey light. "Storm Gate. The scars have a charge-residue. Faint, but it's there. He clipped you."

"The ribs."

"You're not clean. If he survived, he can read these."

"He won't report. I burned him and he walked away. That's not something you put in a file."

Elias released his arm. Hark looked down at the new scars. He hadn't examined them properly since the desert. The environmental-draw lines were wider than the old ones, the channels forced to carry external heat instead of internal debt. He traced one with his thumb. His fingers were steady. The scar was cold.

"The safehouse moved," Elias said. "Farmhouse near the old-growth forest, two days east. The eastern path is clear. I've altered the records."

Hark reached into his coat. The paper was still there—folded five times, the creases worn white, the edges soft from five weeks against his ribs. It had crossed the Hold-Kingdoms and the Dominion desert and a lord's hall and a riverbed where a man had died. It was still legible. He placed it on the table.

Elias looked at it. When he unfolded it, his index finger bent slightly wrong—the joint giving out. He read his own handwriting. Slid the paper back.

"You never opened it."

"Didn't need to."

"But you didn't go."

Hark didn't answer.

"Five weeks," Elias said. "The Storm-mage found you. The bounty hunters found you. The lord's raven found you. Anyone else?"

"A clean-knuckled guard at the mountain pass. Order auxiliary, or a bounty collector. He searched my pack while I slept. Sent word ahead."

"And?"

"I broke from the caravan before the trap closed. Took the Dominion descent instead. He didn't follow."

"You're sure."

"His hands were too clean. He was a reader, not a fighter."

Elias nodded.

"Five teams," he said. "Team Two's Blood-mage went rogue—rumour says he killed his Silencer before they put him down. Team Three's Savants are asking questions two days south. Team Four—you already know. Team Five took the waystation. Marta's cousin is dead." He paused. "Team One is the problem."

"Why?"

"No debt trace. No Gate residue. No witnesses. The other teams leave a signature—ash from the Ash-mage, storm from the Storm-mage, ledger entries from the Savants. Team One leaves nothing. That means no one on that team is casting. Which means they're either a Silencer moving alone, or a Silencer paired with a Savant who's keeping their perception passive. Either way, they're not leaving evidence because they're not using magic. That's not luck. That's training."

Hark filed this. An invisible Silencer with no debt signature. No witnesses. No survivors to describe them.

"If they're not using magic, they can't be tracked by debt."

"Which means the only way to find them is to see them coming. And if you see a Silencer coming, it's already too late. They're trained to close distance before the target knows they're there. A good one—Sharp or better—can cross a room in the time it takes to open a Gate. You won't feel the debt because there's no debt to feel. You'll just feel the blade."

That was worse than Vyktor. Vyktor was a storm—loud, visible, trackable. This was a knife in the dark.

Hark looked at the wooden box. "I told you I'd kill them for you. I've killed one. Rask. Vyktor will come for me, and I'll finish it."

"That's not why you're doing this."

"No."

"But you're going to do it anyway."

"Yes."

Elias nodded.

Hark stood. At the door, the light on the barn's weathered wood caught him—a clean line of pale sun through cloud. He looked at it for half a second.

*Seventy-four. I'm counting this one.*

The words surfaced without permission—her voice, the smile that had barely curved her lips, the first time she'd heard him speak. *How many?* he'd asked, his voice rough from disuse, weeks into their stay. She'd counted him as a working. A healing. He'd never asked if the debt for that one had been worth it.

He looked away from the light. Touched his coat pocket. The new paper was next to the old one. The needle was still there. The flower was not—he'd given it to her on the last night, pressed flat between the pages of her herbalism book, the one with the yellowed paper and the cracked spine. She'd carried it for sixteen years. She was still carrying it.

"When you find her," Elias said, "tell her the eastern path is clear."

Hark nodded. Walked east.

The track led east through the black soil, the sky opening above him, the horizon a flat line of grey-green. He counted what he had left. Water low. Knife intact. Three fragments. Ribs healing. Left arm a ledger of new debt. The needle still in his pocket. The old paper still in his coat—unread for five weeks, now read, still carried.

Lea had wanted to go east. She'd talked about the Confederacy's rivers, the way the wind came down from the hills and made the water shiver. She'd never made it past the Cradle's outer wall.

He walked. The miles passed. The path was clean.

Two days to the old-growth forest. Two days to Renne. He had been walking for five weeks to cover a distance that should have taken two days. The arithmetic was terrible. The path was clean.

Hark folded the map. "Thank you," he said. Elias nodded. Neither looked at the other's eyes.
