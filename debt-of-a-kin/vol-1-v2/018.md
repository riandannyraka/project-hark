# Chapter 18 – The Maelstrom Admiral

The port smelled of salt and old fish and the particular rot of promises made at low tide.

Hark walked the docks with his collar up and his head down, the ash still rattling in his lungs, the burn on his left arm itching beneath the sleeve. Two days since Elias's barn. Two days of walking, the cracked ribs pulling on every deep breath, the cough wet and gritty when it came. He ignored it. He'd been ignoring things for five weeks. He was good at it.

The port was a sprawl of grey water and greyer sky, the Maelstrom League's northern coast hunched under its perpetual overcast. Gulls screamed. Rigging clattered against masts. Somewhere a ship's bell tolled the hour, and the sound carried across the harbour like a warning.

He needed passage. Renne's safehouse was on an island in the southern archipelago—the updated location Elias had given him. Overland travel was too slow, too exposed. A ship cut the time in half. But ships in the Maelstrom League didn't take coin. They took favours.

His eyes moved over the dock. Sailors coiling rope, their hands callused in the pattern of men who'd worked rigging for decades. A fishmonger's blade flashing through scales, quick and precise. A harbormaster's ink-stained fingers counting cargo manifests. No weapons drawn. No obvious threats. The dock was open, the harbour wide—bad cover, good sightlines. He could see anyone coming.

The Admiral's office was above a chandlery, a narrow room overlooking the harbour. Maps covered the walls—the archipelago in ink and parchment, routes marked in red, the Order's patrol patterns annotated in a cramped, precise hand. The Admiral himself was fifty, grey-bearded, with the callused hands of someone who had worked the rigging before he commanded it. His eyes were pale and too still.

Hark read the hands first. Folded on the desk. Too still. Not the stillness of relaxation—the stillness of someone who had already calculated the outcome of this conversation and was waiting for Hark to catch up.

"You want passage south," the Admiral said. "Through the straits. Past the Order's patrol routes."

"Yes."

"The straits are dangerous this season. Storms. Reefs. The Order's been running patrols every third day—looking for a fugitive healer, I'm told. Dangerous cargo, dangerous waters." He paused. "Expensive."

Hark placed a debt-null fragment on the desk. One of three remaining.

The Admiral picked it up. Turned it in the light. "Pre-Order. A life-debt, by the weight. Where did you get this?"

"That's not part of the trade."

"Everything's part of the trade." He set the fragment down. "One fragment buys you passage. But not safe passage. Safe passage costs more."

"How much more?"

"A promise. When the time comes, you owe me a favour. No questions. No refusal. One favour, called at my discretion."

Hark's chest tightened. A blank promise. The kind Joel had warned him about—a debt with no ceiling, called at the holder's convenience, no terms, no limits, no exit. Joel had walked away from a deal like this once, in the Hold-Kingdoms, and the lord who offered it had tried to have him killed for the insult. *A promise is a leash. Don't put one on unless you know who's holding the other end.*

He'd walked away from better deals with smaller hooks. He'd walked away from worse men than the Admiral for asking less.

He counted the alternatives. The straits were patrolled. Other ships wouldn't run them—not for coin, not for fragments. The Admiral's ship was the only one willing to take the risk. A ship got him south in three days. Overland took two weeks—two weeks of walking, two weeks of the Order tightening around Renne's safehouse, two weeks of the invisible team doing whatever invisible teams did. Two weeks meant the safehouse fell before he reached it. Two weeks meant Renne was dead.

The promise was unvalued. Her life wasn't.

That was the arithmetic. It was terrible. It was the only calculation that mattered.

He didn't name the thing beneath the arithmetic—the thing that had been pulling him east since Kellis put the fragments on the tavern table, through deserts and mountains and bodies, for five weeks and fifteen years. The rules didn't have a word for it. He took the deal instead.

"Agreed."

The Admiral smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "The ship leaves at dawn. Don't be late."

---

Alexandria arrived at the port on a different road.

She and Orin had been tracking a lead for two days. The village with the hammer had pointed them toward the coast—a Storm-mage had been seen here recently, a man with burned arms and a permanent twitch beneath his left eye. Vyktor. She didn't know the name yet, but the description was specific enough to follow.

Orin stopped at the edge of the dock. His eyes went distant for a moment—the Savant's look, reading signatures she could not perceive.

"A Storm-mage was here. Recently. The charge in the air is still settling."

Alex processed this. A Storm-mage meant Team Four was active. Team Four meant the hunt was converging. Her hand checked her knife—still there, still balanced. The weight was familiar. The ritual steadied her.

The port was crowded. Sailors, merchants, dockhands, a few mercenaries loitering near the hiring post. Her observational hierarchy activated without thought. Main dock, side alleys, the chandlery stairs, a crane boom that could provide elevated cover—mapped and filed. Sailors' rope-callused grips, a merchant's ink-stained fingers, a mercenary's knife-callused knuckles. Cutlasses, harpoons, the mercenary's blade—plain steel, well-maintained, Edge School pattern she didn't recognize from this distance. Weathered faces, bored expressions, one mercenary's eyes too watchful—an outlier. Filed.

Then her fifth observational target activated before she could suppress it.

He was sitting at the back of a dockside tavern, half-visible through a salt-grimed window. Grey-templed hair. A lean, weathered face. His left hand rested on the table—scarred. Frost-scars, white lines branching from knuckles toward the wrist. Even at this distance, she could see the pattern.

Her chest did something she couldn't name. The scars matched her own—not in shape, but in origin. Fire Gate debt. Old, deep. She'd seen her own hands in the mirror after training sessions, the faint grey lines on her fingers from controlled draws. His were worse. Older. The ledger of a lifetime.

Subject is male, mid-thirties, Fire Gate, significant debt. Frost-scars consistent with Amber-level overdraw. Possible threat.

The translation didn't stick. The feeling beneath it—the ache, the pull—was still there.

"What do you see?"

"A mercenary. Frost-scars. Old debt." Her voice was flat. The Silencer's voice.

Orin followed her gaze. He looked at the man for a long moment. His hands, clasped behind his back, remained steady. "Not our target. The Seed is a Water-mage. This one's Fire Gate."

She knew this. She also knew she couldn't stop looking at his hands.

A cargo cart passed between them. When it cleared, the man was gone. The tavern window was empty.

She didn't mention the ache in her chest. She filed it under irrelevant. The file was getting thick.

---

Hark left the tavern. He needed to check the ship—the one the Admiral had promised, the one that left at dawn.

He moved through the crowd, his observational hierarchy scanning automatically. Hands. Exits. Weapons. Faces. A young woman near the hiring post—blonde, cropped hair, small for her age. Her stance was too balanced for a civilian. A fighter. Silencer training, by the weight distribution. He filed her as a potential threat and looked away.

He didn't look at her hands. If he had, he might have seen the faint grey lines on her fingers—the same debt, the same Gate, the same inheritance written in flesh.

Alex watched him pass. She didn't move. Her eyes kept returning to his hands—the scarred hands, the hands that matched her own. She didn't know why. She filed it.

Their eyes almost met. A fishmonger's cart cut between them. The moment fractured.

Hark reached the ship. The crew was loading cargo—crates of dried fish, barrels of salt, coils of rope. The captain was a League woman with a weathered face and a harpoon scar across her jaw. She looked at him the way she might look at a crate that had been loaded without her permission.

"You're the Admiral's passenger. Don't touch anything. Don't talk to my crew. Stay belowdecks until we clear the straits."

Hark nodded. He didn't ask questions. He was good at not asking questions.

Alex watched from the dock. The grey-templed man boarded the ship. She didn't know his name. She didn't know why her hands were trembling. She clasped them behind her back—the same gesture Orin used—and made them still.

"We should report to the local branch," Orin said. "The Storm-mage residue is fresh. Team Four may have engaged someone."

"Yes." She didn't move.

"Alexandria."

She turned. Followed him into the crowd. She didn't look back at the ship.

They passed on the dock. Neither looked back. The storm was already building.
