# Chapter 17 – The Courier's Run

Marta returned on the third day. She was alone.

Renne was in the farmhouse kitchen, changing the dressing on Lina's arm. The wound was healing clean—new skin, pink and tender, the stitches holding. Lina watched Renne's hands with dark, depthless eyes. The drawing of the grey-eyed Silencer was still in Renne's apron pocket. Neither of them had mentioned it.

Footsteps on the path outside. One set. Not two.

Renne's hands stilled on the bandage. The root cellar door. The back window. The stand of trees beyond the yard. She calculated how fast she could get Lina underground.

The door opened. Marta. Alone. Her face was grey with dust and something worse.

"The girl?"

Marta shook her head. Her voice, when it came, was scraped raw. "The safehouse is gone."

Renne guided her to a chair. Marta's knuckles were so swollen she couldn't close her fingers. She had been walking for three days on arthritic joints, and her boots were worn through at the heel, the leather split, the blood showing through. There was blood on her sleeve—not hers. Not fresh.

Renne didn't ask for the story yet. She filled a basin with cold water, knelt, and began cleaning Marta's hands. The water eased the swelling by degrees. Marta let her. The tremor in Renne's fingers steadied as she worked—the old rhythm, the one that knew what to do when she didn't.

Lina watched from her pallet. Her dark eyes tracked Marta's face. She didn't sign. She already knew.

Marta told it in fragments. Renne pieced them together.

The drover's road had been clear. Elias's altered records had held—no Order patrols, no sand-scribes, nothing but the frozen ruts and the grey sky. The silent girl had walked beside Marta the whole way, holding her swollen hand with small, cold fingers. She hadn't spoken. She hadn't needed to.

They reached the Maelstrom League border on the second day. The safehouse was a fishing shack on the northern coast, a waystation for the network, run by a woman named Liese. She had been with Renne's network for eight years. She had hidden seventeen children in that shack—seventeen lives ferried across the League's waters to islands where the Order didn't look.

"She was supposed to be gone," Marta said. Her voice cracked on the last word. "Liese was supposed to have evacuated. The order went out after the waystation fell. But she stayed. There was a child—a Water-mage, barely five. Too scared to travel. Liese wouldn't leave her."

Marta saw the smoke from the ridge. By the time she reached the shack, it was over.

Team Two. The Hammer. Grey Kestrel and the leashed Blood-mage. They had found the safehouse an hour before Marta arrived. The Blood-mage had done what Blood-mages did—the children's bodies were drained, pale, collapsed in on themselves. Liese was by the door. She had tried to block it with her body. The Blood-mage had gone through her.

"The five-year-old," Renne said. Her voice was steady.

"Taken. They leashed her. Kestrel put the collar on himself. She was still crying for Liese when they carried her out."

The silent girl—the one Marta was escorting—had seen the smoke from the ridge. She pulled her hand from Marta's and ran. Not toward the safehouse. Away. Into the scrub. Marta called after her. The girl didn't stop. Didn't look back.

"I looked for her," Marta said. "Two hours. The scrub goes all the way to the cliffs. She could be anywhere. She could be dead. I don't know." Her voice splintered. "I don't know if she's dead or hiding, and I left her there."

Renne's hands kept moving—wiping the blood from Marta's sleeve, the grime from her knuckles. She didn't say it's not your fault. She didn't say you did everything you could. She'd stopped saying things like that years ago. The words didn't help. The work helped.

"You came back. That's what you could do. You came back."

Marta sat in the ashes of the safehouse before she left. She told Renne this part last, her voice flat, emptied out. The charred timbers. The melted glass of the windows. The smell of salt and burnt flesh. Liese's body by the door. The children's bodies inside. She couldn't bury them—the ground was frozen, and she had no tools, and the Order might still be watching the road.

She sat in the ashes and wept. She was sixty-four years old, and she had been carrying messages for Renne's network for a decade, and she had never wept before. Not when her husband was taken. Not when the first safehouse burned. Not ever. She wept because she was tired, and the children were dead, and she couldn't bury them.

Then she stood. She walked three days back to the farmhouse on feet that bled through her boots. She didn't stop. She didn't sleep. She carried the news because someone had to, and she was the one who'd survived.

The letter was gone. She'd lost it in the scrub, or it was still in her coat when she sat in the ashes, or she'd left it at the safehouse door. She couldn't remember. She'd been running.

The farmhouse was quiet. Lina had turned her face to the wall—not sleeping, just not looking. The boy with the healing rib was still in the root cellar, unaware.

Renne finished cleaning Marta's hands. The water in the basin was brown with dust and old blood. She emptied it out the back door, watched it soak into the frozen earth.

She sat at the kitchen table and began the ritual. Left thumb: a child with fever, the first life she'd pulled back from the edge. Index finger: a farmer's crushed hand, bones she'd knitted while her own fingers screamed. Middle knuckle—she pressed until the pain steadied her. The foundling house. The rain. The weight leaving her arms. She pressed harder. The pain was a door she could close.

The sequence continued. Right palm, the burn-scar from a mage-hunter's fire. Ring finger, the wasting sickness. Little finger, the girl with the broken spine who had walked again. Every scar accounted for. Every life paid for in the ledger of her flesh.

Liese was dead. The children were dead. The girl was gone. The five-year-old was leashed. Marta walked three days on broken feet.

She didn't say I should have been faster. She didn't say I should have saved them. The words didn't help. The work helped.

This is what needs doing.

She poured Marta a drink—the last of the good brandy, saved for emergencies. She poured one for herself. She set them on the kitchen table. Neither of them touched the glass.

Marta stared at the brandy. "The girl. The silent one. She was in the root cellar with you for two weeks. You stitched her arm. You taught her to guard."

"I know."

"She ran into the scrub. She didn't look back."

Renne looked at the candle flame. The scrub led to the cliffs. The cliffs had caves. The girl was Sensitive—she might have found one. She might have found nothing. She might be dead at the bottom of a ravine, or hiding in the dark with her hands over her ears, waiting for a sound that meant safety.

She didn't say she might be alive. She didn't say we can find her. She said what she could prove: the terrain existed, the possibility existed.

"Not impossible yet."

Marta didn't answer. She picked up the brandy. Didn't drink. Put it down again.

The candle burned low. Lina's breathing had evened—asleep, finally. Marta's hands were wrapped in clean bandages, the swelling already starting to recede. Renne sat across from her, the hands ritual completed, the scars quiet for now.

The drawing of the grey-eyed Silencer was still in her apron pocket. The dried flower was still there—the one Hark had given her on the last night, pressed flat, sixteen years old. She touched the pocket once, through the fabric. She didn't take either out.

Renne poured Marta a drink. Neither touched it.
