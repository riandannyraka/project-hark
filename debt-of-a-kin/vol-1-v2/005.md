# Chapter 5 – The Seed's First Ally

The tavern's front room was loud with farmers lying about harvests. Renne passed through it like a ghost and found Marta in the back.

She had walked the half-mile from the farmhouse through frozen fields, her breath clouding in the dark, her hands throbbing with the familiar ache of old debt. The children were still hidden. Lina in the loft, the two Sensitives silent in the root cellar, a neighbour woman Renne trusted with her life now keeping watch in the kitchen. Marta had left an hour before dawn to secure the tavern. The message chain needed to be ready before the hunters closed the distance.

The front room was warm and thick with pipe smoke, the voices of farmers arguing over yields and weather and the price of seed grain. Renne navigated by habit, head down, avoiding eye contact. She was known here as a midwife. A quiet woman with scarred hands who could stop a breech birth and set a broken bone and asked no payment beyond what people could spare. No one looked twice.

A farmer's cracked knuckles wrapped around a tankard. The barkeep's quick fingers counting copper coins on the counter. No threat. She catalogued and dismissed.

She found the back room behind a door that stuck in its frame, a small store-chamber that Marta had turned into a private nook years ago. A single oil lamp burned on a shelf crowded with ledgers, their spines cracked, their pages filled with Marta's cramped script. A chair with a sagging cushion. A second chair, wooden, hard. Two clay cups on a crate that served as a table.

Marta was already pouring wine. Her hands were swollen at the knuckles, the joints thick with arthritis, the fingers弯曲 slightly at the middle bones where the disease had begun to twist them. Renne noted it automatically—the healer's eye, the assessment that never shut off. Inflammation. Reduced grip strength. Pain on flexion. There was nothing to be done for it that wouldn't cost Marta more than the relief was worth. Healing magic left scars. Marta already carried enough.

"You look worse than my knees," Marta said, "and I've got fifty years on you."

Renne didn't argue. She sank into the wooden chair, accepted the wine, and didn't drink. Her own hands were trembling again, a fine vibration in the fingers that had been there since the afternoon lesson, since the pain spike, since Marta's warning about the hunters. She clasped them around the clay cup to steady them. The cup was warm. That was something.

"I need you to carry word to Elias," she said. "Team Five will reach the farmhouse within two days. I need his help to move the children further east."

Marta nodded. She was watching Renne with something sharper than concern, her pale eyes moving over Renne's face, her hands, the way she held herself in the chair. "You're running on empty. When was the last time you slept?"

"Before the last time it mattered."

Marta made a sound that wasn't quite a laugh. She set her cup down and leaned back in the sagging chair, and for a moment she was silent, her swollen fingers interlaced on the table, her breath steady but shallow. Renne heard the tightness in her chest, the old grief stored in the body, the way the lungs never quite filled all the way when you'd been carrying something heavy for too long. She knew the sound. She heard it in herself.

"I wasn't always a tavern keeper," Marta said.

Renne waited.

"My husband was a cartographer. A good one. He mapped the trade routes through the Green Vein, the old roads, the ones that don't appear on Order charts." Marta's voice was flat, the words delivered without inflection, as if she were reading from one of her ledgers. "He drew a route through the Unmapped Edges. A pass that cut three days off the journey from the Confederacy to the Archipelago. It wasn't a secret. It was just... undocumented. The Order didn't like that."

She picked up her cup again. Her fingers gripped it hard, the swollen knuckles whitening.

"They came for him in the night. No warning. No trial. Just a ledger entry." She closed her eyes for a moment, reciting from memory. "'Terminated. Reason: Threat to Order Sanctioned Cartography.' He drew a map. That was his crime. They killed him for drawing a map."

Renne's hands tightened on her own cup. She didn't speak. There was nothing to say. The Order had taken her own losses, her own ledger of grief, and she had learned long ago that words didn't fill the spaces the dead left behind.

"I wasn't afraid then," Marta said. She opened her eyes. "I was too angry to be afraid. I'm not afraid now. I'm just tired." She lifted her cup, took a long drink, and set it down with a soft thud. "But I'll run your messages until my legs give out. It's the only thing I can do that they can't erase."

Renne looked at the lamp flame. It burned steady and small, a tongue of gold in the dim room. The ledgers on the shelf behind Marta's head held years of records—shipments, payments, names and dates and places. A network built on ink and stubbornness.

"Elias needs to alter the land-debt records for the eastern path," she said. Her voice was steadier now, the practical details forcing a structure onto the night. "If the soil signatures along the evacuation route read as fallow—abandoned for a decade—the Order's Savants might overlook it. They'll read the land for magical residue. If the records suggest nothing's been there, they may not look deeper."

Marta was memorising. She never wrote anything down. The details passed into her mind and settled there, filed alongside a hundred other messages she'd carried over the years. "If I'm caught, there's nothing to find."

"You leave at first light. The Order patrols are thinner on the old drover's road."

"And you'll be here, waiting for the hunters."

Renne didn't answer. The lamp flame bent in a draft she couldn't feel.

Marta set down her wine. She reached across the crate and laid her swollen hand over both of Renne's trembling ones. The touch was warm, steady—rough knuckles, dry skin, the pulse in her wrist a slow and even beat. Renne's body catalogued it before her mind caught up. The temperature of Marta's skin. The slight rasp of her palm. The way the contact grounded something that had been drifting loose in Renne's chest since the afternoon, since the lesson, since the flash of memory she hadn't let herself complete.

Her healer's instinct stirred—check the pulse, check the breathing, tend and mend and hold. But this wasn't a patient. This was a friend. And the touch was not a healing. It was a gift.

Renne's eyes stung. She blinked once, hard. The word she couldn't say hovered unformed, the one she'd forbidden herself years ago, the one that felt too much like asking for something she had no right to claim. She replaced it with the familiar wall, the words she'd built brick by brick in the years since the Cradle, since the rain, since the weight leaving her arms.

This is what needs doing.

Marta didn't move her hand. "You need to sleep, girl. I'll watch the road tonight."

Renne didn't promise. She didn't argue. She let her eyes rest, the lamp flame a soft glow through her closed lids, Marta's hand a steady weight over her own. The distant noise of the tavern washed over her—laughter, the scrape of a chair, a voice raised in good-natured argument. The wine sat warm in her stomach. Her hands stopped trembling.

She did not sleep. But she rested, and that was closer to peace than she had been in years.

The oil lamp burned low. Marta withdrew her hand only to refill Renne's cup, then resumed her vigil, her swollen fingers curled loosely on the table. The ledgers watched from their shelf. The night pressed against the tavern walls, and somewhere in the dark, hunters were riding closer, and children were sleeping in a root cellar, and a network of tired old women and frightened young ones was holding its breath.

Renne drank the wine. It was sour and warm. The dried flower on her farmhouse windowsill felt very far away, a small beautiful thing she might not see again. She let the thought pass without holding it.

Marta's hand stayed where it was, covering hers, until the lamp burned dry.
