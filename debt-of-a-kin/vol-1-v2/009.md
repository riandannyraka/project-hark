# Chapter 9 – The Network's Blood

The pounding on the door came in the hour before dawn, when the body is slowest and the blood is coldest. Renne was already awake.

She had been awake for two nights. The lamp on the kitchen table had burned through three fills of oil, and her hands had moved through the hours—checking the bar on the door, checking the shutters, checking the children's breathing in the root cellar below. The neighbour woman had gone home at midnight. Renne had sent her with a jar of honey and a lie about being fine.

The pounding came again. Three strikes. A pause. Two more. The signal.

She crossed the kitchen in four strides, the knife in her hand—kitchen steel, dull from cutting bread and trimming herbs, but weighted and solid. She lifted the bar and pulled the door open.

Lina fell through it.

The girl was small and dripping, her clothes soaked from the stream, her hair plastered to her skull. Behind her, two children Renne had never seen before—a boy and a girl, both younger than Lina, both bleeding from scratches and brambles, both silent. Lina's fingers were clenched around something crushed and damp. The children's hands were empty, clutching each other.

Renne pulled Lina inside, then the others. She kicked the door shut and dropped the bar. The shutters were already closed. No light escaped.

In the lamplight, the blood was visible. It was not theirs—not all of it. Lina's sleeves were soaked to the elbow, dark and sticky, the fabric clinging to her forearms. The girl's dress was spattered across the front. The boy had a gash on his forehead, but the blood there was his own, already clotting.

Renne's hands found Lina's shoulders, checked her pupils, her pulse, the rhythm of her breath. Ragged. Too fast. But her eyes were clear. She was not in shock. She was in the aftermath of something worse.

Lina's hands moved. The crude sign language Renne had taught her over years of silence—a vocabulary of survival, built word by word in this kitchen. *Hunters. Dogs. Found the safehouse. Marta's cousin. Dead.*

Renne's stomach dropped. Marta's cousin ran a waystation two villages over. Three children hidden there.

"The children?"

Lina's hands: *Two escaped. One taken.*

Renne didn't ask which one. The living needed her hands.

She cleared the kitchen table. The salt crock went to the floor. The bread board followed. She spread a boiled sheet across the wood, the linen still faintly warm from the hearth, and turned to the children.

The boy first. His breathing was shallow, catching on every inhale, his lips pale. She pressed her fingers along his ribs and felt the give—a crack, not a break, the bone still holding but compromised. Possible punctured lung. First priority.

Lina second. Lacerations on her arms from running through brambles, the cuts long but superficial. The blood on her sleeves was someone else's.

The girl last. Shock, scratches, no major wounds. Her pupils were equal, her pulse thready but present. Last priority.

Renne lifted the boy onto the table. No magic. A healing working would flare bright as a signal fire—any Savant within a mile would sense it, and the hunters were closer than a mile now. Pure field medicine. Boiled water. Clean linen. A curved needle and gut-thread from the kit she kept in the cupboard, the one she'd assembled years ago for the nights when magic was a luxury she couldn't afford.

"You're doing well," she said. Her voice was low, steady, the voice she used for frightened children and wounded animals and her own reflection on bad nights. "Keep breathing. The rib is cracked, not broken through. I'm going to bind it tight. It will hurt."

She threaded the needle. Her hands were not trembling. When there was work, the body obeyed.

"You've already done the hard part. You ran. You survived. That's the hard part."

The needle dipped. The boy gasped, a sharp inhale that caught on the pain. Renne's fingers found the next point of entry, pulled the thread through, tied it off. The stitches were small and even. Close the wound. Stop the bleeding. Buy time.

She listened to his lungs between each suture. The wheeze lessened with the binding, the rib stabilised, the breath coming easier. No puncture. He would live.

She bound his ribs with linen strips, winding the cloth around his chest in tight, even layers. He whimpered once—a small, animal sound—and then went quiet. His eyes found hers. She held his gaze for three seconds, nodded once, and moved on.

Lina next. Renne cleaned the scratches on her arms with boiled water and a clean cloth. The cuts were superficial, bramble-thorns, already starting to close. The blood on Lina's sleeves was dark and thickening, not hers. Renne didn't ask whose. Not yet.

The girl last. Pupils checked—equal, reactive. Pulse taken—thready but steady. Renne wrapped her in a blanket and set her by the cold hearth with a cup of water. The girl didn't drink. She held the cup in both hands and stared at the barred door.

Three alive. One taken. The network was bleeding. This is what needs doing.

When the children were settled—the boy on a pallet by the hearth, the girl still holding her cup, her eyes fixed on nothing—Lina tugged Renne's sleeve. She held out the crushed paper.

Renne smoothed it on the table. The paper was damp, smeared in places, the edges torn where Lina had ripped it from a larger sheet. Charcoal lines—she must have grabbed a stick from the waystation hearth before she ran, must have drawn this in the dark while the hunters were still in the house, while Marta's cousin was still dying.

The figure was young, female, hair cropped short. The face was smudged, the jaw and mouth rendered in quick, impatient strokes. But the eyes were different. Ash mixed with something wet, dabbed on with a fingertip, a pale silver-grey that caught the lamplight and held it. Lina had wanted those eyes seen.

Behind the girl: a shape in robes. An old man, stooped, hands clasped. A Silencer and her Savant.

Renne's hands stopped.

The tremor came back, fine and fast, running through her fingers. She didn't know this girl. She had never seen her face. But the grey eyes—ash-grey, deliberate, catching the light—Renne knew that shade. She saw it in her own reflection. She saw it in the river memory, the one she didn't let herself name, the one that belonged to a person she hadn't spoken of in fifteen years.

The same grey. The same—

The word would not come. It never did.

Her hands ached. The scars along her knuckles pulled tight. She began the ritual without realising it. Left thumb: a child with fever, the first life she'd pulled back from the edge. Index finger: a farmer's crushed hand, bones she'd knitted while her own fingers screamed. Middle knuckle—

She stopped.

The foundling house. The rain. The weight leaving her arms. The word she had spoken once and never again.

She pulled her hand back. The ritual remained unfinished, the middle knuckle untouched, the scar white and tight in the lamplight.

She folded the drawing. Carefully. Precisely. Once along the centre, then again, until it was a small square of paper that fit in her apron pocket. It settled next to the dried flower—a small brittle weight against her thigh.

Lina watched. Her eyes were dark, depthless. She saw more than she should.

"Who is she?"

Lina shook her head. She only drew what came.

"Is she coming here?"

Lina's hands moved: *Soon. Not tonight. But soon.*

Renne nodded. She didn't know what the drawing meant.

The children slept. Lina curled on a pallet near the boy, one hand reaching toward him even in rest, her fingers twitching with whatever she was dreaming. The girl had finally drunk her water. The cup sat empty on the hearth.

Renne sat at the kitchen table. The lamp burned low, the oil running thin. She completed the ritual. Left thumb. Index finger. Middle knuckle—pressed until the pain steadied her. Right palm, the burn-scar from a mage-hunter's fire. Ring finger, the wasting sickness that had taken everything she had. Little finger, the girl with the broken spine who had walked again.

Each scar a name. Each name a life.

The dried flower was in her hand. She didn't remember taking it from her apron. The petals were crumbling at the edges, blue-grey dust on her palm. She held it without looking.

Outside, the wind picked up. The farmhouse creaked. Somewhere in the dark, hunters were moving—five teams, closing like fingers around a throat. The children breathed. The drawing sat in her pocket. The flower crumbled in her hand.

She didn't know why her hands shook worse after looking at it.
