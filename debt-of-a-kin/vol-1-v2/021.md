# Chapter 21 – The Safehouse Under Siege

The feeling of being watched was different from the feeling of being hunted. Hunters moved. Watchers waited. The watchers had been at the treeline since dusk, and they hadn't moved at all.

Renne stood at the kitchen window, the candle behind her shielded so her silhouette wouldn't show against the glass. She didn't need to see them. She could feel them in the wrongness of the stillness—the birds had stopped, the wind had shifted, the quality of silence outside the farmhouse had the texture of held breath.

She ran the escape routes in her mind. The root cellar tunnel led to the dry creek bed, then to the old drover's road—the one Elias had altered the records for, the one Marta had taken with the silent girl. The tunnel was narrow, cold, and Lina would have to crawl part of the way with her healing arm. The boy's ribs would scream. But they'd fit. They'd make it.

She counted the watchers by the gaps in the treeline—the places where the shadow was too dense, too regular. Three, maybe four. Savants, not Silencers. Silencers would have moved by now. These ones were patient. They were reading. Cataloguing. Waiting for something.

They're not here to kill me. If they wanted me dead, they would have burned the farmhouse an hour ago. They're here to confirm. To read my debt, to map the network, to wait for the other teams to close the net. I'm the bait. The children are the catch.

She checked the tunnel entrance, counted the supplies, steadied the tremor in her hands. This is what needs doing.

She moved to the root cellar. Lina was awake, her dark eyes already on the cellar door. She knew. She always knew. The boy was asleep on his pallet—his breathing deep, the cracked rib healing, but not fast enough. He wouldn't be able to run if the Order breached the house.

Marta was already there, her swollen hands wrapped around a walking stick. She'd been ready since she returned from the burned safehouse—three days of walking on broken feet, and she was still ready. Renne didn't thank her. She didn't need to.

She knelt beside Lina. "The tunnel leads to the creek bed. Follow it until you reach the old drover's road. Marta knows the way. Don't stop. Don't look back. Don't use magic—they'll read it."

Lina's hands moved: *You're not coming.*

"They're after me. If I stay, they stay. If I run with you, they follow. That's the cost."

*Wrong cost.*

Renne almost smiled. Almost. "It's the only sum I know."

Lina reached into her tunic and pulled out a crumpled paper—a new drawing, done in the dark of the root cellar while Renne was watching the treeline. She pressed it into Renne's hand.

Renne smoothed it open. Charcoal and ash, smeared but legible. Two figures: a woman with scarred hands, kneeling. A girl with cropped hair, standing apart, her back half-turned. Between them, a gap—a space that might be distance, or might be years, or might be the shape of a word neither of them could say. The woman was crying. The girl was not.

Renne's hands trembled. She folded the drawing carefully, precisely, and tucked it into her apron pocket—next to the first drawing of the grey-eyed Silencer, next to the dried flower. The pocket was getting crowded.

"Go. Now."

*I'll see you again.*

Renne didn't answer. She couldn't promise that. She could only promise that she'd try.

Marta helped the boy to his feet—he was groggy, wincing, but he didn't complain. Lina went first, her healing arm cradled against her chest. Marta followed, her walking stick scraping the stone. The boy last, his shallow breathing echoing in the narrow dark.

Renne waited until the sounds faded. Then she dragged a salt barrel over the tunnel entrance and scattered loose earth across the floor. If the Savants searched the cellar, they'd find the tunnel. But they'd have to look first. That was all she was buying. Time. Not safety. Time.

The farmhouse was silent. The candle had burned low. She sat at the kitchen table and began the ritual.

Left thumb: the first patient, a child with fever, the life she'd pulled back from the edge. Index finger: a farmer's crushed hand, bones she'd knitted while her own fingers screamed. Middle knuckle: the foundling house. She pressed until the pain steadied her. The rain. The weight leaving her arms. The word she had spoken once and never again.

She pressed harder. The sequence continued. Right palm, the burn-scar from a mage-hunter's fire. Ring finger, the wasting sickness. Little finger, the girl with the broken spine who had walked again. Liese, dead at the safehouse door. The waystation children, drained and pale. The silent girl, lost in the scrub. The five-year-old Water-mage with a collar around her throat. The grey-eyed Silencer in the drawing whose face she had never seen clearly but whose shape she carried in her pocket. Hark, somewhere in the dark, walking toward her or away from her, and the flower he gave her on the last night, and the word he couldn't say.

She touched the middle knuckle one more time. Then she folded her hands in her lap and waited.

Outside, the wind was rising. The storm that had been following Hark was settling over the valley—a low, circular pressure, heavy with unshed rain. The candle guttered. The shutters creaked. Renne felt the weather in her bones, though she didn't know its source. She only knew it felt wrong—too deliberate, too patient, like something waiting for permission to break.

She touched her apron pocket. The flower. The drawings. The weight of paper and petals and fifteen years of silence.

She was alone now. Candle flickered. Wind rose.
