# Chapter 7 – The Soil Savant

The farm was dying. Hark could see it in the colour of the soil before he saw the man who owned it.

He had walked two days through the Grain-Lords' Confederacy, the frozen trade road giving way to flat plains and enormous sky. The land here was grey where it should have been black, leached of whatever had once made it rich. The furrows in the fields ran straight and empty. A barn slumped at one corner, its roof patched with mismatched planks, its shadow long in the overcast light. A woodsman's axe sat in a stump near the door, rusted along the blade. A rake leaned against the barn wall, its handle worn smooth from years of gripping.

His left forearm pulled with each step. The cut was shallow, a blade's kiss from the caravan skirmish, now half-healed and itching under the linen wrap. He had stitched it himself with the dead archer's needle and gut-thread, sitting against a tree at dusk while the light held. Joel had taught him to sew skin before he'd taught him to cut it. The stitches were tight, neat, the wound closing clean. He checked it once a day, changed the linen when the old dressing stiffened. The needle lived in his coat pocket now, a small weight against his ribs.

The farmhouse door opened. An old man stepped out.

Hark read the hands first. Knotted knuckles, swollen joints, fingers slightly crooked. Bone brittleness. Debt written in calcium loss. The body paying for something the man had done for years. Exits: the farmhouse door behind him, open fields in all directions, the barn. No cover. No threat. Weapons: the axe in the stump, rusted, not a combat blade. A belt knife, sheathed, leather cracked with age. Faces last. The old man's eyes were tired but not afraid. He looked at Hark the way a farmer looked at weather.

"You're the one Marta sent word about," the old man said. "The mercenary."

"I need the Seed's location."

The old man's gaze dropped to Hark's hands. The gloves were off. The frost-lines ran white across his knuckles, threading up toward his wrists, two decades of debt mapped in skin. The old man studied them for a long moment. Then his eyes moved to Hark's face.

"You've killed mages."

Hark didn't confirm or deny. "The location."

"Come inside. The sky's too big out here for this conversation."

---

The kitchen was sparse. A table, two chairs, a cold hearth. Hark took the chair with its back to the wall. One door, two windows. Counted.

The old man sat opposite. His name was Elias. He didn't offer food or drink, and Hark didn't ask. They sat in the grey light from the windows, the silence settling between them.

Elias studied him. Not the way a farmer studied weather. The way a Savant studied debt. His eyes moved over Hark's hands, his chest, the air around him, reading signatures Hark couldn't feel.

"You're carrying old debt. Amber. Fire Gate." A pause. His head tilted slightly. "No. Light mutation. Dormant now. You haven't opened that gate in years. The frost-scars on your hands are two decades deep at least."

Hark's chest tightened. His pulse kicked once, hard, against his ribs.

"The Order fears you more than they fear her."

The words landed. Hark's hand stayed on the table. He did not reach for his knife.

He reads debt. Like the ones in the Cradle. The ones who catalogued Lea's lungs and called it a ledger entry. Grey eyes. A small vortex spinning between their palms. The bolt's yellow light. His jaw tightened.

"The location," Hark said. His voice was flat. "You've named my scars. That's not payment."

Elias held his gaze for a moment longer. Then he pushed a folded paper across the table.

"She's at a farmhouse clinic, two days east. Near a stand of old-growth forest. She's expecting Marta, not you."

Hark took the paper. The ink was cramped but legible. Directions, landmarks, a name. The Seed. He folded it and tucked it inside his coat.

"Why do you help her?"

Elias didn't answer immediately. He rose, stiffly, his knotted hands braced on the table for support, and crossed to a shelf on the far wall. He took down a wooden box, plain and unvarnished, and set it on the table between them. He opened it.

A child's clay bird, crude, the thumbprint still pressed into the wing where a small hand had shaped it. A folded page of Order parchment, the Cradle seal still visible along the edge.

Elias unfolded the page. The script was precise, Savant-trained, the ink faded but sharp.

"My son. Twelve years old. Soil-sensitive. The Order came when he was seven. I fought them. I lost."

He tapped the entry with a swollen finger.

"They didn't list his name. Just a designation. 'Terminated. Reason: Resistance.'"

Hark looked at the page. Then at the clay bird. The thumbprint. His jaw clenched.

"Twelve."

Elias refolded the page. His hands were steady despite the swollen joints. "Renne saved three children the year after they took my son. I can't bring my boy back. But I can make sure his ledger entry has company in the 'Resistance' column."

He closed the box. The clay bird disappeared inside it. The thumbprint last.

Hark said: "I'll kill them for you. The ones who come for her."

Elias looked at him. The old man's eyes were tired but sharp. "You don't even know why you're doing this, do you?"

Hark didn't answer. He stood.

---

Outside, the grey fields stretched to the horizon. The sky was still too big. Hark paused at the edge of the yard, adjusting his coat. The stitches pulled in his forearm.

Elias followed him out. The old man's breath clouded in the cold air.

"When you find her, tell her the eastern path is clear. I've altered the records."

Hark nodded once.

A clean line of pale sun fell across the weathered wood of the barn. He looked away.

Elias noticed the bandage on his forearm. The linen was clean but worn, the edge of the wrap visible below Hark's sleeve. "That's fresh."

"It's closed."

"You stitch your own wounds."

"No one else will."

Elias nodded.

Hark turned and walked. The track led east, a thin line of packed earth through the grey fields. He didn't look back. The needle in his coat pocket was the archer's. The stitches in his arm were his own. The message in his coat was for a woman whose hands he remembered before her face, whose name he still couldn't speak.

The clay bird's thumbprint stayed behind his eyes. He didn't blink it away.
