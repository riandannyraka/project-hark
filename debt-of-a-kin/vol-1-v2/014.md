# Chapter 14 – The Healer's Dilemma

The morning had been for stitches and lies. The evening was for arithmetic that didn't add up.

Renne sat beside Lina's pallet in the farmhouse kitchen, the girl's arm cradled in her lap, the bandage unwound. The wound was healing clean. The stitches had held through two nights of restlessness—Lina thrashed in her sleep, fighting things she couldn't sign—and the skin around the sutures was pink but not inflamed. No infection. No drainage. Renne pressed two fingers to the inside of Lina's wrist and counted. Steady. No rattle in the chest. The ribs were fine. The scratches on her arms were scabbing, the dark lines of bramble-thorn already fading.

Lina was awake. Her dark eyes tracked Renne's hands as they worked—the wound checked, the dressing changed, the bandage rewound in tight, even layers. She didn't sign. She didn't need to.

"You should sleep," Renne said.

Lina shook her head. Her hands moved, the crude sign language shaped by years of silence: *The girl with grey eyes is coming.*

Renne didn't answer. She tucked the blanket around Lina's shoulders and stood.

The root cellar was dark and cold, the candle stub on the bottom step burning low. The boy was asleep on a pallet of straw and old blankets, his breathing deeper than it had been the night before. The binding on his ribs was holding. The girl—the one who still didn't speak, who still held her water cup with both hands—was curled beside him, her eyes closed, her chest rising and falling in a rhythm that was almost peaceful. She had finally started drinking.

Renne left the candle burning. Enough light to keep the dark from swallowing them. Not enough to show under the door.

Back in the kitchen, she paused at the windowsill. The dried flower was gone—moved to her apron weeks ago, now a small brittle weight against the folded drawing. The sill was bare. A water ring marked where the clay pot had sat for fifteen years, a pale circle on the wood, and a scatter of dead petals she hadn't swept away. The moonlight through the window was clean and white, catching the ring, making the old wood seem new. She looked at it for a long moment. Then she looked away.

The night was too quiet.

The back door opened without knocking.

Marta came through it like a storm that had already spent itself—breath short, her arthritic hands trembling on the doorframe, her face grey. Renne caught her elbow, guided her to a chair. The older woman's knuckles were swollen, the joints inflamed, the skin stretched tight over bone. She had been gripping something hard—a walking stick, or a weapon. There was blood under her fingernails. Not hers.

"Who?" Renne said.

"Team Five. Quiet Death. They found the waystation."

Renne's hands stopped on Marta's wrist.

Marta's voice was flat. The waystation had been hit at dawn. Calen and Ellera—the Silence-mage and the Ash-mage. They didn't ask questions. They didn't offer terms. The cousin was dead. Two children dead. One taken.

"I got there an hour after," Marta said. "The ash was still warm. They took the youngest—a boy, maybe six. Fire-sensitive. They'll leash him."

Her voice cracked on the last word.

Renne's hands started moving before she told them to. Left thumb: a child with fever, the first life she'd pulled back from the edge. Index finger: a farmer's crushed hand, bones she'd knitted while her own fingers screamed. Middle knuckle—

She stopped. Pressed until the pain steadied her.

Three dead. One taken. The network was bleeding faster than I can stitch it.

Marta had more. Not just Team 5. There were reports of another team in the Confederacy—Team 2, the Hammer. Grey Kestrel and a leashed Blood-mage. Rumour said the Blood-mage went rogue, killed his Silencer. Unconfirmed. Marta wasn't counting them out until she saw a body. They were coming from the east.

Team 3, the Ledger-Keepers. Someone matching their description had been seen making inquiries at a village two days south.

Team 4, the Storm Front. A Storm-mage incident had been reported, but nothing confirmed. No one had seen them in the Confederacy.

"Four teams accounted for," Marta said. "But the fifth team—no one's seen them. No ash, no blood, no storm, no ledger inquiries. They're either not here yet, or they're better than the others."

Renne's hands stilled on the candle. "That's worse."

"Much worse."

"They're closing from all sides," Marta continued. "They're not hunting randomly anymore. They know the safehouse is somewhere in this valley. They're narrowing."

Four teams converging. One invisible. No word from Elias—the eastern path might be compromised. No word from anyone. The network was fraying at the edges, threads snapping one by one.

"We need to move," Renne said. "But Lina can't travel yet. The boy's rib is still knitting. If I move them now, the boy's lung could collapse. Lina's arm will tear open on the road."

"So we stay."

"So I stay. You take the girl. Tonight. The boy and Lina stay with me until they can walk."

Marta started to argue. Renne cut her off—not harsh, just certain.

"I'm the one they want. If I stay, they'll focus here. You can get the girl out while they're watching the farmhouse."

Marta's jaw tightened.

Renne sat at the kitchen table. The candle was burning low. She wrote in code—the network's cipher, a mix of herbal terms and weather patterns that read like a midwife's supply list to anyone who didn't know the key. Lavender for Team 5. Rosemary for Team 2. A frost warning for the eastern path. If the new moon came without a supply order, the garden was lost. Burn the eastern beds. Move the seedlings south.

This is what needs doing.

Marta took the letter. She would leave before dawn, take the girl through the old drover's road—the one Elias had altered the records for. If the records held, the Order wouldn't look there. If they didn't, Marta would die, and the girl would die, and the letter would never reach Elias.

At the door, Marta paused. Looked back at Renne—the healer at the table, the candle guttering, the tremor in her hands barely visible.

"You're not planning to leave, are you?"

"I'm planning to buy you time."

Something passed through Marta's face. She turned and walked into the dark, the girl's small hand in hers, the letter tucked inside her coat.

The door closed. The bar dropped. Renne was alone with the sleeping children and the candle and the quiet.

She lay on her pallet, staring at the ceiling beams. The house creaked—wind, or something else. She didn't get up to check. She hadn't slept more than a few hours at a time since the night Lina came back, and her body was running on the memory of rest. She closed her eyes.

The ravine.

Sixteen years ago. A narrow cut in the earth outside a town called Ketterhollow, where the Crown of Winter's foothills gave way to the Grain-Lords' plains. A hot spring trickle threading through the rocks, steam rising in the cold air, the smell of wet stone and minerals. A boy half-frozen on the bank, feverish, his hands already scarred—frost-lines on his knuckles, new then, still forming, white on reddened skin. She'd pulled him out of the ravine while he was unconscious, dragged him to a cottage, and waited for him to wake.

He'd looked at her like she was a trap and he was too tired to care.

She'd offered her hand. Eventually, he'd taken it.

They'd spent four months in that cottage. She'd taught him to skip stones in the hot spring pools—he'd been terrible at it, all wrist and no patience, the rocks sinking every time. He'd taught her to guard her reservoir, the Cradle technique, the one that had kept him alive. She'd asked about the scars on his hands. He'd told her the ledger. She'd asked if it hurt. He'd said always. They'd shared meals and silences and a bed that was too small for two people who didn't know how to sleep next to someone else.

He'd almost said he wanted to stay. Almost. The word had been on his face, in the way his hands had stopped shaking when she touched them, in the way he'd looked at her across the fire on the last night before he left. She'd seen it. She'd waited. He hadn't spoken.

She didn't replay the ending. She replayed the beginning: the ravine, the steam, the way his hands had stopped shaking when she touched them.

*He's still alive. I don't know how I know. But he is.*

She didn't know where. She only knew—with the certainty of someone who had carried a dried flower for sixteen years—that he hadn't stopped walking.

Before sleep took her, her mind returned to the fifth team. The invisible one. No ash, no blood, no storm. Moving through the net without leaving a trace.

The grey-eyed Silencer in Lina's drawing. The girl with the cropped hair and the steady hands.

What if the fifth team is one person? What if she's already here?

She didn't answer herself. The candle had burned out. The moonlight on the water ring had moved. The house creaked again, and this time she didn't tell herself it was the wind.

She rose. Lit a fresh candle. Set it on the bare windowsill where the dried flower used to be. The flame was small, steady.

She lit a candle. She waited.
