# Chapter 12 – The Sun-Scorched Expanse

The desert was the first place he'd ever felt genuinely invisible.

The scrub gave way to sand an hour after dawn, the last twisted oaks surrendering to a horizon that was all heat-shimmer and pale stone. The air dried to something that felt like it had been baked and left to cool for a thousand years. Hark walked alone, his jaw still throbbing from the Iron Rib woman's fist, his throat tender where her hands had pressed. The bruise had spread overnight, a dark bloom across his windpipe. It hurt to swallow. He swallowed anyway.

The Dominion was not the Hold-Kingdoms. There were no toll gates, no lord's halls, no pretense of hospitality. The desert was the gate. If you survived it, you were permitted.

The sand stretched empty to the horizon. A lizard sunned itself on a rock, its tiny fingers splayed, its throat pulsing with heat. No hands to read. Exits everywhere and nowhere—no cover, no choke points, no blind corners. If someone followed, he'd see them an hour before they reached him. If someone was already ahead, same. Weapons: his knife, the secondary blade, the needle in his pocket. Faces: none. The sun was a white hole in the sky, and he didn't look at it.

Midday. A patrol crested a dune.

Three riders in light robes, their faces wrapped against the sun. Not Order. Temple-trained. The golden eye of the Sun-Pharaoh's sigil glinted at their throats—a slit-pupilled gaze that seemed to watch even from embroidered thread.

Sand-scribes. Joel's voice surfaced, a lesson from years ago, delivered in a tent with a dying man's rasp: *Order Savants read debt. Dominion scribes read heat. Same gift, different master. The scribes don't answer to the Cradle. They answer to the priesthood. That makes them less thorough but more dangerous—they don't have to file reports before they kill you.*

The scribes scanned him. Hark felt nothing—he never did—but he watched their eyes unfocus, the way a Savant's did when reading debt. The lead scribe's gaze passed over him, registered nothing, moved on. An Insensitive. A void. Not worth stopping.

The patrol rode past. Hark didn't exhale. He didn't need to.

One advantage to burning out your reservoir when you're ten. The desert reads mages. I read as nothing.

---

Late afternoon. A waystation huddled against a rock outcropping—a stone building, low and square, its walls whitewashed against the heat. A well. A handful of date palms. A merchant's tent pitched in the shade, its canvas stirring in the dry wind.

The waystationer was old, leather-skinned, a Dominion native whose hands were busy sorting clay beads into a wooden tray. Hark read him in the space of a doorway: hands occupied, non-threatening. Exits: open desert, well, tent flap. Weapons: a curved knife at the merchant's belt, the sheath studded with coloured glass. Ornamental.

Hark needed water. He had coin. The waystationer glanced at the copper pieces in Hark's palm and waved them off.

"Coin's no good here. Scribes tax the coin. I trade in favours now."

Hark waited.

The waystationer's eyes moved over him—not the unfocused gaze of a debt-reader, but something older, cannier. A man who had survived the desert by knowing what to look for.

"You're carrying something old. Debt that's gone cold. But you've got fragments on you. I can smell them."

Hark's jaw tightened. Two fragments remaining. He reached into his belt pouch, pulled one out, tossed it onto the mat.

The waystationer held it to the light. The fragment was smooth, pale, the size of a thumb-joint. It caught the sun and threw it back in a colourless glint.

"You know how to use these?"

"No."

"Want me to show you?"

Hark said nothing.

The waystationer gestured toward the back room. A child peeked out—a girl, maybe seven, barefoot, dark-eyed. The air around her carried a faint charge, the kind Hark remembered from the Cradle dormitories, from the training halls, from the cot next to his. Sensitive. Low-level. Unregistered.

The girl's feet were bare. Lea's had been bare too, in the Cradle dormitory, cold on the stone floor. She'd complained about it every night until she stopped complaining about anything.

"The sand-scribes sweep this post twice a month," the waystationer said. "She hides in the well when they come. But the well doesn't mask her debt—it just muffles it. A close sweep, they'd find her."

He pressed the fragment to the doorframe. Applied pressure.

The stone sighed. A fine line spidered across its surface, a colourless pulse spreading outward like heat haze without the heat. The air around the girl went still. The faint charge Hark had felt—the prickling awareness of untrained magic—faded to nothing.

"One sweep. That's all it buys. But one sweep is enough."

Hark watched the girl. She was still barefoot. Still Sensitive. For the next hour, the sand-scribes would see nothing.

Fragment buys time. Not safety. Time.

Someone had spent a life-debt to make this stone, and now it was being cracked to buy an hour for a child no one would ever know about.

A plume of dust rose on the horizon. Riders. Order patrol—four, maybe five, with a Savant in grey robes at the centre.

The waystationer moved fast. The girl vanished into the well, a brief splash and then silence. Hark stayed where he was. Running marked a man. He sat in the shade of the date palms, drew his knife and the oilstone. Three strokes per side. Check edge. Repeat. The rhythm was a quiet wall against the sound of approaching hooves.

The patrol swept through. The Savant's eyes unfocused, scanning the waystation's debt-residue. Hark watched without appearing to watch. The fragment was working. The girl was White. Hark was void. The Savant's gaze passed over him without pausing—a man sitting in the shade, sharpening a blade, nothing more.

The patrol rode on.

The girl climbed out of the well, dripping, silent. The waystationer exhaled. His hands were shaking. He pressed them flat against his thighs and held them there.

Hark took his water, his supplies. He didn't say goodbye. He walked deeper into the desert.

---

Night fell fast. The heat bled out of the sand, and the cold rushed in—a dry, clear cold, sharper than the Crown of Winter's snow. Hark camped at a rock outcropping, his back to the stone, the desert open in all directions. The stars were too bright. He didn't look at them.

He inventoried his body. Jaw still sore. Throat tender. Shoulder aching from the alley, the old injury a low constant. Frost-scars throbbing in the cold. One fragment remaining. The needle in his pocket. The paper from Elias.

He didn't think about Renne. He thought about the miles left to cross. The desert was wide. The Seed was somewhere beyond it. He would reach her or he wouldn't.

Movement at the base of the outcropping.

A shape, darker than the shadow. Human.

Hark approached, knife ready. The shape resolved: a man, young—maybe twenty, twenty-two. His lips were cracked, his eyes sunken, the skin around them pulled tight as a drum. His hands were fissured, the cracks running deep into the flesh, not from heat but from within. A Water-mage who had been drawing on his internal reservoir too long, desiccating himself from the inside out.

The mage saw Hark. His mouth moved. The word was *water*.

Hark assessed. The mage was Amber debt, possibly Red. He had been running. From the Order, from the desert, from himself. He would not survive the night. Water would buy him hours, not days.

Hark had one waterskin. The arithmetic was simple.

He didn't share.

The mage begged. His voice cracked on the single word, broke into a whisper, broke into nothing. Hark stood there. He counted exits. Counted breaths. Counted the seconds until the begging stopped.

Arithmetic. Not cruelty. Arithmetic.

The mage died.

The mage was young. Younger than Hark had been when Lea died. He'd been ten. Lea had been eleven. He'd held her hand while she bled out. He hadn't held this man's hand. That was the difference. That was all.

He knelt. Took the dead mage's debt-null fragments—two more, small, unremarkable, their smooth weight familiar in his palm. He slipped them into his belt pouch. Stood.

The mage's reservoir collapsed.

A Water-mage's death meant moisture—the last fluid in his body, the last borrowed water—bleeding back into the stone. The sudden temperature shift as moisture met heat-saturated rock sent a thermal spike directly beneath Hark's feet.

His Fire Gate resonated.

Not his choice. A reflex from a decade of Cradle training, the channel in his frost-scars activating before his conscious mind could clamp it. His fingers warmed. The scars pulsed faintly, a dull orange glow beneath his gloves. The stone beneath his boots hissed—steam, barely visible, instantly gone.

He shut it down. Pressed his gloved hands to the cold sand. The glow died. The frost-scars ached, a new thread of cold winding toward his wrist.

That was a mistake. Catalogued. Don't repeat.

He walked on. He didn't look back at the body.

---

Vyktor felt the thermal anomaly before his instruments did.

A heat-signature from a stone source, at night, where no heat should be. To an ordinary mage or sand-scribe, it would read as geothermal noise—the desert's bones shifting, nothing more. To Vyktor, whose Storm mutation made him exquisitely sensitive to thermal differentials, it read as something that did not belong.

He had been tracking a ghost with frost-scar debt and a dormant Fire Gate for two days.

He just found him.
