# Chapter 2 – The Scalpel

The dummy had no hands. That was the problem.

Alexandria circled it in the grey morning light, knife loose in her grip, the blade angled flat along her forearm. The training hall was cold stone and colder air, the kind of cold that made the body slow if you let it. She didn't let it.

Exits. The main door stood ajar to her left, the corridor beyond empty. Two high windows on the east wall, rectangles of pale sky, and a cross-breeze that carried the smell of oil and old sweat. Both windows too narrow for a body. Sightlines clear in all directions. She catalogued and dismissed.

The dummy hung from its post, scarred wood gleaming where a thousand strikes had polished the grain. Arms outstretched. No hands. Some previous trainee had sheared them off with a bad cut, and the instructors hadn't replaced them. Without hands, there was no weapon to read, no angle of attack to anticipate. Threat geometry degraded.

She stepped in. Hesitated half a beat, reading the empty wrists, recalculating angles. Her blade hung in the air.

"You think too much."

Vellen's voice came from behind her, flat and carrying. The instructor stood near the door, arms crossed, a block of old muscle in grey robes. She hadn't heard him enter. That was a failure. She filed it.

"The geometry won't save you when a mage has already started casting."

Alexandria didn't answer. She reset her stance, weight on the balls of her feet, and struck. The knife entered the dummy's throat—where the throat would be—in a single clean motion. No wasted movement. The wood thudded and rocked on its post. She stepped back, breathing steady, and noted her pulse. Unchanged. Strike landed. Next.

The geometry was the only thing that would save her. She knew it and did not say it.

Vellen made a sound that wasn't approval. "You're assigned to Savant Orin. Field operation. He's waiting."

---

The old man entered the hall as if summoned by his name. Alexandria's attention shifted without moving her head.

Exits. Not relevant. He wasn't a threat.

Hands. Empty, loose at his sides. Not reaching. The skin was thin over the knuckles, mapped with veins, the fingers slightly crooked. Steady. No tremor. She watched them for a fraction longer than necessary and didn't know why.

Weapons. None visible. The grey Savant robes could conceal a small blade; she estimated probability and dismissed it.

Face. Pale eyes set in a face that had forgotten how to express surprise. He looked at her as if reading a page he'd already memorised. She looked away as soon as the data was collected. No lingering.

Vellen nodded at the old man. "She's ready."

Orin didn't acknowledge the instructor. He spoke to Alexandria. "You're the scalpel. I'm the map. Don't cut until I tell you."

A weapon analogy. She understood it. The body is a weapon. A scalpel was precise, single-purpose, clean. She was that. She gave a short nod and nothing else.

---

The briefing room was a stone box with one door, no windows, one table, and two chairs. Tight geometry. Poor sightlines if the door was breached. Alexandria mapped the space in three seconds and took the chair that kept her back to the wall and the door in peripheral vision. Orin sat opposite. She downgraded the alert level. He wasn't a threat. Not yet.

He placed a file on the table. The cover bore an Amber seal, the wax still glossy under the thin light of a single glow-stone set in the ceiling. Amber debt. Chronic. A caster who'd been paying for years in body and function.

"The Seed," Orin said. "A healer. Unregistered. Operating in the Grain-Lords' Confederacy. She's been moving Sensitive children out of Order reach for at least five years. Capture or terminate. Preference for capture."

Alexandria opened the file. The paper was old, the ink fading at the edges. She scanned with tactical eyes, discarding narrative, extracting data. Known associates. Last confirmed location—a way station two days east of the Grain-Lords' capital. Magical signature classified as Healing mutation, Water Gate.

She noted the debt classification again. Amber. Chronic pain in the hands. A healer who'd healed too much. Joints would be compromised. Grip strength reduced. Vulnerable to joint locks.

"Why a Silencer?" She looked up. "Healing mutation is non-combative."

"She is protected," Orin said. "Former mercenaries. A few defectors. We expect resistance."

Alexandria returned her eyes to the file. The next page was a Savant's ledger, cramped handwriting in columns—dates, locations, reported sightings. She turned the page.

Her eyes caught on a notation near the bottom.

*Possible connection to an abandoned childbirth at the Cradle foundling house. Records incomplete. Infant presumed deceased or absorbed into foundling stock.*

The words didn't move. The rest of the room did.

Her mind stuttered. The phrase surfaced without permission, the one she'd always used, the clinical designation she'd constructed years ago to fill the space where something else belonged. *The woman who gave birth.* No face. No name. A biological event. A closed file.

Her knuckles whitened on the edge of the table.

She released the pressure immediately. Her hands returned to her lap. She looked up, mapped the room again—exits, sightlines, the door still closed, the cross-breeze absent here, the glow-stone humming faintly. Threat assessment: none. Data acquired. Alert level nominal.

She turned the page. The notation was behind her now.

She did not ask about it. Irrelevant. Filed.

Orin's hands rested on the file, fingers interlaced. She watched them for another half-second. Still steady. No tremor.

She rose. "When do we leave."

"Dawn," Orin said. "Grain-Lords' Confederacy. Pack for wet ground."

---

The training hall was empty when she returned. The dummy hung silent on its post, its handless arms spread in a wood-sculpted shrug. She didn't look at it.

She took her knife kit from the bench and sat with her back to the wall. The oilstone was worn smooth in the centre from use. She laid out the blades—primary knife, secondary, a thin boot-knife she never wore inside the Cradle but maintained anyway. Routine was a structure. A structure was a weapon.

Oil on the stone. Blade to the surface. One stroke. Two. Three. The sound was a whisper, metal on grit, steady as a pulse. She counted strokes. Twenty per side. Then the strop. Then the edge check, thumb hovering a hair's-breadth from the steel, reading the bite without touching. Her body knew the ritual. Her mind went quiet. Not peaceful—quiet. The difference mattered.

She cleaned the blades, sheathed them, and sat for a moment in the grey silence. The morning was gone. The day had moved around her without her noticing.

---

Evening. The mess hall was a long low room of stone tables and iron benches, lit by tallow candles that guttered in the draft from the main doors. Alexandria entered and didn't sit.

She scanned. Exits. Main doors east, service door west past the kitchens. Both clear. Sightlines. The room was half-full, trainees in grey clustered at three tables, a scattering of older Order personnel near the hearth. The noise was low, voices layered over the clink of bowls and spoons.

No one looked at her. She preferred it that way.

Mess hall's quiet tonight.

She didn't name the absence. The quiet was just quiet. The empty benches were just empty. The laughter from the far table was just a sound, no different from the wind in the high windows or the scrape of a ladle on iron. She collected a bowl of broth and a heel of dark bread and sat at a bench near the east exit, alone. Optimal position. Clear path to the door. No disadvantage.

She ate without tasting. The broth was warm. That was all that mattered.

The other trainees left in groups, their voices trailing into the corridor. The candles burned lower. A kitchen hand came and cleared the bowls from the empty tables. Alexandria finished her bread, stood, and walked out without a backwards glance.

---

The dormitory was dark. Rows of cots stretched the length of the room, each with a grey blanket and a flat pillow, each identical. Hers was the third from the south wall. She lay on her back on the thin mattress and stared at the ceiling beams, grey on grey in the darkness.

She replayed the mission parameters. Target: the Seed. Healing mutation, Water Gate. Amber debt. Chronic hand pain. Vulnerable to joint locks. Likely protected. Grain-Lords' Confederacy. Wet ground. Pack accordingly.

Dawn departure with Savant Orin. His hands had been steady.

She did not think about the notation in the file. She did not think about the woman who gave birth. She did not think about the foundling house, its grey walls and iron cots, the infants who came in and the children who went out, the ones who were trained and the ones who were not, the space where a mother's name should have been and wasn't.

She did not think about it.

She closed her eyes and listened to the breathing of the other trainees, the rustle of blankets, the distant tread of a night patrol in the corridor. The sounds were data. The dark was just dark.

Mess hall's quiet tonight.

The phrase came back without her summoning it, a flat observation, a sentence she'd filed and couldn't unfile. She examined it once, turned it over, found no tactical value, and let it go.

She slept.
