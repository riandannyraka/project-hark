# Chapter 1 – The Contract

The contract said three. He’d found four.

Snow thigh-deep in the dead-end alley behind the grain store. The cold had teeth and it had been chewing on him for an hour. He stood in the dark where the torchlight from the main street didn't reach and let the numbers settle.

The hands first. Always the hands. Axe-man with a worn glove, fingers flexing on the haft. Cudgel, held loose by a man who didn't know what loose cost. Two daggers, no guards, grips wrong. A pair of fools who'd never been cut.

Exits. The alley ended in a brick wall behind him. Stacked crates to his left, snow-drifted. The only way out was through the four men between him and the street.

Weapons. His own knife, the one with the wrapped hilt, already in his hand. The ugly secondary blade at the small of his back, a flat piece of iron with a leather cord for a grip. No crossguard. It bit the hand that used it. He'd used it before.

Faces. The axe-man had calm eyes. That was the problem. The others were afraid—you could smell it, the way they shifted, the way their breath came too fast and plumed white in the dark. The axe-man just watched. Patient. A man who'd seen worse than a lone knife in an alley.

Hark didn't retreat. The contract was sealed. Reputation was the only asset a Class C could not afford to lose.

Snow creaked. The axe-man's head turned, and Hark was already moving.

The cudgel-man swung wide. Hark stepped inside the arc, trapped the wrist with his off-hand, and drove the knife up under the jaw. The blade punched through soft tissue and stopped on bone. Blood sluiced hot over his knuckles. He let the body drop and turned.

The two dagger-men were still processing. One on his left, one on his right. Hark kicked a plume of snow into the left man's face and pivoted right. The right-hand man stabbed. Hark took it on the ribs, felt the blade skate off the leather of his jerkin, and drove his own knife in under the arm. It hit something vital on the way in and stuck. He let it go and reached for the secondary blade at his back.

The left man was still blinded, swiping snow from his eyes. Hark stepped in and opened his throat with the ugly knife. The cut was deep. The man made a sound like a wet bellows and sat down in the snow.

The axe-man came. Not rushing. A short axe, notched and heavy. He swung, and Hark parried late. The blade rang off the alley wall, and the haft caught him across the shoulder—a glancing blow, but enough to send a bolt of dull pain down to his elbow. His left arm went numb. He counted the rhythm, one swing, two, the backswing always a half-beat slower. On the third swing he stepped inside the man's guard and laid the ugly knife across his throat. The cut was not clean. It tore. The axe dropped first, then the man. He stared at Hark for two seconds with those calm eyes, and then the calm went out of them and he collapsed.

Hark stood. Breath harsh in his throat. The ache in his shoulder was spreading, a hot bruise uncoiling under the skin. Snow drifted down and settled on the bodies.

He retrieved his knife. It came out of the ribs with a sound he didn't need to name. He wiped both blades on the dead axe-man's cloak, slow, methodical, the motion a kind of quiet he understood. His hands were steady. The frost-scars on his knuckles stood white under the blood, old marks from debts paid long ago.

The grain store door was unlocked. Inside, behind a loose board under the counter, he found the sponsor's pay-chest. Tin box, no lock. Inside, a leather coin-pouch and two fragments of debt-null. He picked up the fragments. They were smooth, cool against his fingers. The weight of cancelled obligation. He slipped them into his belt-pouch without counting the coin. That could wait.

Back outside, the snow was already filling his tracks. He watched it work for a moment. Tracks covered. That's something.

The shoulder throbbed. He rolled it, catalogued the damage. Deep bruise. Won't need stitching. His left hand would be stiff for a day, maybe two. He could still hold a knife.

He walked toward the orange light spilling from the Crown of Winter tavern.

---

Inside, the air was thick with woodsmoke and old ale and the wet-wool smell of men who'd been working in the cold. A low fire guttered in the hearth. Hark took the corner table, back to the wall, and counted exits. Two. Front door and a side door past the barrels. Enough.

His body was already wearing the fight. Shoulder stiffening, blood dried brown under his fingernails, the cold still lodged deep in his scars. He ordered ale and didn't drink it. The mug sat in front of him, steam rising, a prop.

Kellis came in a quarter-hour later. Hark saw the hands first. Empty. Clean. Ink stains on the thumb and forefinger. Quill-man, not a fighter. He wore a merchant's coat with a fur collar and walked like a man who expected the ground to stay where he put it.

Kellis sat down across from him. "The grain store's quiet tonight."

Hark said nothing.

"I have a job," Kellis said. He placed a small leather folder on the table. "Someone the Order wants. They call her the Seed. She's operating in the Grain-Lords' Confederacy, moving children. Sensitive children. The Order has sent multiple teams. None have come back with her."

The fragments in Hark's belt-pouch were smooth weights. He could feel them through the leather.

"Payment," Kellis said, "in debt-null fragments. Enough to clear your ledger for a year. Maybe more."

Hark looked at the folder. Then at the fire. Then at his own hands, the frost-scars mapping the knuckles like old riverbeds.

Kellis hadn't mentioned the fourth man. Could be bad information. Could be a test. He paid on time before. That's something.

Bad odds. Too many teams had failed. The kind of job Joel would've walked away from.

His pulse quickened. His hand found the knife hilt under the table. The leather was worn smooth from the same touch, over and over.

Kellis opened the folder. A name. A location. A list of last sightings. The fragments gleamed in Hark's peripheral vision like small cold promises. His chest ached. He looked away.

Wouldn't mind getting paid. Had worse.

He nodded. Once.

Kellis slid the folder across the table. Hark put his hand on it and didn't open it.

He left the tavern. The cold hit him in the face, clean and sharp. The snow was still falling, thick and silent. Behind him, the orange light of the tavern window shrank to a smear, then nothing.

The snow erased his tracks as he walked.

He didn't ask why. The rules forbade the question.
