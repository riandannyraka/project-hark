# Chapter 1 – The Contract

---

The contract said three. He'd found four.

Six hours ago, now. Hark turned the cup in his fingers and didn't drink.

The tavern was too loud — the fire too hot, throwing orange against the low ceiling and making the far corners swim. He'd chosen the corner with his back to two walls and a sight-line to the door because it was the corner with his back to two walls and a sight-line to the door, and that was how it was, and had always been, and didn't require examination.

Twelve men at the tables. Three at the bar. The barman. A woman by the window who was alone and watching the snow. Sixteen total. He catalogued them the way a ledger catalogues entries: no sentiment, no priority. Just the figures.

Hands first.

The men at the tables were drinking or playing cards, hands visible, predictable — the loose grip of men who expected the evening to cost them coin and nothing else. The barman's hands were occupied with a rag and a glass he was presumably cleaning and presumably going to clean forever, because he'd been at it since Hark arrived. The woman by the window had hers folded in her lap. Too still for a woman sitting alone in a tavern at midnight. He'd noted that. He'd move her into a different column if she moved first.

Exits: the main door, which he could see. The kitchen, beyond the bar, from which steam appeared in intermittent puffs like a man thinking reluctant thoughts. The window behind him that he hadn't tested but that faced a ground-floor drop into the alley — manageable if the alternative was worse, and in his experience the alternative was usually worse. Three options. Four if he counted the fire, which he generally didn't unless things had already deteriorated to a point where fire was on the table and not just in the hearth.

His own weapons: the knife at his right hip, sheathed and reachable. The shorter blade at his left, less comfortable, the one he reached for when the first wasn't enough. Joel's sword leaning against the wall at his shoulder — impractical for tight quarters, but he liked having it within arm's reach the way some men needed the sight of a familiar face. He didn't know what that said about him. He didn't ask.

The extra man from this afternoon had been the problem. The contract had described three guards at the access point. He'd arrived and found four, the fourth positioned behind a door he hadn't expected, and the two seconds spent recalculating had cost him a nick on the forearm and a rib that was either fractured or badly bruised — he hadn't investigated and didn't plan to. The job was done. The client would get what he'd paid for. Hark would collect what had been agreed. The thin margin between those two numbers was where he operated, and three years in the Hold-Kingdoms had accustomed him to thin margins.

He put the cup down. He watched the door.

Kellis came through it at the quarter-hour.

---

He was hooded — good wool, no fraying, the kind of quality that meant money or careful maintenance, and Kellis was not a man who maintained things he hadn't had to. He moved through the tavern the way men moved when they were certain they weren't being watched and had made the mistake of believing certainty. Not hurrying. Not pausing. A route to Hark's corner that involved two minor course corrections around tables, suggesting he'd read the layout before entering. That was professional. The profession wasn't reassuring.

He sat down. Didn't ask if the chair was available. Didn't signal the barman. He folded his hands on the table and looked at Hark and said nothing.

Hark looked at the hands.

Too still.

Not the stillness of a man at rest — that stillness had small variations, the unconscious micro-shifts of fingers finding comfortable positions, the reflexive adjustments of a body at ease. These hands were held. The difference was small and would have meant nothing to anyone who wasn't watching, but Hark had spent two decades reading hands and this was the stillness of a man who'd been told that hands gave men away and had spent time training his not to. Which meant someone who knew the same things Hark knew. Which meant someone to watch more carefully.

"Kellis," Hark said.

"Hark." A pause — the measuring kind. "The fourth man. Recovered?"

"He wasn't mine to recover."

"He talked."

"Men who get hit in the throat often find it difficult."

Kellis smiled. It was a smile that had been decided in advance. "Nothing useful. I checked." He unfolded his hands and placed them flat on the table, the gesture of a man showing his palms. *See, nothing.* He was competent at the vocabulary of performed transparency. "I have work."

"You always have work."

"This is different."

Hark picked up the cup. Looked at the drink. Put it down. "I'm listening."

---

Find a woman. That was the job.

Known as the Seed. Last reliable sighting: the Grain-Lords' Confederacy, eastern reaches. No confirmed current location. No solid physical description — mid-thirties, slight, probably travelling under a false name. A healer when she needed a face to wear. Known to operate a network: Sensitive children, the kind the Order would otherwise collect, moved through routes no one had been able to map. She'd been doing it a long time. Long enough that the network had become the story and the woman had receded behind it.

Find her. Confirm location. Current alias. Clean work, Kellis said — low exposure, no action required beyond confirmation.

"Clean work." Hark looked at the far wall. "The last broker who described a job as clean work meant clean for everyone except the person performing it."

"That was a different broker."

"Different broker. Similar outcome." He turned the cup. "Payment."

Kellis reached into his coat. What he placed on the table were three discs — no larger than a coin, matte black, faintly iridescent at the edges in the way oil looks on moving water. They sat on the table's wood as if they'd decided the rules regarding light and surface reflection didn't apply to them. Which, in a manner of speaking, they had.

He stopped looking at them. He looked at the candle on the wall. The candle was ordinary. His chest ached.

"Debt-null fragments," Kellis said. "One now. Two on confirmation."

"I counted three."

"One now. Two on confirmation. As I said."

Hark had stopped breathing without noticing. He started again, carefully. One debt-null fragment was enough to cover a year of careful working — a year where the frost in his knuckles didn't spread further, where he could pull the minimum warmth he needed without calculating the cost by morning, where the Light stayed locked and the ledger on his hands stayed at its current length. Two meant something. Three meant a number he hadn't let himself approach in a long time because it led somewhere the rules didn't permit him to go.

He looked at the candle and stayed there.

"Someone wants her found very much," he said.

"Someone wants her found."

"Someone with enough reach to acquire debt-null fragments and enough operational sense to know I'm currently in the Hold-Kingdoms and recently between contracts."

Kellis didn't respond to that directly, which was response enough.

"Who placed it?"

"Above your access tier."

"That's not a name."

"No." A small pause. "You should know — the bounty is still active."

His pulse moved. His hand found the knife — not a draw, just a settling, his fingers against the handle the way they sometimes needed to settle when a conversation shifted into a different register. The tavern was loud. Someone at the cards table was arguing about a hand in the cheerful way that meant he'd won. The woman by the window hadn't moved. Kellis's hands were flat on the table and his geometry was neutral and there was no immediate threat reading at a level worth responding to, and none of that was the point.

"The Red Mark," Hark said.

"Still active. I'm not here to collect it." Kellis's voice was level — the flatness of a man who'd had this conversation before and had decided the direct approach was most efficient. "If I were here to collect it, we'd be having a different conversation. Someone wants the Seed found. Someone — possibly the same person, possibly not; I genuinely don't know — wants you to find her. The bounty being active means you're already a man carrying exposure. This contract requires discretion. The payment reflects both."

The hand stayed on the knife. He looked at Kellis's face for the first time — habit, reluctant, the fourth item on the list. The broker had the particular stillness of someone whose expression had been trained into a neutral surface. Not lying, exactly. Not telling the full truth, exactly. The usual operating range of a man who'd spent twenty years working the spaces between known jurisdictions.

"He pays on time," Hark had told himself two years ago, when he'd first worked through Kellis. "Hasn't run yet. That's something." It was still something. It wasn't more than that. He didn't look for more than that.

"Why the Confederacy," he said. Not a question — the inflection of a man stating something he expected an answer to.

"It's where she was last seen."

"Two years ago."

"Eighteen months."

"Still not recent."

"No." Kellis didn't elaborate. "The eastern reaches is the best starting point available. What she does requires infrastructure — routes, contacts, hiding places. Infrastructure leaves trace. A good finder will find trace."

Hark thought about the eastern Confederacy. He'd been born there, technically. Flat land, enormous sky, black soil that belonged to somebody else. He hadn't been back in fifteen years. He didn't examine the coincidence. The rules covered that category of examination.

He should fold. That was the play — too many unknown variables, client chain too opaque to trust, the Red Mark already active and a long journey to territory that held memories he'd filed and didn't plan to retrieve. Three weeks' travel at minimum. His ribs hurt. The job had been four men instead of three. The angles were bad.

He was going to take it.

"Two fragments now," he said. "Two on confirmation."

Kellis looked at him. "One now. Two on confirmation. As offered."

"Two now. Two on confirmation. Total of four — because you knew my schedule well enough to approach me the evening I finished my last contract, which means the surveillance cost me something I didn't agree to, and I'm pricing that in."

A pause. The fire cracked. Kellis's hands stayed flat.

"Two now," he said. "Two on confirmation."

Hark nodded. Kellis slid two of the discs across the table. They moved without sound. They caught no light. He kept his eyes down and off them, picked them up by touch, and put them in his breast pocket without looking. He didn't feel them through the cloth. If his chest ached after that he couldn't distinguish the cause.

Kellis stood.

"The alias will be medical — she operates as a healer. If she's in the Confederacy, she'll be near a trading town or a river junction. Somewhere children move through." He settled his coat. "Look for a woman who asks about children before she asks about coin. That's the tell."

Hark said nothing.

"Don't draw Order attention." A pause at the edge of the table. "The Red Mark is separate. Keep it that way."

He was through the main door and gone before Hark had finished watching him go. Good exit. He appreciated clean exits in people regardless of everything else.

---

He sat in the corner for a while longer. He counted the room again. Twelve men at the tables. Three at the bar. The barman and his glass. The woman by the window finally moved — standing, straightening her coat, making for the door. He watched her hands as she passed. Empty. She glanced at him without looking at him, which was a skill worth noting, and walked out into the snow.

Hark left coin on the table for the drink he hadn't touched. He picked up the sword. He went through the kitchen, where a boy was scraping a pot and didn't look up, and out the back door into the alley.

The snow was coming down in the big slow flakes that the Crown of Winter produced in mid-season, steady and indifferent. He stood in the alley for a moment, his breath a pale shape in the cold air. His rib hurt. The frost-scars on his knuckles were the same white lines they'd always been, the ledger unchanged, the debt neither better nor worse than it had been this morning.

He pulled his coat closed and walked.

By the time he reached the end of the alley, his tracks had already gone soft at the edges. By the time he reached the road, they were gone.

He didn't ask himself why he was going. The rules forbade the question.
