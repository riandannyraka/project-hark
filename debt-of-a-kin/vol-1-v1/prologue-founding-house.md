# Prologue – The Foundling House

---

Rain has been falling since the road out of Kerath.

She stopped counting hours somewhere between the second checkpoint and the grey outer wall of the Cradle, when the child stopped moving in her arms and she had to check — fingers to the small throat, the pulse still there, steady, that small drum that had no name yet.

She stands now outside a grey stone door. The building has no windows facing her. Just stone and rain and the sound of water running off the lintel into the dark below it, splitting where the stone has cracked and doesn't meet flush. She has been standing here long enough that the cold has found the gaps in her coat. The child is still warm. She keeps her pressed close, one arm crooked beneath her, the other pressed over her like a roof.

Her hands are shaking.

They've been shaking since the border — since the debt took its accounting and left its record across the tops of her fingers, the old lines from the first fever she ever drew, the newer ones from the birth, the whole ugly ledger of what healing costs written on her skin in white and silver. She knows the cost. She agreed to none of it, and she paid it anyway, the way the body always pays: without asking whether you're ready, without waiting for you to say *yes, take this, I consent to the arithmetic.* 

The child's warmth is the only warm thing left in the world.

She shifts her slightly, just to feel it again — the small weight redistributing, the exhale of breath against her neck — and then she looks at the grey stone door, and she knows she is not going to keep standing here. She is cold and her hands are shaking and there is a fact in front of her that she has known since Kerath, that she has known since before Kerath, that she has known in the marrow of her, in the way you know the turn of a tide before you see the water move. 

She lifts her free hand and knocks.

---

The door opens before she finishes.

A Sister stands there. Clean hands. Grey robes. Eyes that are not unkind and not kind — that register her the way a wound registers: *yes, this is here, yes, I see it, yes, I know what to do.* 

"Name?" 

Renne hears her own throat work. "Alexandria."

The Sister's stylus moves.

"Your name?"

"No one."

Silence. The Sister doesn't push. This must happen often enough — girls arriving in the rain with something they can no longer carry. Renne looks at the Sister's clean hands. Wonders if she bleeds under them, somewhere deep, in the places that don't show. She can't diagnose what she can't see. She has learned to stop trying with things she can't see.

The Sister steps back to let her in.

---

The corridor is dry. After the rain, that is the first thing — the absence of water, the stillness of air that has never moved. Lanterns in iron brackets. The smell of clean linen and something below it, the cold stone smell of a building that has stood so long it has forgotten the ground it grew from. 

She follows the Sister past a closed door, another, a turn, another. Her boots are wet. They leave prints. She watches them, briefly. Evidence of where she has been.

The room at the end has cots lining the walls. A dozen, maybe more, arranged with the same precise spacing as teeth. Most are occupied — she can see the small mounds of breathing shapes, hear the various soft rhythms of children who do not know yet what they are, who have not yet been sorted into what they will become. 

She stops at the empty cot the Sister indicates. Looks down at it. The linen is clean. Somebody washes it. That matters, she tells herself. That matters.

The child stirs.

Renne — holds her. One moment more. The warmth of her. The impossible smallness. The scar on her own palm where she pressed too hard in the last hour before the border, trying to hold something back that the debt was determined to take, and the debt took it anyway, because the debt always finds the thing you need most and takes that first.

She breathes.

The child's weight is water in her arms. She can feel the tide approaching — the Sister behind her, waiting, hands folded, the cot below — and she understands now that the body already knew. The body has been walking toward this for months. The body has already done it, in the way that things you can't survive get done before you can decide whether to do them. She is only arriving at what the body decided in a room in Kerath at seventeen, in the dark, with her hands too scarred to stop shaking and no one else in the world who knew where she was.

She lowers her into the cot.

Her hands are shaking so hard she is afraid she will drop her. She does not drop her. She lowers her down with the steadiness she learned pulling glass from wounds in the back rooms of market towns: you do not let the tremble into your fingers. The tremble is in your chest. The tremble is yours. It does not belong to the patient.

The child's eyes open.

Dark. Still. Considering.

She does not cry.

Renne straightens. Her hands want to reach back down. She does not let them.

"She was born healthy." Her voice is level. She learned that too — the voice that does not betray what the hands are doing. "The birth was clean. No complications."

She does not say: I already know what she is. I felt it at the border, the small bright thing in her, precise and quiet, the kind that doesn't announce itself.

She does not say: I called her something else, in the dark, before I gave her a name the Order could write down.

She does not say: she's a —

Her hands close. The old scars pull, white and sudden, and she breathes through her back teeth until the pain passes.

"We'll note it," the Sister says, and makes another mark.

---

The door at the end of the corridor is heavier than the one she entered through. She pushes it with both palms and the rain comes back all at once — cold, sharp, the smell of mud and wet stone and the dark miles of the Cradle's grounds between her and the nearest road.

She walks.

She does not look back.

Not because she has mastered herself. She hasn't — the inside of her chest is something she cannot name, something past her own training, an infection without a source she can locate or drain. She does not look back because she knows that if she looks back she will see the grey stone door, closed, and that will be correct, and she cannot be correct about that yet. The body needs one more minute of not-knowing. Give it the rain and the dark and the sound of her own boots in the mud.

She walks until the building is behind her. Until the sound of rain on the roof is replaced by rain on her own shoulders, her own face, her own shaking hands.

She stops.

Presses her hands to her chest. Touches the first scar — the one that runs from the base of her forefinger to the wrist, the old one, the fever in Kerath when she was fifteen and her Gate came open for the first time and she didn't know yet what anything cost. Then the next. Then the next. She counts them the way she always counts them — each one a working, each one a patient who lived, each one a debt she agreed to pay and paid.

There are more than she remembered. There are always more than she remembered.

The rain is cold on her face. She is precise about this: not tears. Rain.

She closes her eyes.

"I'll find you again."

Her voice is very small. Water on stone.

"I swear it."

She opens her eyes. The Cradle's outer ring sits grey and massive in the dark behind her. Somewhere inside it, in a cot in a room of sleeping children, a girl who doesn't cry lies awake with dark, considering eyes. 

Renne turns. She puts the building at her back. She walks.

The road is mud and the rain is cold and her hands are shaking and she will be walking for hours yet before she reaches the first safe house, the first fire, the first moment she can sit down and let the tremble out of her chest into open air where it can dissolve without touching anything.

But she will walk. She will always walk. This is what she knows how to do.

The rain falls.

She does not stop.

---

*In the Order's files, one floor below the room with the cots, the birth record is entered, the debt notation made, and the column for next of kin left blank. It will stay blank for fifteen years. Then someone will find it, and the column will stop being blank, and everything the column kept tidy will stop being tidy, and none of that is yet.*

*For now: a grey stone door. A cot with clean linen. A girl who doesn't cry.*

*Rain.*
