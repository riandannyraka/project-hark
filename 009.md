# Chapter 9: The Observation

Day one. The harbour.

Hark stood at the railing above the fish market and watched. Not the boats—the people. The fishermen hauling their catch, the merchants arguing over prices, the dockworkers loading crates onto wagons bound inland. He watched their hands. Callused. Wet. Some missing fingers. The hands of men who worked for a living and didn't ask questions. None of them were looking at him. None of them were looking for anyone.

He moved on.

The Factorage. Morning light through grimy windows. Verrol at his desk, same as always, pen moving across the ledger in its steady rhythm. Clean hands. Steady hands. The hands of a man who logged contracts and remembered faces and didn't flinch at the smell of blood. He didn't look up when Hark passed the window. He never looked up. That was Verrol's way. That was either nothing or it was everything.

Graen's office. The door was closed. Walnut shells on the floor outside, crushed into the boards. Fresh ones. Graen was inside, eating his feelings, cracking shells with his thumbs. The ritual continued. The guildmaster was consistent. Consistency was either innocence or a very good mask.

Hark walked on.

The Weeping Dog. Midday. Empty except for Soric, who was restacking bottles behind the bar with his one hand. The stump of his left arm moved in its familiar rhythm, compensating for an absence that had long since stopped being a surprise. Soric didn't look at Hark when he passed the window. Didn't need to. Soric had already said his piece. The rumor was delivered. The splinter was lodged. The rest was Hark's problem.

He stood across the street from the Weeping Dog and watched the door for an hour. No one went in. Two fishermen came out. Neither of them looked like Order. Neither of them looked like anything except tired men who'd spent their morning hauling nets.

No patterns. The rumor might be nothing.

Day two. The street outside the ropewalk.

Same faces. Same rhythms. The net-maker's apprentice came out at dawn to hang the morning's work. The baker's boy delivered bread to the chandlery at mid-morning. The old woman with the limp walked her dog past the ropewalk at noon, same as she'd done every day for the three years Hark had been living above it. He'd never spoken to her. He didn't know her name. He knew her limp. He knew her dog was missing an ear. He knew she never looked up at his window.

No one else did either. No one was watching the ropewalk. No one was asking questions at the tavern. No one was lingering at the harbour with the particular stillness of a man who was waiting for someone rather than something.

The rumor might be nothing. The number was probably wrong. Soric's numbers were always fluid. *Seven. Could be more. Could be fewer.*

But the part about the training didn't change. Someone was teaching mages to guard without the Order's methods. That wasn't a number. That was a fact. If it was true, the Order would hunt them. If the Order hunted them, they'd send Cradle-hounds and a Savant and enough mage-detectors to sweep every island in the archipelago. They'd find the escapees. They'd find anyone who'd helped them. They'd find anyone who knew.

Hark didn't want to be found. He'd spent fifteen years not being found. The rumor was a splinter he couldn't remove, and splinters festered if you left them in.

Day three. The glove.

He sat on the edge of the cot in the ropewalk room and laid out his tools. Needle. Waxed cord. The left glove, the loose thread still tucked under the seam where he'd shoved it a week ago. A temporary fix. Temporary was over.

The thread came free with a tug. The seam gaped along his index finger, the leather curling back to reveal the darker leather underneath—the original glove, the part that hadn't been patched yet. He'd repaired these gloves four times. The palms were more patches than original hide. The fingers were stiff with old stitches, the leather cracked along the knuckles from years of gripping swords and hilts and the cold that never left his hands.

He threaded the needle. Waxed cord, black, the same cord he used for bowstrings. It was stronger than it needed to be. That was the point.

His mother had taught him to sew.

He'd been five, maybe six. Small enough that his feet didn't reach the floor when he sat at the kitchen table. She'd put a needle in his hand and shown him how to thread it, her fingers quick and sure, the motion so practiced it looked like magic. Not the real kind. The ordinary kind. The kind that came from doing something ten thousand times.

"You'll need this," she'd said. Her voice was low, a little rough, the voice of a woman who'd been talking to herself for years and wasn't used to an audience. "Clothes cost. Thread is cheap. You learn to fix what breaks, you save more than coin."

He'd watched her hands. The needle dipping in and out of the fabric. The way she pulled each stitch tight, tested it, moved on to the next. He'd memorized the pattern. The rhythm. The quiet certainty of a woman who'd learned that things broke and could be mended, if you caught them early enough, if you didn't let the tear spread.

She'd been darning a sock that had more holes than fabric. He remembered that. The sock was grey, or had been once, and her thumb kept poking through the heel where the wool had worn thin. She'd patched it anyway. She always patched things. She never threw anything away.

She would be proud, probably. That he'd remembered. That the stitches were neat and even and held. She would be sadder. That he was stitching his own gloves in a rented room above a ropewalk, fifteen years later, still running, still hiding his hands. She would not say it. She had never said the hard things aloud. She had just pressed her palm to his forehead and frowned and said he was warm but not sick, warm but not sick, and the word she was looking for was *sensitive* and she didn't have it.

He didn't think about that. He counted stitches.

One. The needle pierced the leather, the cord following, waxed and strong.

Two. The edges of the seam came together, the gap narrowing.

Three. The rhythm settled into his hands the way it always did, the motion old and familiar, the body remembering what the mind couldn't afford to.

Four. Five. Six.

He kept counting. The counting was the point. The counting left no room for anything else. No room for the splinter. No room for the rumor. No room for the dream of a campfire and faces around it and people who understood what the silk felt like. No room for Lea's grey eyes or Joel's yellowed fingers or his mother's voice saying *Damian* like it was something she was losing.

Seven. Eight. Nine.

The glove took shape under his hands. The seam closed. The leather was stiff where the new stitches pulled it tight, but it would soften with wear. Everything softened with wear. Even the cold in his fingers, over time, had become something he could ignore. Mostly.

Ten. Eleven. Twelve.

He tied off the cord. Cut the excess with his belt knife. Held the glove up to the grey light from the salt-crusted window. The stitches were small and even and barely visible against the black leather. Good work. Joel would have approved. Joel had never learned to sew. He'd paid other people to do it, or worn his holes until they became ventilation. "A man with a needle is a man who plans to live," he'd said once, watching Hark patch a tear in his coat. "You planning to live, Hark?"

"Apparently."

"Good. Keep doing it."

He pulled the glove on. The leather slid over his knuckles, snug and familiar. He flexed his fingers. The frost-scars were invisible beneath the leather—white lines on white skin, the ledger in flesh, the permanent record of every working he'd ever done. No one could read them. No one ever would. That was the point of the gloves. That was the point of everything.

He flexed again. The glove held.

Day three, evening. The decision.

He sat on the cot with his back against the wall and laid out the facts like coins on a table.

One: The rumor existed. Escaped mages, seven of them, in the islands. Guarding techniques being taught outside the Order. Soric believed it enough to mention it. Soric didn't mention things he didn't believe.

Two: If the rumor reached Salthaven, it had reached other places. Port towns talked to each other. Sailors carried news the way rats carried fleas. The Order had ears everywhere—mage-detectors, informants, Savants who read debt and reported what they saw. If the rumor was true, the Order already knew.

Three: The Order would respond. They always responded. A cluster of escaped mages was a threat to their monopoly. A mage teaching guarding techniques was an existential crisis. They'd send hunters. Cradle-hounds to track the magical signatures. A Savant to read the debt-patterns. Silencers to handle any resistance. The operation would be quiet, professional, and utterly without mercy.

Four: The hunters would sweep the islands. They'd find the escapees. They'd find anyone nearby—any unmarked mage, any Insensitive who'd helped, any person with the wrong kind of scars on their hands. Hark's scars were hidden. Hark's gate was guarded. Hark was a thousand miles from the islands and no one in Salthaven knew what he was.

But if the hunters came north. If the sweep expanded. If someone in Salthaven talked—Soric, Verrol, Graen, the old woman with the limp, anyone who'd seen a man in gloves who never took them off—

He needed to know. Not to help. He didn't help. Helping was a bad contract with worse terms. Helping got you a bolt through the chest and a name left beside a body in the forest. Helping got you dead.

He needed to assess. If the escapees were a pattern—a cluster the Order would hunt, a beacon that would draw hunters like blood in the water—he needed to know how big the pattern was. How far the ripples spread. Whether they'd reach Salthaven, or the Confederacy, or the ropewalk room with the salt-crusted window and the lock he checked twice every night.

If they would, he needed to be gone before the hunters arrived. New city. New name. New gloves. The same cold in his fingers and the same ledger in his flesh and the same fifteen years of running ahead of him.

If they wouldn't, he could stay. The contracts were steady. The room was cheap. Soric poured without being asked. The routine was a cage, but it was his cage, and he'd built it himself, stitch by stitch.

He stood. Crossed to the window. The harbour was dark, the fishing boats lit by lanterns, small flames bobbing on black water. The streets were empty. The rain had stopped. The splinter was still there.

Tomorrow. He'd ask Verrol about contracts in the south. Caravan work. Message running. Any excuse to get closer to the islands without announcing why. Verrol wouldn't ask. Verrol never asked. That was Verrol's way. That was the only reason Hark was still alive.

He turned away from the window. Did not lock the door. Did not check the lock twice. The routine was already broken. The decision was already made. He lay on the cot and stared at the ceiling—crack east to west, water stain shaped like a hand—and did not sleep.

The rumor was a splinter. The splinter was a door. The door was opening, and Hark was walking through it, and he didn't know what was on the other side.

That was the problem. That was always the problem.
