# Chapter 19: The False Trail

Three days. Three islands. A trail of fire and frost and the bodies of things that had been in the wrong place at the wrong time.

Hark ran.

The first island was scrub and shale, the ground loose underfoot, the vegetation dry enough to burn. He burned it. A bolt of base-state flame into a stand of dead trees, the heat roaring up, the smoke visible for miles. The debt settled in his fingers—new frost, new white lines, the cold climbing past his wrists. He kept running. Behind him, the fire spread. Behind the fire, the Order would be following. He made sure of it.

The second island was forested, dense, the canopy so thick the sky was a rumour. He killed an ogre there—not for a contract, not for silver, just because it was in his path. Heat-theft. His bare hand on its chest, the warmth draining out of it, the frost spreading across its skin. It died confused. He left the body where it fell. Another signature. Another entry in the ledger. The Savant would read it. The Savant would follow.

The third island was rocks and tide pools and a fishing village he didn't enter. He skirted the edges, left a pulse of Light at the crest of a hill—brief, controlled, just bright enough to be seen. The fever-heat climbed. His skin steamed in the cold air. The frost reached his elbows. He closed his gate and kept moving.

He ate what he could find. Dried fish from a market stall he passed without stopping. A handful of berries that might have been poisonous. Water from streams that might have been clean. He didn't taste any of it. Food was fuel. Fuel was function. The rest was noise.

He slept in snatches. An hour against a tree. Two hours in a cave that smelled of bat guano and old bones. The cold in his fingers was constant now—not a wave, not a spike, just a permanent weight that made every grip uncertain. He wrapped his hands in strips of cloth torn from his shirt. It didn't help. He did it anyway.

Marta's stew. Corin's fidgeting. The cottage. The hearth. The blanket.

Renne's hands. The vortex. The kiss.

He ran. The memories ran with him. He didn't name them. He didn't let them go.

He had been running for fifteen years.

First from the Order, then from himself, then from the cold in his chest that he refused to name. The carriage and the silk and the handler's hands. The dormitory and the training floor and the instructor's flat voice. The forest and the fever spikes and the years of being no one in particular. The Confederacy. The Dominion. The Hold-Kingdoms. The ropewalk room and the Factorage and the Weeping Dog and a thousand contracts that paid in silver and asked no questions.

He had run across oceans and mountains and plains. He had run until his legs gave out, and then he had crawled, and then he had run again. His legs ached. His shoulders ached. The cold in his fingers was weather now—permanent, unremarkable, the same grey sky every morning.

But he could not stop. His legs kept moving. His lungs kept pulling air. The body remembered what the mind refused: stopping meant settling, and settling meant the debt came due, and the debt was Lea's grey eyes and the bolt through her chest and the silence after.

He didn't think about what he was running toward. He didn't think about the cottage, or the hearth, or the blanket Marta had put over him without asking. He didn't think about Renne's hands, still at last, holding his.

He ran. That was enough. That had to be enough.

He ran until his body gave out.

The cave was small and dark and cold, a wound in the limestone of the fourth island, the walls sweating, the floor scattered with the bones of small animals. He crawled inside. His legs wouldn't hold him anymore. His hands were shaking—not from fear, from exhaustion, the fine tremor of a body that had spent everything and was running on fumes. He sat against the wall. The stone was cold. He was colder.

Moss on north-facing stone. Green so deep it was almost black in the dark.

The image surfaced. The stone was cold under his fingers—he could feel it, even now, even through the gloves. The moss was thick and damp and alive. Asking nothing. Just there.

He held it. Held it until the panic stopped clawing. Held it until his heart slowed and his breathing steadied and the cold in his fingers was just cold again. He didn't sleep. He never slept when they were close. He sat in the dark and waited for dawn.

They came with the light.

The Savant. The same grey tabard. The same clean hands. Behind her, three Silencers in half-plate, crossbows raised, faces blank. More than before. She'd brought reinforcements. She'd read his trail and calculated the odds and brought enough to finish it.

He stood. His legs screamed. His arm was still bleeding from the graze three days ago—the wound hadn't closed, the blood crusted on his sleeve, fresh red seeping through when he moved. His sword was in his hand. He didn't remember drawing it. Joel's blade. The grip was warm. The edge was still sharp.

The Silencers fanned out. The Savant watched from behind them. Her hands were still folded. Her eyes were still on his hands.

"You've led us quite a chase," she said. "Impressive stamina. The Order's training, again."

"The Order can go to hell."

"Probably. But not today." She tilted her head. "You're outnumbered. You're wounded. You're exhausted. You could surrender. The Cradle is still standing. There's always room for a trained mage who's willing to return to the fold."

"I'd rather die."

"That's the expected response." She nodded to the Silencers. "Kill him."

They advanced. Three of them. Crossbows up. Steel gleaming in the grey dawn light. Hark raised his sword. His hands were cold. His legs were shaking.

He stood anyway.

The moss was somewhere at the back of his mind—green and dark and alive. Renne's hands. The kiss. Salt on his lips.

He held them. Held them and didn't let go.

The Silencers closed in. The mist rose from the grass. The sun came up, pale and cold over the water.

His hands were cold. His legs were shaking. The sword was steady.

He'd been running for fifteen years. He was done running.
