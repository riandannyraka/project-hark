# Chapter 10: The Trail

The ferryman had one eye and a voice like gravel rolling downhill. He talked the whole way across the channel—about the weather, about the fishing, about his daughter who'd married a net-maker in some village Hark had never heard of. Hark stood at the rail and let the words wash over him. He was good at that. Listening without hearing. Hearing without responding. The ferryman didn't seem to mind. Some people talked to fill the silence. Hark let them. Silence was a tool. Other people's noise was a blanket.

The first island was called Kelpwick. A dozen houses huddled around a harbour the size of a bathtub. The inn had no name. The innkeeper had no teeth. Hark bought a meal he didn't taste and sat in a corner with his back to the wall and listened.

The fishermen talked about the weather. The weather was bad. The fishermen talked about the catch. The catch was worse. The fishermen talked about the war—not the war, there was no war, but the tension between the League and the Order that everyone felt and no one named. The Order was pressing. The League was pushing back. The islands were caught between them like grain between millstones.

No one mentioned escaped mages. No one mentioned a woman with grey eyes who taught guarding. Hark drank his ale and waited. The fishermen went home. The innkeeper swept the floor. Hark went to his room and locked the door and checked the lock twice and lay on a cot that was harder than the one in the ropewalk room. He didn't sleep. The splinter was still there.

The second island was called Driftwood. Bigger than Kelpwick. A market, a temple, a harbour with actual docks instead of a jetty. Hark walked the market for three hours, buying nothing, watching everything. He bought a strip of dried fish from a stall. Ate it standing up. The vendor mentioned a cousin who'd seen a mage once—a Water-mage, down in the southern islands, who could freeze the sea with her bare hands. Hark asked if the cousin had a name. The vendor didn't know. Hark bought another strip of fish and moved on.

The tavern in Driftwood was called the Gull's Rest. The sign was a seagull sleeping on a piling. Someone had painted it badly. The bird looked drunk. Hark took a table near the back and ordered ale and waited.

The barmaid was young, dark-skinned, with a tattoo on her wrist that might have been a fish or might have been a knife. She brought his ale and didn't smile. "You're new."

"Passing through."

"Everyone's passing through." She wiped her hands on her apron. "Where you headed?"

"South."

"South's big. Got a lot of islands."

"Looking for work. Caravan guard. Message running. Whatever pays."

She looked at him for a long moment. Her eyes dropped to his gloves. Then to his face. Then back to the bar. "South's got work. South's also got trouble. League patrols. Order sniffing around. People mind their business down there."

"Good policy."

"It's the only policy." She picked up her tray. "You want another ale, ask. Don't ask questions. Questions cost extra."

She walked away. Hark drank his ale. Her hands had been steady. Her eyes had been sharp. She knew something. She wasn't telling. That was fair. He wasn't asking.

He slept that night in a room above the Gull's Rest. The dream came—Lea's grey eyes, the bolt, the pressure collapsing—and he woke with his heart hammering and his gate bleeding heat. He clamped it down. The room was cold. His fingers were colder. He didn't sleep again.

The third island had no name on any map Hark had seen. The ferryman who took him there was a silent man with a scarred face and a dog that sat in the bow and watched the water like it was waiting for something to surface. The crossing was rough. The wind came up from the south and the waves came with it, and the ferry pitched like a drunk. Hark gripped the rail and watched the horizon. His stomach clenched. He'd never been a good sailor. The sea was Water's domain, not Fire's, and his body knew it.

A Lightning-mage could calm the waves. Could smooth the charge in the air, settle the static, talk the storm into silence. A Water-mage could ride them—shape the swells, steer the current, make the sea a weapon or a road. Hark was neither. He was Fire, and Fire had no purchase on water, and the sea did not care what he was. The sea just moved, and he moved with it, and his stomach moved in the opposite direction.

He didn't vomit. Barely. The ferryman pretended not to notice. The dog gave him a look that might have been sympathy or might have been contempt. Hard to tell with dogs.

The island was small. Green hills rising from a rocky shore. A village at the water's edge, boats pulled up on the shingle, nets drying in the sun. It was quiet. The quiet of a place that didn't get many visitors. The kind of quiet that noticed when someone new arrived.

Hark walked up from the dock. The path was dirt, packed hard by feet and weather. A child was sitting on a rock near the water, fishing with a line and a bent pin. She looked up when he passed. Young. Maybe eight. Bare feet. A knife on her belt that was too big for her.

"Looking for someone?" She didn't stop fishing.

"Maybe."

"Maybe's not a person."

"Person I'm looking for might not want to be found."

The child considered this. "Most people here don't want to be found. That's why they're here."

"Smart place."

"We like it." She pulled her line up. Checked the bait. Dropped it back in. "Who you looking for?"

He'd heard the description three times now. Different islands. Different voices. The same words. *Grey eyes. Dark hair. Hands that never stop moving.* The last part had caught him the first time—a hook in the chest, a pull behind the sternum, a door opening that he'd kept locked for fifteen years. He'd closed it again. He'd always closed it. But the description kept coming, and the door kept rattling, and his chest was tight in a way that had nothing to do with the ferry crossing.

"Water-mage," he said. "Teaches guarding. Been free for a while."

The child squinted at him. "You Order?"

"No."

"Bounty hunter?"

"No."

"Then why you looking?"

"Need to know something."

The child pulled her line up again. Wound it around the stick. Stood up. She was smaller than he'd thought. The knife on her belt bumped against her thigh. "The lady who makes the water dance," she said. "She lives up there." She pointed toward the hills. A path wound up through the scrub, disappearing into a stand of wind-twisted trees. "You want me to take you?"

"No."

"You sure? Path splits twice. Wrong turn takes you to old Kellen's place, and old Kellen's got a goat that hates strangers."

"I'll find it."

The child shrugged. "Your funeral." She walked off toward the village, bare feet slapping on the packed dirt. The knife bounced against her leg. She didn't look back.

Hark stood at the foot of the path. The hills rose ahead of him. The sea was behind him. The sky was grey, threatening rain. His hands were cold. His chest was tight. He did not name the feeling. He never named it. Naming things made them real. Real things cost something.

Lea's hands had always been in motion.

Even when she was still, her fingers were moving. Tapping against the cot frame. Tracing patterns in the air. Shaping the wind into small vortices that spun between her palms and then dissipated, leaving the dormitory air a little more stirred than it had been. He'd watched her do it a hundred times. In the dark. During training. In the quiet moments between fear and the next fear.

"It helps me think," she'd said, when he asked. They were nine, maybe ten. The dormitory was quiet. The other children were asleep. Her fingers were spinning a tiny cyclone above her chest, the air flickering silver in the faint light from the high window. "The wind wants to move. If I don't let it move, it builds up. Makes my chest tight. The instructors say I should guard harder, but guarding harder makes it worse. This makes it better."

He'd watched her hands for hours after that. He learned to read her mood by the speed of her fingers. Fast meant afraid—little gusts of wind escaping her control, the air pressure spiking around her cot. Slow meant thinking—the vortex steady and deliberate, her grey eyes focused on something he couldn't see. Still meant something was wrong. Still meant she was too tired to move, or too sad, or too afraid to show it.

He had not seen her hands still until the bolt took her.

Then they were very still. Her fingers curled against her palm, the vortex gone, the air around her collapsing inward as her gate failed. He'd held her hand. Squeezed it. She'd squeezed back—once, faint, already fading. Then nothing. Then cold.

Her hands had been warm when she showed him the vortex. The first time. The time he'd told her it was beautiful. Her palm had pressed against his, cool skin and warm skin, and the air between them had spun like a promise. She'd smiled. She'd said she wanted to make the water shiver. She'd said she was going to take him with her when she ran.

She had taken him. She hadn't made it herself.

He started walking.

The path was steep. The scrub gave way to trees—wind-twisted, salt-bitten, their trunks bent inland by years of storms. The path split twice, just as the child had promised. He took the left fork the first time, the right fork the second time, and both times he didn't hesitate. He didn't know where he was going. He was following something that wasn't a trail. A pull. A pressure. The same thing that had made him leave Salthaven and cross three channels and ask questions without asking them.

The woman in the description was not Lea. Lea was dead. He'd closed her eyes. He'd left his name beside her body. He'd spent fifteen years dreaming her last breath and waking with his gate bleeding heat. The woman on the island was a stranger—a Water-mage, not Wind. She'd been free for a decade, not dead at eleven. The description was a coincidence. Grey eyes and dark hair were common enough. Hands that never stopped moving was a trait, not a signature.

But the pull was there. The splinter. The door that wouldn't stay closed. He walked, and his hands were cold, and his chest was tight, and he didn't stop.

The path crested a rise. The trees opened. A house stood in a hollow below—small, stone, a thatched roof with smoke curling from the chimney. A garden out front. A rain barrel. A clothesline strung between two trees, the laundry snapping in the wind off the sea. A woman was kneeling in the garden, her back to him, her hands working the soil. Dark hair. Lean shoulders. She didn't look up.

Hark stood at the edge of the hollow. The wind was cold. The sea was grey. The woman's hands moved in the dirt—planting something, weeding something, the motion steady and sure. He watched her hands for a long moment. He didn't move. He didn't speak.

She wasn't Lea. He knew that. He'd known it before he started walking. But the pull was still there—a hook in his chest, a door he couldn't close—and the woman's hands were still moving, and somewhere in the hills above the sea, a Water-mage who'd been free for a decade was teaching others to guard.

He walked down into the hollow.
