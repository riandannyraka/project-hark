# THE EDGE FRAMEWORK

## A Treatise on Martial Craft, the Schools of Steel, and Why the Common Soldier is Never Powerless

*A Note on Audience: This book is written for the recruit, the instructor, and the commander. The veteran sellsword will find nothing new here except the shape of their own calluses described in cold prose. The instructor will find the pedagogy of the blade. The commander will find the reason a thousand common soldiers still matter in an age of mages.*

---

# BOOK ONE: THE MARTIAL PRINCIPLE

## 1. What Martial Power Is

Magic is borrowing. The mage takes from the world or their body, and every taking leaves a debt that must be paid — in flesh, in landscape, in time.

Martial power is neither borrowing nor spending. It is **application**.

The warrior does not redirect energy. The warrior does not burn fuel in some esoteric furnace. The warrior takes what already exists — mass, momentum, leverage, timing, the mechanical limits of the human body — and applies it with precision.

A sword cut is not borrowed force. It is the body's natural strength, trained and focused. A parry is not spent will. It is geometry and timing learned until they are reflex. A held position is not burning conviction. It is posture and breath and the conditioned endurance of muscle.

This is why warriors are common and mages are scarce. The capacity to borrow is congenital — a Gate opens at birth or it never does. The capacity to apply is universal. Every human born with a body can, in principle, learn to use it martially.

**The First Principle:** Martial power is trained application. Nothing is taken. Nothing is owed.

---

## 2. The Three Foundations

Every martial act rests on three foundations, all trainable, none innate:

**Condition:** The body's physical capacity — strength, speed, endurance, flexibility, recovery. Built through repetitive effort. Maintained through discipline. Lost through neglect. Condition is the raw material that technique shapes.

**Technique:** The specific patterns of movement and decision that convert condition into effective action — how to stand, how to strike, how to parry, how to move, how to read an opponent. Technique is learned, drilled, internalised until it operates faster than conscious thought.

**Judgement:** The capacity to apply the right technique at the right moment, to read the situation, to know when to commit and when to wait, when to advance and when to withdraw. Judgement cannot be drilled directly. It is cultivated through experience, reflection, and — most reliably — surviving one's own mistakes.

These three are universal. Every competent warrior possesses all three. The Schools differ only in how they prioritise and combine them.

---

## 3. The Edge

The trained warrior's advantage over the untrained is called the **Edge**.

The Edge is not supernatural. It is not a separate power. It is simply the cumulative difference between trained application and untrained flailing. It manifests in ways so mundane they are almost invisible:

- The trained fighter's blade arrives a fraction of a second faster — not because they are stronger, but because their movement contains no wasted motion.
- The trained fighter seems to read attacks before they happen — not through prescience, but because their eye catches the tells that untrained fighters broadcast unconsciously.
- The trained fighter breathes steadily through exertion that leaves a novice gasping — not through greater lung capacity, but through learned breath control and economy of motion.
- The trained fighter remains calm under pressure — not through unnatural courage, but through familiarity; they have seen this situation before, drilled it, survived it.

To the untrained eye, a master warrior can seem almost magical. They are not. They are simply refined. Every movement has been shaved of inefficiency. Every reaction has been internalised to reflex. The Edge is the gap between the body's raw potential and the body's trained performance.

---

## 4. The Limit Principle

**The Second Principle:** The Edge has limits. They are the limits of the body and the situation.

A warrior cannot cut through plate steel with a longsword. The steel is harder than flesh. Geometry and leverage can redirect force, but they cannot multiply it beyond the body's capacity.

A warrior cannot fight indefinitely. Condition is finite. Muscles fatigue. Focus drifts. The body must rest and recover — not to pay a metaphysical debt, but simply because it is a biological machine with biological limits.

A warrior cannot ignore a mortal wound. A mage might, through desperate borrowing, keep fighting with a shattered body. A warrior who takes a blade through the lung goes down, same as anyone else. The Edge does not transcend flesh. It works within it.

This is the crucial asymmetry between mage and warrior. A mage can, in extremis, overdraw, accumulate ruinous debt, and continue casting past the point of safety. A warrior cannot. The warrior's limit is absolute and non-negotiable. When the body fails, it fails. There is no reserve of borrowed power to tap.

This is also why warriors fear mages and mages fear armies. A single mage at full debt can devastate. But a hundred warriors with steady Edge will eventually close the distance, and when they do, the mage's body is as vulnerable as anyone's.

---

## 5. Training and the Unorthodox Path

The Edge can be acquired through two broad paths:

**Formal Training:** The School path. A student learns under an instructor who transmits a codified curriculum of condition, technique, and judgement. Schools are hierarchical, systematic, and conservative. They produce reliable, standardised warriors. Their weakness is predictability: every graduate of the Iron Rib School parries a high cut the same way, and an opponent who knows the School knows what to expect.

**The Unorthodox Path:** The rough path. A fighter learns through experience — brawls, mercenary work, battlefield survival, apprenticeship to a solitary master, theft of techniques from multiple Schools, innovation born of necessity. Unorthodox fighters lack the systematic foundation of the Schools. They often have gaps in their conditioning and blind spots in their technique. But they compensate with unpredictability, adaptability, and techniques no School would sanction — not because they are forbidden, but because they are ugly, situational, or born of desperation.

Most warriors are a mix. The veteran soldier who trained formally in youth and has since learned a dozen battlefield tricks no instructor would teach. The duelist who mastered one School and then spent years stealing techniques from opponents. The brawler who eventually found a teacher to fill in their gaps.

Neither path is inherently superior. The formal fighter is more reliable. The unorthodox fighter is less predictable. Circumstance determines advantage.

# BOOK TWO: THE SCHOOLS OF EDGE

*A Note on Terminology: The Schools are not mystical traditions. They are pedagogical lineages — specific approaches to training condition, technique, and judgement that have proven effective enough to be passed down through generations of instructors. A School's techniques can be learned by anyone with a body and sufficient dedication. There is no initiation, no oath, no investiture. Only training.*

---

## THE STEEL SCHOOLS

*Condition-focused. Direct, powerful, demanding. The Schools of the front line.*

### 1. THE IRON RIB SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Strength and endurance through graduated resistance.

**Signature Technique:** The Rooted Guard — a stance and breathing pattern that allows the practitioner to absorb and redirect incoming force through skeletal alignment rather than muscular strain. An Iron Rib fighter in full guard is notoriously difficult to move or stagger.

**Training Method:** Progressive weight-bearing drills, extended guard-holding under simulated pressure, paired impact conditioning.

**Weakness:** Limited mobility in full guard. An Iron Rib fighter who commits to holding ground cannot quickly reposition.

**Common Among:** Heavy infantry, siege defenders, bodyguards.

**Notable Practitioner:** Commander Vasha Koln, who held the Sunken Gate for seven hours against twelve consecutive assaults. She was found afterwards still in Rooted Guard, unconscious but upright, her knees locked and her shield arm broken in three places. She survived.

---

### 2. THE LONG DRIVE SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Explosive power and continuous forward pressure.

**Signature Technique:** The Cascade — a series of strikes delivered with full commitment, each blow's momentum feeding into the next, creating a rhythm that forces the opponent into a purely defensive spiral. A Long Drive cascade, once established, is extremely difficult to interrupt without yielding ground.

**Training Method:** Heavy bag work, striking-post sequences, sprint-and-strike interval drills.

**Weakness:** High energy consumption. A Long Drive fighter who fails to end the exchange quickly is left winded and vulnerable.

**Common Among:** Shock troops, breach-assault specialists, duelists seeking first-blood victories.

**Notable Practitioner:** "Hammer" Jek, a mercenary captain whose Long Drive cascade was measured at seventeen uninterrupted strikes in a sanctioned duel. His opponent yielded on the twelfth. Jek delivered five more before he heard the yield. The arbiters ruled it the opponent's fault for yielding too quietly.

---

### 3. THE TORTOISE SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Defensive economy and attrition.

**Signature Technique:** The Shell Sequence — a minimal-motion parry pattern that covers all primary lines of attack with the smallest possible movements, conserving energy while forcing the opponent to overcommit. A Tortoise fighter wins not by striking but by waiting.

**Training Method:** Repetitive parry drills against multiple attackers, extended sparring sessions focused purely on defence, grip-strength conditioning.

**Weakness:** Limited offensive capacity. A Tortoise fighter who cannot transition from defence to counterattack is merely delaying defeat.

**Common Among:** Caravan guards, rearguard specialists, older fighters who have outlived their aggression.

**Notable Practitioner:** Old Marta, a caravan guard who fought off a pack of eight brigands at the age of sixty-one. She killed none. She simply deflected everything they threw at her until they exhausted themselves, then told them to go home. They did.

---

## THE WIND SCHOOLS

*Technique-focused. Fast, precise, economical. The Schools of the duelist.*

### 4. THE GREY HERRING SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Deception and misdirection.

**Signature Technique:** The False Window — the deliberate presentation of an apparent opening that is in fact a trap. The Grey Herring fighter invites a specific attack, having already prepared the counter. The art lies in making the invitation indistinguishable from a genuine mistake.

**Training Method:** Paired deception drills, feint-fluency exercises, study of opponent psychology and common responses.

**Weakness:** Reliance on opponent predictability. A Grey Herring fighter facing an opponent who does not take the bait — or who is too inexperienced to recognise the invitation — loses their primary advantage.

**Common Among:** Duelists, fencing masters, judicial champions.

**Notable Practitioner:** Kellan Voss, who won seventeen consecutive judicial duels without suffering a single wound. His eighteenth opponent, a young woman with no formal training, ignored every false opening and struck him in the shoulder by accident. Voss conceded the duel and hired her as a student.

---

### 5. THE RED COIN SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Speed and precision through economy of motion.

**Signature Technique:** The Single Exchange — the philosophy that any duel should be decided in one action. Red Coin fighters train to deliver a decisive strike from a neutral position with no preparatory movement, no telegraph, and no warning. The strike simply happens, and the duel is over.

**Training Method:** Thousands of repetitions of a single strike from every conceivable starting position, reaction drills against random stimuli, breath-timing exercises.

**Weakness:** If the Single Exchange fails — if the opponent parries or evades — the Red Coin fighter often lacks a developed follow-up game. The School bets everything on the first move.

**Common Among:** Professional duelists, assassins, honour-guard champions.

**Notable Practitioner:** Eris the Needle, whose Single Exchange was timed at less than two-tenths of a second from stillness to penetration. She lost only one duel in her career, to a Mirror mage who was reading her intent rather than her movement. She survived the loss. The mage did not survive their next encounter, which occurred outside a formal duel setting. Eris was not a duellist that day.

---

### 6. THE WHITE WATER SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Flow and redirection.

**Signature Technique:** The Borrowed Force — a method of receiving an opponent's attack and redirecting its energy rather than blocking or evading it. A White Water fighter does not oppose force. They guide it past them, often into a position that unbalances the attacker.

**Training Method:** Push-hands style paired sensitivity drills, falling and rolling practice, grip-release exercises to prevent instinctive resistance.

**Weakness:** High sensitivity requirement. A White Water fighter must feel the opponent's force to redirect it. Against an opponent who does not commit — who feints and probes without giving real pressure — the technique finds no purchase.

**Common Among:** Unarmed combat specialists, bodyguards operating in confined spaces, fighters who expect to face larger opponents.

**Notable Practitioner:** Toren Rill, a small man who served as personal guard to three consecutive merchant-lords. He was thrown through furniture many times. His charges were never touched. When asked about his technique, he said: "I don't stop the blow. I just make sure it's me it hits, and I'm very good at hitting things."

---

## THE SHADOW SCHOOLS

*Judgement-focused. Tactical, adaptive, indirect. The Schools of the commander and the survivor.*

### 7. THE GREY VEIL SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Situational awareness and threat prioritisation.

**Signature Technique:** The Battlefield Eye — a trained perceptual habit that allows the practitioner to track multiple threats, allies, and environmental factors simultaneously without fixating on any single element. A Grey Veil fighter sees the whole field, not just the opponent in front of them.

**Training Method:** Multi-threat sparring against increasing numbers of opponents, memorisation and recall drills under physical stress, simulated battlefield scenarios with hidden variables.

**Weakness:** Information overload. A Grey Veil fighter who takes in more than they can process is as paralysed as a novice. The School teaches filtering, but the filter can be overwhelmed.

**Common Among:** Field commanders, banner-guards, skirmish leaders.

**Notable Practitioner:** Marshal Sera Venn, who commanded the rearguard at the Red Crossing. She tracked seven separate enemy advances, repositioned her forces thirteen times, and extracted four-fifths of her command across the river before the bridge fell. She lost an eye to an arrow during the third repositioning. She mentioned it to no one until the battle was over.

---

### 8. THE BROKEN GROUND SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Environmental exploitation and improvisation.

**Signature Technique:** The Unfair Advantage — the systematic use of terrain, available objects, and situational elements to compensate for disparities in condition or technique. A Broken Ground fighter does not fight fair. They fight in doorways, on stairs, in mud, with chairs, with handfuls of sand, with the environment itself as a weapon.

**Training Method:** Sparring in deliberately difficult and varied terrain, object-utilisation drills, scenario training where the fighter begins at a deliberate disadvantage and must equalise.

**Weakness:** Environment dependency. A Broken Ground fighter in a featureless, empty space — a flat arena, a bare room — loses most of their tools. They are not helpless, but they are diminished.

**Common Among:** Irregular fighters, street-brawlers turned professional, scouts operating behind enemy lines.

**Notable Practitioner:** "Gutter" Dan, a criminal-turned-scout who once killed a fully armoured knight by leading him into a laundry and drowning him in a dye vat. "He was bigger than me," Dan explained. "The vat wasn't."

---

### 9. THE LAST BREATH SCHOOL

**Core Emphasis:** Survival and recovery from imminent defeat.

**Signature Technique:** The Turn — a trained response to being beaten. When a Last Breath fighter is disarmed, knocked down, wounded, or cornered, they do not panic. They execute a practised sequence of survival actions — creating distance, finding cover, deploying a backup weapon, exploiting the opponent's momentary overconfidence — that turns the moment of defeat into a moment of possibility.

**Training Method:** Sparring from deliberately compromised positions, simulated wound drills, survival-scenario training where the fighter begins already "dead" and must reverse the outcome.

**Weakness:** Last Breath techniques are inherently desperation measures. They work best when the opponent believes the fight is over and drops their guard. Against a disciplined opponent who presses the advantage without pause, the Turn is much harder to execute.

**Common Among:** Bodyguards expecting ambush, soldiers who have survived lost battles, anyone who has been defeated and resolved not to be again.

**Notable Practitioner:** Captain Rennick Ast, who was captured, disarmed, bound, and thrown at his captor's feet during a parley gone wrong. His Turn involved headbutting his captor's knee, seizing the man's own dagger with his bound hands, cutting his bonds on the blade while it was still in the captor's belt, and killing three men before anyone understood what was happening. He does not recommend the method.

---

## THE UNORTHODOX PATH

*Not a School. The absence of School. The way of the rough fighter.*

The Unorthodox Path is not taught. It is accumulated. Its practitioners are called irregulars, gutter-fighters, scrappers, or simply survivors.

**Characteristics:**

- Techniques stolen or adapted from multiple Schools, often imperfectly understood but effective nonetheless.
- Heavy reliance on surprise, aggression, and unconventional tactics.
- Significant gaps in conditioning and formal technique, compensated by adaptability and a willingness to do what School-trained fighters consider "dishonourable."
- Often underestimated by formal fighters, to the formal fighter's detriment.
- High variance. Some irregulars are incompetent blusterers. Some are as dangerous as any School master.

**Common Techniques:**

- **The Ambush:** The fight begins before the opponent knows it has begun.
- **The Equaliser:** Use of weapons or tools the Schools consider degenerate — throwing sand, spitting, headbutts, groin strikes, biting.
- **The Cold Open:** Starting combat mid-sentence, mid-step, mid-breath, with no warning or formal commencement.
- **The Shuffle:** Deliberately switching between School techniques and irregular chaos to confuse opponents who are trained to recognise patterns.
- **The Pragmatic Retreat:** Fleeing a losing fight without shame, and returning later with more friends.

**Weakness:** Inconsistency. An irregular fighter may defeat a School master one day and lose to a novice the next, because their methods depend heavily on context, surprise, and the opponent's unfamiliarity with chaos.

**Notable Practitioner:** Hark. Hark was collected by the Order as a child Sensitive. Hark escaped before full training. Hark cannot work magic — his Gate never fully opened — but he knows enough about mages to understand their weaknesses. He fights like a man who has spent his life among predators and learned to bite. He belongs to no School. He is, by his own admission, a collection of dirty tricks held together by spite. Mages who underestimate him tend not to repeat the mistake. Most don't survive the first lesson.

# BOOK THREE: THE EDGE IN PRACTICE

## 1. Warriors Against Warriors

When two trained warriors face each other, the outcome depends on the interplay of their Schools, their individual Edge, and circumstance.

A Steel fighter with superior condition will press a Wind fighter, denying them the time and space to set up their precision techniques. A Wind fighter with superior speed will pick apart a Steel fighter's defences before the Steel fighter can bring their power to bear. A Shadow fighter will refuse the engagement until the terrain favours them.

The Schooled fighter knows what to expect from another Schooled fighter. The irregular is the wild card. This is why experienced warriors often find irregulars more troubling than fellow School graduates — and why the wise commander values both.

---

## 2. Warriors Against Mages

The warrior's Edge does not negate magic. It does not provide immunity. But it does provide opportunity.

A mage's debt system means every spell costs something. A warrior who understands this fights the mage's stamina, not their power. Evasion, cover, and pressure force the mage to spend. Every flame bolt dodged or blocked with a mundane shield is a debt the mage must pay. Every moment the warrior stays alive is a moment the mage's internal reservoir depletes.

The Schools that fare best against mages are the ones that emphasise closing distance quickly (Long Drive), evading until the mage exhausts themselves (Tortoise, White Water), or exploiting terrain to break line of sight (Broken Ground). The Grey Herring School fares poorly — mages do not read physical tells the way warriors do, and the false window is meaningless against an opponent who attacks with fire rather than steel.

The Unorthodox Path fares surprisingly well. Mages are rare. Most mages are trained to fight other mages or to deal with Schooled warriors. An irregular who does not move like a fighter, does not telegraph aggression, and opens the engagement by throwing a rock is outside the mage's training set.

---

## 3. The Collective Edge

**The Third Principle:** Warriors reinforce each other.

A single warrior against a mage is a fair fight, weighted toward the mage. Ten warriors against a mage is a problem. A hundred warriors against a mage is a conclusion.

This is not magic. It is simply the arithmetic of application. A mage can track one opponent, two, five. Against ten closing from different angles, the mage's attention fragments. Against a hundred, the mage cannot prioritise targets fast enough. Some will reach them. And when they do, the mage's body is as fragile as anyone's.

This is why mages do not rule alone. This is why the Order collects Sensitives rather than conquering nations. This is why kingdoms still field armies. The Edge, multiplied across a thousand common soldiers, closes the gap that a single mage's borrowing creates.

Magic is scarce. The Edge is common. In the long run, the common wins.

# APPENDIX: UNIVERSAL RULES SUMMARY

1. **Martial power is trained application.** Nothing is borrowed. Nothing is owed. The warrior takes what already exists — a body, a blade, a situation — and applies it with skill.

2. **The Edge is the gap between trained and untrained.** It is the product of condition, technique, and judgement. All three are trainable by anyone.

3. **The Edge has limits.** They are the limits of the body and the situation. A warrior cannot transcend flesh. When the body fails, there is no reserve.

4. **The Schools are pedagogical lineages.** They teach specific combinations of condition, technique, and judgement. No initiation is required. Only training.

5. **The Unorthodox Path is accumulated experience.** It is the rough fighter's way — stolen techniques, dirty tricks, and survival-hardened instinct.

6. **Warriors reinforce each other.** The Edge multiplies in numbers. A thousand common soldiers can do what a single champion cannot.

7. **Magic is scarce. The Edge is common.** This is why armies still matter.
