# Chapter 11: The Vortex

The hollow was quiet. Wind in the scrub. A gull crying somewhere out over the water. The woman in the garden didn't look up when he reached the bottom of the path. Her hands were still moving, working the soil, pulling weeds with the same steady rhythm he'd watched from the ridge. Dark hair pulled back. Lean shoulders. A net draped over a wooden frame near the cottage door, half-mended, the twine loose at one end.

Hark stopped at the edge of the garden. He watched her hands. He always watched hands.

These hands were never still. Even in the dirt, they moved with a restlessness that was either habit or necessity. The fingers curled and uncurled. The wrists turned. The soil shifted around them, dark and damp, and the weeds came free one by one, and the motion never stopped. It wasn't nervous. It was something else. The way Lea's hands had moved, shaping vortices in the dormitory dark, because the wind wanted to move and she had to let it.

The woman finished the row. Brushed the dirt from her knees. And then, without looking up, without acknowledging him at all, she opened her palm and shaped water into a vortex no bigger than a candle flame.

It spun between her fingers. Silver and silent. Precise. Controlled. The water came from the rain barrel beside the cottage—he saw the thread of it, thin as a hair, rising from the surface and coiling through the air into her hand. The vortex turned. The light caught it. The memory hit him like a crossbow bolt.

*Lea's hand. Pressed against his. The air between their palms spinning silver in the dormitory dark. Her grey eyes focused, intent. Her voice, low and proud: "It's beautiful." His voice, young and stupid: "You make the air weird." Her laugh, small and quickly stifled. Both of them frozen, listening for footsteps. No one came.*

The woman looked up.

Grey eyes. Dark hair. Her hands never stopped—the vortex still spinning between her palms, the water still turning, the light still catching. She looked at him the way Soric looked at a new face in the Weeping Dog: measuring. Calculating. Not afraid. Not yet.

"You're the Fire-mage they've been whispering about."

Hark didn't answer. His voice was somewhere in his throat, locked behind a door he'd closed fifteen years ago. He could feel the heat bleeding off him—not magic, just body heat, the furnace he couldn't fully shut down—and he knew she could feel it too. Water Gates sensed temperature differently than Fire Gates. She'd know what he was. She'd probably known before he crested the ridge.

"I'm the one who wants to be left alone," he said. It came out flat. The way everything came out flat.

"Then why are you still here?"

He didn't have an answer. He didn't move. The vortex spun between her palms. Silver and silent. He was ten years old again, and Lea was beside him, and the world had not yet ended.

The woman waited. Her hands kept moving—the vortex steady, the water catching the grey light from the clouded sky. She didn't seem to mind the silence. Some people filled silence with noise. Some people let it sit. She was the second kind. That was rare. That was either a good sign or a very bad one.

"You're staring," she said.

"You're doing the thing."

She tilted her head. "What thing."

"The vortex. Silver. Small."

Her hands stilled. The water dropped—just water again, splashing onto the dirt at her feet. The sound was small and ordinary and somehow worse than the silence. She looked at him differently now. Not measuring. Something else. The way Verrol looked at a contract that didn't add up.

"You've seen it before."

Hark didn't answer. He didn't have to.

Lea's hands had gone still once. Only once. The night she showed him the vortex. She'd held it between her palms—silver and silent, a miniature storm contained in the cup of their fingers—and he'd told her it was beautiful. She'd smiled. She'd said the instructors wanted her doing pressure-blades and gust-forcing, combat aeromancy, but she didn't want to fight. She wanted to make the water shiver.

Then the vortex had guttered out. Her breath had caught—not the normal catch of exertion, something deeper, wrong. She'd pulled her hand back and pressed it to her chest. Her breathing went shallow and ragged, a thin reedy sound like wind through a cracked window. Her lips lost colour. The space around her cot went still. The pressure collapsed inward as her body clawed back whatever it had spent.

*"The healers say my lung capacity is permanently reduced."* Her voice, hoarse and flat. Her hands, still on the blanket. *"Every working shaves a little more off. By the time I'm thirty, I might not be able to run. I'm eleven years old."*

Her hands had been so still. He'd held them. He'd not known what else to do. He still didn't.

Hark shoved his hands into his pockets. The gloves were on. The scars were hidden. The cold in his fingers was deeper than it had been in weeks—the frost writing new lines, the ledger updating itself—and he didn't want to look at his own hands. He wanted to look at hers. He kept looking at hers.

The woman let the water fall. Wiped her palms on her trousers. The motion was practical, unhurried. She wasn't afraid of him. That was interesting. Most people were afraid of something. The ones who weren't were either stupid or dangerous.

"You're the Water-mage," he said. "The one teaching guarding."

She didn't deny it. Didn't confirm it. She just looked at him with those grey eyes—grey like a winter sky, grey like the sea before a storm—and said: "I'm the one who survived long enough to learn."

The words landed. The way Soric's words landed. The way a crossbow bolt landed. Hark stood at the edge of her garden with his hands in his pockets and his voice locked behind a door he couldn't open and the memory of a girl who'd said the same thing, in different words, on a cot in the dark.

*"When I get out, I'm going back to the river. The one near my village. The wind comes down from the hills and makes the water shiver. I'm going to stand there and breathe, and no one's going to tell me to do it again."*

Lea hadn't survived long enough to learn. Lea had survived long enough to run, and then the bolt had taken her, and the learning had stopped. This woman had kept going. Ten years. Guarding techniques. Teaching others. The thing Lea had wanted—to be free, to use her gate on her own terms, to make the water shiver—this woman had done it. Was still doing it.

He didn't know what to do with that. He didn't know what to do with any of it.

He sat down on a stump at the edge of the garden. It was old, the wood silvered by weather, the rings worn smooth. He didn't ask permission. He just sat. The woman watched him for a moment, then turned back to her garden. She didn't tell him to leave. He didn't tell her why he was there.

The sun began to set. The light went orange, then red, then grey. The wind picked up. The woman finished her weeding and moved to the net—the half-mended one draped over the wooden frame. She sat on a stool and picked up the twine and her hands started moving again, mending the holes, knotting the cord. The motion was different from the vortex, but the same in the ways that mattered. Precise. Rhythmic. Never still.

He watched her hands. He didn't speak. That was enough.

Somewhere in the hills, a bird called. The sea crashed against the rocks below. The woman mended her net and the water in the rain barrel stirred occasionally—a ripple here, a splash there, the element responding to her presence without being asked. She was guarded. He could feel it. Her internal reservoir was locked tight, the same way his was, the same way any escaped mage learned to lock it. But the water still knew she was there. The water still listened.

"You're Fire," she said, without looking up from the net.

"Yes."

"Light mutation. The scars go up to your elbows, probably. You hide them under the gloves."

He didn't answer.

"You're not Order. Your debt-pattern is wrong—too deep, too uneven. You learned to guard, but you learned the hard way. Someone pushed you. Someone pushed you hard." She tied off a knot. Tested it. Moved to the next hole. "And you've seen the vortex before. Not mine. Someone else's. Someone who's dead."

The word hit him in the chest. A physical thing. A bolt of his own. He didn't flinch. He'd learned not to flinch.

"Wind Gate," he said. His voice came out rougher than he meant it to. "She couldn't do water. She did air. The vortex was the same."

"But she's dead."

"Yes."

The woman tied another knot. The net was taking shape under her hands—the holes closing, the twine tightening. "The Order."

"I killed the man who killed her."

"Good."

He looked up. She was still working, her hands still moving, her face half in shadow. She hadn't said it like a question. She'd said it like a fact. *Good.* The word settled into the silence between them and stayed there.

"She was eleven," he said. He didn't know why he said it. He never talked about Lea. He'd never talked about Lea to anyone except Joel, and Joel was dead, and the words had been locked in his chest for fifteen years. But the woman had grey eyes and hands that never stopped moving, and the vortex was still there in the air between them even though it wasn't, and the words came out before he could stop them.

"I'm sorry," the woman said.

"Don't."

"All right."

The sun finished setting. The grey light faded into blue, then black. The woman lit a lantern—a small flame, ordinary, no magic—and hung it from the frame of the net. The light caught her hands. The motion of her fingers. The twine pulling tight. He watched. He didn't speak. She didn't ask him to.

The door in his chest was still open. The splinter was still there. But the woman's hands were still moving, and the water in the rain barrel was still stirring, and somewhere in the hills above the sea, a Fire-mage who'd been running for fifteen years was sitting on a stump in a stranger's garden, watching a stranger mend a net, and not leaving.

He didn't know what that meant. He didn't name it. He sat on the stump and watched her hands and let the silence be enough.
